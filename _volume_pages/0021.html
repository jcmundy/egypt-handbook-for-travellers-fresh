{% raw %}(2). Coinage. Passports. Custom House.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Coinage (comp. the tables before the title-page and at the end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the book). The Egyptian Pound ('Livre Egyptienne'; ^E.)is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worth 20s. 6d., and is divided into 100 Piastres, worth 10 Milliemes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each. The Arabic name for the piastre is Kirsh (pi. Kurush; pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nounced in Cairo Hrsh, 'urush), but the European name is every¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where current. Travellers should note the distinction that is still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequently made between the 'great piastre' (kirsh tarifa) worth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10 milliemes and the 'little (or half) piastre' (kirsh sagh), worth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5 milliemes. — Egyptian gold coins are seldom met with, their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place being taken by the British sovereign (Gineh inglisi = 97 pias.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5 mill.), the French Napoleon (20 fr.; Bint = 77 pias. 2 mill.), and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Turkish pound (Mejidiyeh = 87 pias. 772 mill. = 18s.), all of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are legally current. At Alexandria and Suez, and a few other points,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reckoning in francs is still common. Where British influence is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strong, as in places with large garrisons, the word Shilling is used for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Rub'a Riyal, which is equivalent to about Is. S^fed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Obsolete coins are rare in Egypt, but counterfeit or depreciated (i.e. worn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or perforated) pieces are common enough. As these are more likely to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offered to foreigners than to natives, travellers should be on their guard{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against them when obtaining change- A liberal supply of small change is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more essential in the East than anywhere else (comp. pp. xxii, 26).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passports are usually asked for at all the Egyptian ports, and if{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the traveller is unprovided with one he is liable to detention and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great inconvenience. The passport is given up at the custom-house{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and reclaimed at the traveller's consulate or at Cairo. Bankers fre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quently require strangers to establish their identity by some such{% endraw %}
{% raw %}document; and the countenance and help of consuls must also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}depend upon the proof of nationality offered to them by the traveller.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A British Foreign Office Passport (price 2s.; agent's fee 1*. 6d.) may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obtained in London through W. J. Adams, 59 Fleet Street; Buss, 440 West{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Strand; C. Smith & Sons, 63 Charing Cross, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Custom House. The custom-house examination is generally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carried out with great thoroughness, though with perfect politeness.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The objects chiefly sought for are tobacco and cigars, on which a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat high tax is levied (25, 40, or 100 pias. per kilogramme or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2^5 lbs., according to quality, comp. p. xxx). The exportation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}antiquities is forbidden, except with a special certificate of permis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sion ; and luggage is accordingly examined again as the traveller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quits the country. The custom-house is now under European man¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}agement, and it is advisable to refrain from an attempt to facilitate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}matters by bakshish (p. xx). If luggage be forwarded across the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frontier, the keys must be sent with it; but it is very desirable to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}superintend the custom-house examination in person.{% endraw %}
