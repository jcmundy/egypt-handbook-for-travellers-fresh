{% raw %}Railways. The official time-tables are published in the Indi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cateur des Chemins de fer de I'Egypte, which is sold for 1 pias. at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief railway-stations, and is also to be seen in the larger hotels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The railway-carriages resemble those of France or Italy. First-class{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passengers are permitted to take a reasonable quantity of small lug¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gage with them into the carriages. The second-class carriages are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}comfortable enough for day-journeys on the main routes (Alexandria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Cairo, Cairo to Mansura, Cairo to Suez, Cairo to Assuan), especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the express - trains; and their use effects a very considerable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}saving in fares. But on branch-lines all travellers should take{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first-class tickets. The third-class carriages are quite unsuited for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Europeans. The management of the traffic, except in the case of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}express-trains, is not very satisfactory. The process of booking{% endraw %}
{% raw %}luggage is especially slow and troublesome. The traveller should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}therefore be at the station fully half-an-hour before the hour for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}starting, as the ticket-clerks are entitled to close the office ten{% endraw %}
{% raw %}minutes before the departure of the train. The personal tickets are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}printed in English and Arabic, the luggage-tickets in Arabic only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The luggage-tariff is somewhat complicated; 55lbs. of hand-luggage{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are free. — In hot weather the dust, which penetrates the carriages{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even when the windows are closed, renders railway travelling in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt exceedingly unpleasant. At the chief stations on the express-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}routes there are Railway Buffets in the European style. At other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stations refreshments are brought to the carriage-windows (bargain¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing necessary; 3-5 oranges 1/2-l pias.). The water offered for sale{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is better abstained from.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Narrow Gauge Railways. The Egyptian Light Railways cover{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Delta and the Fayum (p. 174) with a network of lines, which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}though of little importance to the ordinary tourist, enable the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plorer and the specialist to reach various remote sites with com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}parative ease. These lines do not yet appear in the 'Indicateur',{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but they are mentioned at the appropriate places in our text.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Electric Tramways ply in Alexandria and Cairo. They have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two classes; Europeans invariably patronize the first only.{% endraw %}
