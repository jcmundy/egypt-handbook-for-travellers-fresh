{% raw %}are usually to be found standing about near the hotels and one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these maybe employed as interpreter (though offers of farther service{% endraw %}
{% raw %}should be firmly declined). The traveller should keep his eye on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the direction taken by the cab, as sometimes the cabman drives{% endraw %}
{% raw %}straight ahead in complete ignorance of the way, and requires to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guided, e.g. by being touched with a stick on the right or left arm{% endraw %}
{% raw %}according to the turning, or with the words yemlnak (to the right),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shemalak (to the left), dughri (straight on). The cabs usually drive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rapidly, so that their use saves time and strength.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Donkeys (Arab, homar) still form the best means of conveyance,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not only in the smaller towns and on the bridle-paths in the country,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but also in the environs of Cairo, as they can go anywhere and are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not confined to the dusty carriage-roads. Egyptian donkeys are of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}much finer, swifter, and more spirited race than the European, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are at the same time patient and persevering. Those in the towns are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally well saddled and bridled in Oriental style. In the country{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both the donkeys and equipment are inferior; saddles, stirrups, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even bridles are occasionally conspicuous by their absence. As the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gait of some of the donkeys is very uneasy when they break into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a trot, care should be taken not to engage one with this defect for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an excursion of any length. The best method of guiding the donkey{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is to strike it lightly on the head with a stick. The donkey-boys{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Arab, hammaf) are fond of showing off tiie pace of their beasts,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and often drive them unpleasantly fast, though galloping is for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bidden in the towns. The rider who prefers a slower pace shouts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'ala mahlak or 'ala mahlakum; if a quicker pace is wanted, yalla,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yalla, or mdshl, or s&k el-homar; if a halt is to be made, osbur, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the English word 'stop'. The donkey-boys (especially at Cairo) are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually active and intelligent, though occasionally mischievous and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impudent. At some of the most frequented tourist-resorts the trav¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eller has to protect himself from the charge of the importunate light{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cavalry by threatening to use his stick.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(4). Hotels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The large hotels in Cairo and its environs are among the best in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the world, combining western comfort with eastern luxury. Almost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as much may be said for the leading houses at Luxor and Assuan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(comp. pp. 233, 327), while there are very fair hotels at Alexandria,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Port Sa'id, and a few other places. They are managed mainly on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}American system, a fixed sum daily (p. 24) being paid for lodging{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and board, the latter consisting of breakfast, luncheon, and dinner.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wine, beer, and other liquors, which are extras, are dear the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cheapest wine costing 10-15 pias. per bottle, and British and Ger¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man beer about the same. The waiter's fee should be calculated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at about 5 per cent of the bill. Clothing given out to wash is charged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the rate of 272-3 fr. per dozen articles for gentlemen's garments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4-5 fr. per dozen for ladies' garments, quite irrespective of size.{% endraw %}
