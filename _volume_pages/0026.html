{% raw %}CONSULATES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(6). Public Safety. Consulates. Courts of Justice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Public Safety. The authority of the Khedive is so well estab¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lished throughout the whole of Egypt that travellers are as safe as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Europe. Weapons for self-defence are an unnecessary encum¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brance. — Fowling-pieces may be purchased in Cairo or they may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be had for hire at the principal hotels. Sportsmen who bring their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}own guns will find it very troublesome to clear them at the custom¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house, and cartridges also are contraband (p. xv). Ammunition (in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cluding Lefaucheux cartridges) may be bought in Cairo, but not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}higher up, where only coarse gun-powder can be obtained.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Consulates. Consuls in the East enjoy the same privilege of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exterritoriality as ambassadors in other countries. On public occas¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ions they are attended by kavasses, or armed consular officers. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distinction is sometimes made between professional ('consules{% endraw %}
{% raw %}missi') and commercial consuls ; and there are consuls general (who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}act also as political agents), consuls, vice-consuls, and consular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}agents, possessing various degrees of authority. In all cases of em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ergency the traveller should apply for advice to the nearest consul{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his country.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Courts of Justice. In place of the exclusive consular juris¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}diction to which foreigners were formerly liable, a system of Mixed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tribunals was established in 1876. The judges consist of natives{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and foreigners (the latter generally appointed by the Khedive from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}qualified officials nominated by the Great Powers), who give their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}verdicts in accordance with Egyptian law, founded on that of France{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Italy. Cases in which the Khedive himself and the Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}government are concerned are also tried before this tribunal, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}includes courts of first and second instance. The courts of the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}instance are at Cairo, Alexandria, and Mansura, and there is a dele¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gation at Port Sa'id. The appeal-court is at Alexandria. Lists of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}qualified barristers are exhibited in the anterooms of the courts. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cases between natives, and all criminal cases, are tried by the Native{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Courts, established in 1884. Courts of the first instance are situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Cairo, Alexandria, Benisuef, Assiut, and Keneh, and also (with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more limited jurisdiction) at Tanta and Mansura (Zakazik). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appeal-court is at Cairo; about half the number of its judges are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Europeans. The procedure is based upon the Code Napoleon.{% endraw %}
