{% raw %}DRAGOMANS.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The traveller should take care to he amply supplied with small{% endraw %}

{% raw %}the country (comp. pp. xv, 26).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Travellers about to make a tour of any length may avoid all the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}petty annoyances incident to direct dealings with the natives by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}placing themselves under the care of a Dragoman (Arab. Tur-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gemdn). The name is also appropriated to themselves by the ordinary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}commissionnaires in Cairo, Alexandria, Port Sa'id, Luxor, Assuan,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}etc. Most of them speak English, French, and Italian. Charges, see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 28. Dragomans proper, who generally consider it beneath their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dignity to escort their employers through the streets of towns, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually employed for the longer tours only, such as the voyage up{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile (p. 184), the journey to the Fayum (p. 174), and a visit to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the less frequented towns in the Delta. Before engaging a drago¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man, the traveller should carefully enquire into his record at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hotel and the consulate. For a fixed price per day the dragoman con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tracts to supply the necessary riding animals and boats and to defray{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the entire cost of lodging and provisioning the party, including all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hotel-bills. The contract, which must be signed at the consulate,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}should expressly determine all details as far as possible, including{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the duration of the journey, with due provision for prolonging it if{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desired. It is usual to pay the dragoman one-half of the total stip¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ulated sum before starting, and the remainder on the return, or one-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}third before starting, one-third during the journey, and the remain¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing third on its conclusion. Both parties to the contract should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bind themselves to submit disputes or differences to the arbitration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the consul.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The dragomans are inclined to assume a patronising manner{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards their employers, while they generally treat their own coun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trymen with an air of vast superiority. The sooner this imperti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nence is checked, the more satisfactory will be the traveller's sub¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sequent relations with his guide. Above all, travellers should never{% endraw %}
{% raw %}permit their dragoman to 'explain' the monuments. These men are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}without exception quite uneducated, without the least knowledge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the historic or aesthetic significance of the monuments; and their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}•explanations' are merely garbled versions of what they have picked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up from guide-books or from the remarks of previous travellers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the successful termination of the journey travellers are too{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apt from motives of good nature to write a more favourable testi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monial for their dragoman than he really deserves; but this is truly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an act of injustice to his subsequent employers, and tends to con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}firm him in his faults. The testimonial therefore should not omit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to mention any serious cause for dissatisfaction.{% endraw %}
