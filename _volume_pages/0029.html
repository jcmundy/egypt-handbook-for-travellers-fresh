{% raw %}(8). Arabian Cafes. Story Tellers. Musicians. Singers, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arabian Cafes (kahwa) are frequented by the lower classes ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clusively. The front generally consists of woodwork with a few open{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arches. Outside the door runs a mastaba, or raised seat of stone or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brick, two or three feet in height and of about the same width,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}covered with mats, and there are similar seats on two or three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sides of the interior. Coffee is served by the kahwegi at 1/i~i pias.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}per cup (fingdn), and several nargllehs or shlshehs and gozehs (water-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pipes) are kept in readiness for the use of customers. The tumbdk,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a kind, of Persian tobacco, smoked in the latter is sometimes mixed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the intoxicating hashish (hemp, Cannabis indica), the strong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and unmistakable smell of which is often perceptible even in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}street. The sale of hashish is now nominally prohibited in Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Story Tellers (who in private domestic circles are generally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}women) still form a characteristic Oriental institution. Wherever{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they make their appearance, whether in the public streets or the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coffee-house, in the densely peopled alleys of the large towns, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the smallest country villages, or among the tents of the wandering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arabs, they are sure to attract an attentive, easily pleased, and ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceedingly grateful crowd. The more sensational the tale, the better,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the oftener is the narrator applauded with protracted cries of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Aah', or 'Allah', or 'Allahu akbar 1'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the story-tellers belong to the so-called Sho'ara (sing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shd'ir), literally 'singers'. They are also known as 'Andtireh (sing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Antari) or Abu-Zldlyeh, according as their theme consists of tales{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and romances from the history of 'Antar, a Beduin hero, or from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of Abu Zed. Others again are called Mohadditin, i.e. narrators{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of history, their province being the recital in prose of passages from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the history of Sultan Ez-Zahir Bibars, who reigned over Egypt in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1260-79 (p. xcvi). The entertainments of the lalf Uleh u Itleti{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(thousand and one nights) are, however, no longer heard, as popular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}superstition has branded this collection of tales as 'unlucky'. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}themes of the whole fraternity are too often of an immoral character.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Musicians by profession, called Alatiyeh (sing. Alati), are in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dispensable on every festive occasion. The usual instruments are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the rekk or tambourine with little bells, the nakkdreh or semi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spherical tambourine, the zemr or hautbois, the tabl beledi or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}drum, the tabl shdmi or kettle-drum, and the darabukeh, a kind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of funnel-shaped drum (generally made of earthenware, but some¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}times of wood inlaid with mother-of-pearl and tortoise-shell, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a fish-skin stretched over the broad end), which last is accompanied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the zummdra, a kind of double flute. A better class of instru¬{% endraw %}
