{% raw %}The Egyptians consider themselves a highly musical people. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian sings when indulging in his kef (p. xxvi), whether sitting on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his heels or stretched out on his mat, when driving his donkey, when{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carrying stones and mortar up a scaffolding, when working in the fields,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and when rowing. He sings whether alone or in company, regarding his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vocal music as a means of lightening his labour and of sweetening his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}repose. A peculiarity of the Egyptian songs, however, is that they have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no tune, though they have a certain rhythm, which is always dependent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the text. They are sung through the nose on seven or eight different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}notes, on which the performer wanders up and down. The character{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of this so-called music is exceedingly monotonous and, to a European ear,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}displeasing. The songs (mawwdl or shughl) are all of a lyrical description,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most of them are erotic and often pointless and meaningless. Some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them, however, extol the pleasures of friendship and rational enjoyment,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or express derision of an enemy, or contempt for the rustic fellah.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Female Singers QAwalim, sing. 'Almeh or 'Alimeh; i.e.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'learned women') of a good class are now very rare and perform{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only in the harems of wealthy natives.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Female Dancers, or Ghawdzi (sing. Ghaziyeh), were for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merly one of the chief curiosities of Egypt, but for some years past{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they have been prohibited from performing in the streets. Really{% endraw %}
{% raw %}good dancers are said to be now rare; the performances in the cafes-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ohantants in Cairo are very inferior.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Snake Charmers (Rifd'lyeh, sing. Rifa'i; p. lxvi) exhibit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}performances of a very marvellous character, as credible European{% endraw %}
{% raw %}residents in Cairo have testified; but the traveller will rarely come{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in contact with them except by lucky accident. The men and boys{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who exhibit small snakes in the streets or at the hotels must of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course not be confounded with the Rifa'iyeh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Jugglers (Hdwi) of Egypt are similar to those of other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}countries. The performances of the Buffoons (Kuruddti or Mohab-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bazi) are disgracefully indelicate.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(9). Baths.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The baths of Egypt, with their hot-air chambers, are those com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monly known as Turkish, but they are neither so clean nor so well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fitted up as some of those in the larger cities of Europe. A Turkish{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bath is particularly refreshing after a long journey, and is an ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mirable preventive of colds and rheumatism. The baths are always{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cleanest in the early morning. Fridays are to be avoided, as numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Muslims bathe early on that day, which is their Sabbath. When a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cloth is hung up at the entrance to the baths, it indicates that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}women only are admitted.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}cupola (hosh el-hammdm), having a fountain of cold water in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centre (fasklyeh), and the bathing towels hung around on strings.{% endraw %}
