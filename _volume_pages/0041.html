{% raw %}XXXI{% endraw %}
{% raw %}II. Geographical and Political Notice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a. Area and Subdivisions of Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt proper, the country between the mouth ofthe Nile and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the First Cataract (comp. p. 335), is a small region with well-defined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}natural boundaries on three sides. On the'N. is the Mediterranean{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sea, on the E. the Arabian Desert and the Red Sea, and on the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Libyan Desert. The S. boundary is not marked by any natural{% endraw %}
{% raw %}feature, and has therefore at all ages been liable to alteration. Its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fluctuations, sometimes to the N., sometimes to the S., form a kind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of standard of the fluctuating political power of Egypt, and the causes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the variations involve a great part of Egyptian history from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most ancient times down to the present day.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}When Mohammed 'Ali, the founder of the modern vassal king¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dom of Egypt (comp. p. xcix), died in 1849, he bequeathed to his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}successor a power extending far to the S. of the First Cataract, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}including not only the Nubian Valley of the Nile, with the Nubian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert-regions, but also the so-called Egyptian Sudan (Beled es-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sudan, 'land of the blacks'), consisting of the districts of Tdka,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Senndar, and Kordofan. The Khedive Isma'il (p. c) pushed his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boundaries towards the S. until they comprised the whole course of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the White Nile and the greater part of the river-region of the Bahr{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Ghazdl, and finally extended to about 2° N. latitude. But these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}territories were lost again even more rapidly than they had been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}won. The rebellion of the ATab tribes that broke out in 1883 under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Mahdi (pp. lxxii, cii) not only utterly destroyed the new Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}power on the White Nile, but also wrested the entire Sudan as far{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as Lower Nubia from the Khedive. The campaigns of 1896-98 and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the capture of Omdurman (p. 388), however, finally united the Sudan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with Egypt, though under totally altered conditions. Thus Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strictly so called now includes the valley of the Nile up to a point{% endraw %}
{% raw %}25 M. to the N. of Wadi Haifa, the desert-strip along the Red Sea,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the coast to the W. of Alexandria as far as the Gulf of Solun, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great Libyan Desert with the five Oases, the greater part ofthe Sinai{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Peninsula, and the region of El-'Arish (comp. Baedeker's Palestine,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 143). Its area, exclusive of the deserts, may be estimated at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 12,970 sq. M., of which about 9160 sq. M. are cultivable. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sudan, which begins on the Nile a little to the N. of Wadi Haifa{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and on the Red Sea at 22° N. lat, is under a special Anglo-Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}administration (comp. p. 380).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the earliest times Egypt has been divided into two parts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of very unequal size, known as Lower and Upper Egypt. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boundary between these is still, as in antiquity, to the S. of Cairo.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}ract only, but is now prolonged to the S. to Wadi Haifa. Politically,{% endraw %}
