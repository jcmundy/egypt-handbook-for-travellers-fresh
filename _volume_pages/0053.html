{% raw %}very dark, bronze-coloured complexion, and their luxuriant growth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of hair, shading their heads like a cloud, or hanging down in num¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}berless plaits over their necks and shoulders. Their figures are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beautifully symmetrical, and more or less slender in accordance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with their means of subsistence, and their limbs are gracefully and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}delicately formed. In other respects they resemble all the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}children of the desert, as in the purity of their complexion, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}peculiar thinness of their necks, and the premature wrinkling of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the skin of their faces. Compared with their bold and quarrelsome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}neighbours the Bisharin (the ancient 'Ichthyophagi'), the 'Ababdeh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who correspond to the 'Troglodytes' of the Greeks, are exceedingly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gentle and inoffensive.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Besides the Bega, there are numerous Beduins who inhabit the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}steppes and deserts belonging to the region of the Nile, but beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the limits of Egypt, and range as far as the confines of the heathen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}negro-races on the left bank of the Nile, nearly to 9° N. latitude;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but with these we have not at present to deal. As regards the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beduins proper of the N., their common home, the desert, seems{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to have exerted a unifying effect upon races that were originally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}different, and the peculiar characteristics of each have gradually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}disappeared before the uniform environment of all.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There are three important Beduin tribes in the peninsula of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mount Sinai: the Terdbiyln; the Tlhdya, who occupy the heart of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the peninsula, between Suez and 'Akaba; and the Sawdrkeh or El-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Araylsh, to the north of the latter. In Upper Egypt, besides the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Ababdeh, the only Beduins who occupy the eastern bank of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile are the Bern Wasel and the Atwdni, or Hawdddt, who, however,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have now settled on both banks of the Thehan Nile valley and are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gradually blending with the fellahin, and the Md'azeh, who dwell{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in groups among the limestone mountains between Suez and Keneh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where there are good pastures at places. Most of the Arabian Beduins,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the other hand, who belong to Egypt, confine themselves to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}westernbank of the Nile. They occupy the whole of this side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river from the Fayum as far as Abydos near Girgeh, and it is mainly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with their aid that communication is maintained with the western{% endraw %}
{% raw %}oases, peopled by a totally different race (comp. pp. xlv, xlvi), who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}till the ground and possess no camels, being probably allied to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Berbers of Northern Africa (one of the numerous Libyan tribes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned in ancient inscriptions).{% endraw %}
