{% raw %}xliv THE MODERN EGYPTIANS. Town Arabs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be found nowhere except in their desert home, where to a great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extent they still retain the spirit of independence, the courage, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the restlessness of their ancestors. As in the time of Herodotus, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tent of the Beduin is still his home. Where it is pitched is a matter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of indifference to him, if only the pegs which secure it be firmly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}driven into the earth, if it shelter his wife and child from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}burning sunshine and the chilly night-air, and if pasturage-ground{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a spring be within reach. At Ramleh on the coast, near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexandria, the traveller will have an opportunity of seeing a whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colony of the poorest class encamped in their tents, where they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}live in the most frugal possible manner, with a few miserable goats{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the fowls which subsist on the rubbish in their neighbourhood.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Though professors of El-Islam, they are considerably less strict in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their observances than the fellahin of the valley of the Nile, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are themselves sufficiently lax, and above all they sadly neglect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the religious duty of cleanliness. They do not observe the practice{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of praying five times a day, and they are as a rule but slightly ac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quainted with the Koran. Relics of their old star-worship can still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be traced among their customs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The traveller will occasionally observe Beduins in the bazaars{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the armourers and leather-merchants, and will be struck with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the proud and manly bearing of these bronzed children of the desert,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whose sharp, bearded features and steady gaze betoken firmness and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resolution. In Egypt the traveller need not fear their predatory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}propensities.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(4). Arabian Dwellers in Towns. Those Arabs with whom the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller usually comes in contact in towns are shopkeepers, officials,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}servants, coachmen, and donkey-attendants, or perhaps these last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only, as most of the best shops are kept by Europeans, while in of¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ficial and legal matters his intercourse with the natives is carried{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on through the medium of his consul. These Arabs are generally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a much more mixed origin than the fellahin. It thus happens{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that the citizens of the Egyptian towns consist of persons of every{% endraw %}
{% raw %}complexion from dark brown to white, with the features of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worshippers of Osiris or the sharp profile of the Beduins, and with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the slender figure of the fellah or the corpulence of the Turk.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Among the lower classes frequent intermarriage with negro-women{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has darkened the complexion and thickened the features of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offspring; while the higher ranks, being descended from white{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slaves or Turkish mothers, more nearly resemble the European type.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As the inhabitants of the towns could not be so much oppressed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their rulers as the peasantry, we find that they exhibit a more in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dependent spirit, greater enterprise, and a more cheerful disposition{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than the fellahin. At the same time they are not free from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dreamy character peculiar to Orientals, nor from a tinge of the apathy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of fatalism; and their indolence contrasts strongly with the industry{% endraw %}
