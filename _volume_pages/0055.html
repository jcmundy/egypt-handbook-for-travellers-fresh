{% raw %}(5). Berbers. The name Berberi (plur. barabrd) is applied to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nubian inhabitants of the Nile-valley between the neighbour¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hood of Assuan and the Fourth Cataract. The Egyptians and Nubians{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are radically different, and the dislike between the two races is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carried to such an extent that Nubians never marry Egyptian wives.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Nubians are inferior to the Egyptians in industry and energy,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially in tilling the soil, and in physical (and perhaps also in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intellectual) vigour ; and they are more superstitious and fanatical,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as is indicated by the numerous amulets they wear round their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}necks and arms. They are, however, superior to the Egyptians in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cleanliness, honesty, and subordination, and possess a more highly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}developed sense of honour. The traveller must not expect to find{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them very sincerely attached or grateful, any more than the native{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians, but as servants they are certainly preferable. The Nubian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}language, which is divided into the three dialects of Kenus, Mahas,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Dongola, belongs to a special group of the African tongues; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dr. Brugsch is of opinion that it may afford a clue to the inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pretation of the still undeciphered Ethiopian (Meroitic) inscriptions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Nubian part of the Nile-valley.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Those Berbers'who do not learn Arabic grammatically never speak{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it thoroughly well; but it is generally, though imperfectly, understood{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Nubia. The traveller must therefore not expect to learn good Arabic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from his Nubian servants. In their native country the Berbers till{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the banks of the Nile, hut their land is of very limited extent and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}poorly cultivated; and as their harvests are scanty they are rarely able{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to support large families. They accordingly often emigrate at an early{% endraw %}
{% raw %}age to the richer lowlands, chiefly to the large towns, in quest of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}employment. When the Berber has succeeded in amassing a mod¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erate fortune, he returns to settle in his native country, of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}throughout his whole career he never entirely loses sight, and to{% endraw %}
