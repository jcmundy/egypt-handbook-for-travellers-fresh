{% raw %}THE MODERN EGYPTIANS. Negroes.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Each of these five classes is admirably organised as a kind of guild,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a shekh of its own, who levies a tax from each member, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guarantees the character and abilities of members when hired. Thefts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are very Tarely committed by the Nubians, but in cases of the kind the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shekh compels the whole of his subjects to contribute to repair the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}loss, and cases have been known in which several hundred pounds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have been recovered in this way. The result is that there is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strict mutual system of supervision, and suspected characters are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unceremoniously excluded from the fraternity. Nubian women are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seldom seen in Egypt except as slaves.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(6.) Sudan Negroes. Like the Berbers, most of the negroes in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt are professors of El-Islam, to the easily intelligible doctrines{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which they readily and zealously attach themselves. Most of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}older negroes and negresses with whom the traveller meets have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}originally been brought to Egypt as slaves, and belong to natives,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by whom they are treated more like members of the family than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}like servants. Although every slave who desires to be emancipated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may now with the aid of government sever the ties which hind him{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to his master, most of the negroes prefer to remain on the old foot¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing with the family which supports them and relieves them of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}anxiety of providing for themselves. The eunuchs, who also belong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}almost exclusively to the negro races, but are rapidly becoming{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rarer, very seldom avail themselves of this opportunity of regaining{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their liberty, as their emancipation would necessarily terminate the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}life of ease and luxury in which they delight. Under the present{% endraw %}
{% raw %}government slavery is very rapidly approaching complete extinction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Egypt, chiefly in consequence of changes in the mode of living,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the growing preference of the wealthy for paid servants. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}negroes who voluntarily settle in Egypt, constituting a body of con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}siderable size, form the dregs of the people and are employed in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most menial offices.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the negro-races of Central Africa to the N. of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}equator are represented at Cairo, particularly in the rank and file of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the negro regiments.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(7). Turks. Although the dynasty of the viceroys of Egypt is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Turkish origin (see p. xcviii), a comparatively small section of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}community belongs to that nation. According to the census of 1897{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there are 40,126 Turks in Egypt, but among these are reckoned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Turkish subjects from every part of the Ottoman empire. Only a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few are genuine Osmanlis. The Turks of Egypt are chiefly to be found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the towns, where most of them are government officials, soldiers,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and merchants. The Turkish language is little understood in Egypt.{% endraw %}
