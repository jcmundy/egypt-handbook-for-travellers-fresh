{% raw %}The Valley of the Nile from Khartum to the Delta, although{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from its great length (15° of latitude) necessarily possessing gTeat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}varieties of climate, forms one long unbroken tract of country, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fertilising soil of which is brought down by the Blue Nile from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abyssinian mountains.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The breadth of the Valley of the Nile, including the barren land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}immediately flanking it, varies from 5 to 9!/.2 miles in Nubia, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from 12^2 to 31 miles in Egypt. The banks, of which the eastern is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called the 'Arabian', and the western the 'Libyan', rise at places{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to upwards of 1000 ft., resembling two large canal-embankments,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between which the river has forced its passage through the plateau{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 'Nubian sandstone' (which extends to the Gebel Silsileh), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through the nummulite limestone of Upper and Central Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The breadth of the cultivable alluvial soil corresponds with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above varying width, but nowhere exceeds 10 miles. The soil de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posited by the Nile averages 33-38ft. deep in Egypt, but near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kalyub at the head of the Delta it increases to about 50 ft., the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bottom of it being at places below the level of the sea. The bed of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the river is also of considerable depth, and at low water the mud-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banks (gef) rise above its surface to a height of 25 ft. in Upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt, and 14 ft. at Cairo. These are also the depths of the various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}irrigation-wells.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Nile soil is unlike any other in the world in its composition.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}blende, and epidote , 6 per cent of oxide of iron, and 4 per cent of car¬{% endraw %}
