{% raw %}GEOLOGICAL NOTICE.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amidst the desert sand of the isthmus, which even in Lower Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forms a substratum underlying the Nile mud, and in the E. part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert is nearly covered with a solid gypseous and saline crust, the rock{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasionally crops up , or has been uncovered in the course of the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cavation of the canal. Near the Shaluf station (p. 171) a greenish-grey,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gypseous marl overlies the solid limestone, which contains the tertiary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marine conchylia, sharks' teeth, and remains of crocodiles and am¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phibious mammalia. The same formation occurs in other places also,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and ridges of the early tertiary nummulite limestone likewise occasionally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rise from the plain. At several points on the coast of the Red Sea,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}particularly near Koser (p. 347), at a height of 600-950 ft. above the sea-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}level, we find rock of the late tertiary or diluvial era containing coral,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which shows how much the land must have risen since that period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the postpliocene, or latest tertiary period, belong several isolated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deposits of sandstone near Cairo, in which are found the beautiful fos¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sil sea-urchins (Clypeaster Aegyptiacus) frequently offered for sale near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Pyramids (p. 113). One of the principal geological curiosities near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cairo is the Petrified Forest (comp. p. 111).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Above Cairo, to the S., the Nile is flanked by ranges of hills, beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, to the E., stretches the Arabian desert, and to the W. the Libyan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert. The hills on both banks of the Nile consist of early tertiary num¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mulite limestone. The strata dip gradually from south to north, so that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the farther we ascend the Nile the older are the strata that we meet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with. To the south of Edfu the nummulite limestone disappears, being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}succeeded by quartzose sandstone, belonging to the middle or upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chalk formation, and forming considerable cliffs at the Gebel Silsileh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 320), which confine the river within a narrow bed. This last formation,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}known as 'Nubian sandstone', covers many thousands of square miles of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubia and the Sudan. From Assuan to Silsileh the Nile flows through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubian sandstone, but near the ancient Syene a transverse barrier of granite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and 'syenite' advances from the east, forming the boundary between Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Nubia. This harrier extends eastwards for about 180 miles, forming{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a very irregular chain of barren hills 900-1300 ft. in height. The Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has forced a passage for itself through this hard rock, exposing to view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at places the beautiful red felspar crystals which it contains, and forms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the first cataract at Assuan (p. 347).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(2) The Aeabian Desert" (pp. 344 et seq.). Parallel with the coast of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Red Sea, a broad and massive range of mountains, rising to a height of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6600 ft. and consisting of crystalline rocks (granite, syenite, diorite, por¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phyry, hornblende-slate , gneiss, mica-slate, etc.), runs through the Arabian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Desert, sending forth numerous ramifications into the interior of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}country. This range is adjoined on the E. by roof-shaped, stratified form¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ations. At first there occurs a considerable stratum of Nubian sandstone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}next to which we find a series of clayey and calcareous strata belonging{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the upper white chalk formation. These strata are succeeded by ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tensive masses of limestone, belonging to the nummulite formation, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stretching to the Nile. These extensive mountains, with their numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}profound ravines and boldly shaped masses of rock, impart a most im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posing character to the Arabian Desert. This region is by no means so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}destitute of vegetation as is usually supposed; for, although without oases,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it contains, particularly in the N. part, a number of springs and natural{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cisterns, which are filled by the rare, but often copious, rains of winter.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(3). The Libyan Deseet. This region again presents an entirely dif¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferent character. It consists of an immense, monotonous, and stony table¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land, 650-1000 ft. above the level of the Nile, extending between the Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the oases of Khargeh, Dakhel, Farafra; and Bahriyeh. The surface{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the desert rises in gradations, each preceded by a broad girdle of isolated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mounds, which have obviously been formed by erosion, the materials{% endraw %}
{% raw %}having been washed down from the adjoining plateau. The whole of this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stony and absolutely unwatered plain, the monotony of which is only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}varied by a few solitary ranges of sand-hills, consists of nummulite lime¬{% endraw %}
