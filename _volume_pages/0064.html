{% raw %}d. Agriculture and Vegetation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I. Capabilities of the Soil. In the time of the Pharaohs the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian agricultural year was divided into three equal parts,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the period of the inundation (from the end of June to the end of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}October), that of the growing of the crops (from the end of Oc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tober to the end of February), and that of the harvest (from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}end of February to the end of June). At the present day there are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two principal seasons, corresponding to our summer and winter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}besides which there is a short additional season, corresponding with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the late summer or early autumn of the European year. The land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is extremely fertile, hut it is not so incapable of exhaustion as it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sometimes represented to be. Many of the crops, as elsewhere, must{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasionally be followed by a fallow period ; others thrive only when{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a certain rotation is observed (such as wheat, followed by clover and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beans); and some fields require to be artificially manured. Occa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sionally two crops are yielded by the same field in the same season{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(wheat and saffron, wheat and clover, etc.). The great extension with¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the last thirty or forty years of the cultivation of the sugar-cane,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which requires a great deal of moisture, and ofthe cotton-plant, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}requires extremely little, has necessitated considerable modifications{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the modes of irrigation and cultivation hitherto in use. As both of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these crops are of a very exhausting character, the land must either{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be more frequently left fallow, or must be artificially manured. The{% endraw %}
