{% raw %}e. The Climate of Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By Leigh Canney, M.D. (Lond.), F.R. Met. Soc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The blue cloudless sky, the powerful sunlight, and the dry{% endraw %}
{% raw %}warm air are among the first facts that strike the traveller on his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arrival in Egypt; and his surprise increases when he observes that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the conditions remain uniform day after day, and are, in short, so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely the rule that 'the weather' ceases to be a topic of conver¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sation. If from the top of the hills or cliffs bordering the Nile valley{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the S. of Cairo he looks out on the boundless deserts on either{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side, the visitor will realize at once that Ejiypt is practically a part{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Sahara, a verdant strip of fertile soil, 8-12 M. wide, depend¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ent for its existence upon the Nile; and that the refreshing purity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the atmosphere is essetitially due to the proximity of the desert.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The atmosphere of the Libyan Desert, to the W. of the Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}valley, is so dry in winter, that dew is rarely seen even when the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thermometer falls to freezing-point; and a shower condensed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the upper air is often dissipated before it can reach the earth. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}daily range of temperature recorded here by Rohlfs was 35°, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is more than is usual in Egypt; the minimum noted was 6°-8°{% endraw %}
{% raw %}below freezing-point and the maximum 122°. During the warm{% endraw %}
{% raw %}season (from about May to October) the hot desert air ascends and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a current of cold air streams in from the Mediterranean. Thus arises{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a North Wind, the famous 'Etesian Wind' of Herodotus, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}greatly tempers the heat of summer throughout Egypt, and which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is more and more deprived of moisture as it proceeds farther and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther to the S. During the remaining months West Winds prevail,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, rising in the N. and central portions of the Libyan Desert, reach{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lower Egypt from the S.W. and Upper Egypt from the N.W. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}winter the W. wind is often piercingly cold at Cairo and Alexandria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and cool in Upper Egypt, owing to the fact that the Libyan Desert{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Sahara are colder than Cairo by 3° or 4°, while Cairo is colder{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in winter than Alexandria by 3°. In spring, during the fifty days{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before the summer-solstice, a strong, hot, dry, and sand-laden wind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the S.W. blows at irregular intervals for a day or two at a time,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and is known as the Khamsin (from Arab, khamasln, fifty). The sun{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is then often obscured, the temperature may be raised to 100-106°,{% endraw %}
