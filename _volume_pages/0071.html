{% raw %}In addition to the Mediterranean Sea and the Libyan Desert a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}third factor influencing the climate of Egypt is found in the extent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of cultivated land. Alexandria, owing to the prevailing winds, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the influence of both sea and desert in winter, but from May{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to October under that of the sea alone. Cairo, at the apex of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Delta, is partly affected by the desert in winter, but from May to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}October it is wholly under the influence of the Delta and its cultivated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land. Upper Egypt is much less affected by this third factor than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lower Egypt, for the bends of the Nile valley and the very limited{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivation permit the N. and N.W. winds to pass over large tracts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of desert before it reaches Upper Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The mean Temperature in winter at Cairo and in the greater{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of the Delta is 56°Fahr. ; the temperature is lowest in the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}half of Jan., being then about that of London or Berlin at the end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of September. In spring the mean is 78°, in summer 83° (maximum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}95°, or during the Khamsin 115°), in autumn 66°. Alexandria is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more influenced by the sea, and it is therefore warmer in winter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and cooler in summer than Cairo. The mean winter temperature is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}59° (minimum 41°). In Upper Egypt, where the desert climate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prevails almost exclusively, the mean winter temperature is 66°{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(minimum 37°), and in summer the heat sometimes rises to 122°{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the shade. Throughout the whole of Egypt the temperature{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reaches the minimum just before sunrise, when it is very cold, rises{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quickly until 11 a. m., then more slowly till it touches the max¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imum at 2 or 3 p. m., after which it gradually falls again. There{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is no sudden fall of temperature at sunset, except in the cultivated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land. The temperature at night is considerably affected by vege¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tation. Observations made with automatic recording instruments in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the neighbourhood of Cairo and at Luxor have demonstrated that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when the crops are high the nocturnal temperature sinks much lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the cultivated land than in the towns or in the desert.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The temperatures mentioned above will probably seem high to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}any Northern traveller; but as a matter of fact they are not felt to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be oppressive owing to the great Dryness of the Egyptian climate,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which has a drying power thrice as great as that of the air in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Engadine and thus rapidly absorbs all moisture generated at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surface of the body. Rain is a rare phenomenon in Upper Egypt.{% endraw %}
