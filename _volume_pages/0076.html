{% raw %}lxvi DOCTRINES OF EL-ISLAM.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Magog will burst the barrier beyond which they were banished by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexander the Great. The end of all things will be ushered in by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the trumpet-blasts of the angel Asrafil; the first of these blasts will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kill every living being ; a second will awaken the dead. Then follows{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Judgment; the righteous cross to Paradise by a bridge of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hair's breadth, while the wicked fall from the bridge into the abyss{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of hell. Some Muslims believe in a kind of limbo, like that of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hebrews and Greeks, while others maintain that the souls of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dead proceed directly to the gates of Paradise. At the Judgment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every man is judged according to the books of the recording angels{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. lxiv). The good have the book placed in their right hands,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but it is placed in the left hands of the wicked, bound behind.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their backs. The scales in which good and evil deeds are weighed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}play an important part in deciding the soul's fate, a detail which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gave rise to the subsequent doctrine of the efficacy of works. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}doctrine is carried so far that works of supererogation are believed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to he placed to the credit ofthe believer. The daemons and animals,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}too, must be judged. Hell, as well as heaven, has different regions ;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and there is also a purgatory, from which release is possible. Par¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adise is depicted by Mohammed, in consonance with his thoroughly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sensual character, as a place of entirely material delights.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The course of all events, including the salvation or perdition{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of every individual, is, according to the Koran, absolutely predes¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tined ; although several later sects have endeavoured to modify this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terrible doctrine. It is these views, however, which give rise to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the pride of the Muslims. By virtue of their faith they regard them¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}selves as certainly elect, and they make no attempt to convert oth¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ers , as they have no power to alter the irrevocable decrees of God.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the second place the Koran is considered to contain, not only a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}standard of ethics, but also the foundation of a complete code of law.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Morality of El-Islam was specially adapted by its founder{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the character of the Arabs. Of duties to one's neighbour, charity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the most highly praised, and instances of its practice are not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfrequent. Hospitality is much practised by the Beduins, and by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the peasantry also in those districts which are not overrun with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travellers. Frugality is another virtue of the Arabs, though too apt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with them to degenerate into avarice and cupidity. The law of debtor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and creditor is lenient. Lending money at interest is forbidden by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Koran, but is nevertheless largely practised, the lowest rate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Syria being 12 per cent. The prohibition against eating unclean{% endraw %}
{% raw %}animals, such as swine, is older than El-Islam. It is impossible{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to decide whether the prohibition of intoxicating drinks is due to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the prevalence of habits of over-indulgence, such as are depicted by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the poets before Mohammed's time, or is based on sanitary consider¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ations. Wine, however, and-even brandy, are largely consumed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the upper classes, especially among the Turks.{% endraw %}
