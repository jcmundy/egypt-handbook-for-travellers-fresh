{% raw %}With regard to theological, legal, and still more to ritualistic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}questions, El-Islam has not always been free from dissension. There{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are in the first place four Orthodox sects, the Haneptes, the Shdfe-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'ites, the Malekites, and the Hambalites, named after their founders.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In addition to these must he mentioned the schools of Free Think¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ers, who sprang up at an early period, partly owing to the influence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Greek philosophy. The orthodox party, however, triumphed, not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only over these heretics, but also in its struggle against the volup¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tuousness and luxury of the most glorious period of the khalifs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ascetism and fanaticism were also largely developed among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}professors of El-Islam, and another phase of religious thought was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pure Mysticism, which arose chiefly in Persia. The mystics (sup) in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terpret many texts of the Koran allegorically, and their system there¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fore frequently degenerated into Pantheism. It was by mystics who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still remained within the pale of El-Islam (such as the famous Ibn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-'Arabi, born in 1164) that the Orders of Dervishes were founded.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dervishes (daruish, plur. dardwish). That earthly life is worthless, that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it is a delusion, and at best a period of probation, are sentiments of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequent recurrence in the Koran. This pessimist view of life has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confirmed by Mohammed's conception of the Supreme Being, on whose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}awe-inspiring attributes he has chiefly dwelt, thus filling his adherents{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a profound dread of their Creator. The result of this doctrine was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to induce devout persons to retire altogether from the wicked world, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scene of vanity and disappointment, and to devote themselves to the prac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tice of ascetic exercises, with a view to ensure their happiness in a iuture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}state. The fundamental aim of this asceticism was to strive after a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}knowledge of God by cultivating a kind of half-conscious and ecstatic ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altation of mind. A mystic love of God was deemed the great passport{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which enabled the worshipper to fall into this ecstatic trance, and to lose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself so completely in contemplation as to destroy his own individuality{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(fand) and blend it with that of the Deity (ittihdd). As in Europe the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monastic system and the mendicant orders sprang from the example of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}penitents and hermits who had renounced the world, so in the Mohammedan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}world asceticism was rapidly developed into an organised system of men¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dicancy. At an early period many noble thinkers (such as the Persians{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sa'di and Hafiz) and talented poets enrolled themselves in the ranks of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ascetics, but the dervishes of the present day have entirely lost the spirit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of their prototypes, and have retained nothing hut the mere physical ca¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pacity for throwing themselves into a mechanical state of ecstasy and rend¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ering themselves proof against external sensations.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Rifa'a el-Kebir, possess a monastery near the mosque of Sultan Hasan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(see p. 49), and are recognisable by their black flags and black or'dark{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blue turbans. The best-known sects of this order are the Vldd 'Ilwdn,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or 'Ilwdniyeh Dervishes, and the Sa'diyeh Dervishes. The former are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}noted for their extraordinary performances at festivals, such as thrusting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}iron nails into their eyes and arms, breaking large stones against their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chests, as they lie on their backs on the ground, and swallowing burning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}charcoal and fragments of glass. The Sa'diyeh, who usually carry{% endraw %}
{% raw %}green flags, are snake-charmers (p. xxiv), and on the Friday on which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the birthday of the prophet is celebrated used to allow their shekh to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ride over them on horseback (the dSseh; p. lxxvi).{% endraw %}
