{% raw %}(2) The Kddirtyeh (sing, kddiri), an order founded by the celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Seiyid 'Abd el-Kadir el-Gilarii, have white banners and white turbans.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of them spend their time in fishing, and in their processions they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carry nets of different colours, fishing-rods, and other insignia of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief pursuit.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(3). The Senusiyeh, founded by the Algerian Mohammed ben-'Ali es-SenUsi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. lx59), have spread over Arabia and the entire N. of Africa, especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Egyptian oases of the Libyan desert. The residence of the chief{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the order was recently removed from the oasis of Gaghabub to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}oasis of Kufr. The teaching of Senusi was directed towards a return to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the original strictness of Islam and to its emancipation from the dominion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Europeans and other heretics. The members of the order abstain from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}music, singing, dancing, tobacco, and coffee.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(4). The Sammdniyeh, established at the end of the 18th cent, by Shekh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sammdn, practise extreme asceticism, seeking seclusion for the purpose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in cells, caves, deserts, and even in the sea. Mohammed Ahmed, the mahdi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Khartum (p. cii), who appeared at El-Obeid in 1880 as the 'Summoned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of God', joined this order.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(5) The Ahmediyeh (sing, ahmedi), the order of Seiyid Ahmed el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bedawi, are recognised by their red banners and red turbans. This order{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is very numerous and is much respected. It is divided into many sects,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but of these the two most important only need be mentioned. One of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Shinndwiyeh, who play an important part in the ceremonies at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tomb of Seiyid Ahmed at Tanta (p. 22). The other sect is that of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Uldd Nuh, who are generally young men, wearing high pointed caps with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a plume of strips of coloured cloth, and a number of small balls strung{% endraw %}
{% raw %}across their breasts, and carrying wooden swords and a kind of whip{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made of a thick plait of rope.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There are also many other orders which it is unnecessary to enumerate.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ceremony of the admission of members to all these orders is a very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}simple matter. The candidate (el-murid) performs the customary ablutions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sits down on the ground beside the superior (el-murshid, or spiritual,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leader), gives him his hand, and repeats after him a set form of words,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in which he expresses penitence for his sins and his determination to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reform, and calls Allah to witness that he will never quit the order. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceremony terminates with three recitals of the confession of faith by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}murid, the joint repetition of thefatha (p. lxviii), and a kissing of hands.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The religious exercises of all the dervishes consist chiefly in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}performance of Zikrs (i.e. pious devotions, or invocations of Allah; see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}below). Almost all the dervishes in Egypt are small tradesmen, artizans,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or peasants. Most of them are married men, and they take part in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceremonies peculiar to their order at stated seasons only. Some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them, however, make it their business to attend festivals and funerals{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the purpose of exhibiting their zikrs. These last are called fukara{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(sing, fakir), i. e. 'poor men'. Others again support themselves by drawing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}water (h'emali; see p. 37). Those who lead a vagrant life and subsist on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alms are comparatively few in number. The dervishes of this class{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually wear a kind of gown (dilk) composed of shreds of rags of various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colours sewn together, or a shaggy coat of skins, and carry a stick{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with strips of cloth of various colours attached to the upper end. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}considerable number of them are insane, in which case they are highly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}revered by the people, and are regarded as specially favoured by God,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who has taken their spirits to heaven, while he has left their earthly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tabernacle behind.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Zikrs of the Dancing and the Howling Dervishes are the best{% endraw %}
{% raw %}known.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The howling or shouting dervishes perform their zikr at Cairo on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Frid. (1.30-2.30 p.m.) in the little mosque beside the Kasr el-'Ain (p. 41).{% endraw %}
