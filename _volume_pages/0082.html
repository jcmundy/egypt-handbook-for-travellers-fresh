{% raw %}The Worship op Saints and Martyrs was inculcated in con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nection with El-Islam at an early period. The faithful undertook{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pilgrimages to the graves of the departed in the belief that death{% endraw %}
{% raw %}did not interrupt the possibility of communication with them.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thus the tomb of Mohammed at Medina, and that of his grandson{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hosen at Kerbela, became particularly famous, and every little{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town soon boasted, of the tomb of its particular saint. In many of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the villages the traveller will observe small dome-covered build¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings with grated windows. These are saints' tombs and are called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Shekhs' (comp. p. clxiii). 'Shekh' also means a chief or old man.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shreds of cloth are often seen suspended from the gratings of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tombs, or on certain trees which are considered sacred, having{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been placed there by devout persons. About the end of the 18th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century a reaction against the abuses of El-Islam sprang up in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Central Arabia. The Wahhabites, or Wahhabees, named after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their founder 'Abd el-Wahhab, endeavoured to restore the religion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to its original purity; they destroyed all tombs of saints, including{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even those of Mohammed and Hosen, as objects of superstitious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reverence, and sought to restore the primitive simplicity of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prophet's code of morals ; they even forbade smoking as being in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}toxicating. They soon became a great political power, and had not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mohammed 'Ali deemed it his interest to suppress them, their in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fluence would have been far more widely extended than it now is.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We have hitherto spoken of the doctrines of the Sunnites (from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sunna, 'tradition'), who form one great sect of El-Islam. At an early{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period the Shi'ites (from shl'a, 'sect') seceded from the Sunnites.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}They assigned to 'AH, the son-in-law of Mohammed, a rank equal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or even superior to that of the prophet himself; they regarded him{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as an incarnation of the Deity, and believed in the divine mission{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the imams descended from him. El-Mahdi, the last of these, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}believed by them not to have died, but to be awaiting in conceal¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment the coming of the last day. The Persians are all Shi'ites. To¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards the West also Shi'itism was widely disseminated at an early{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period, particularly in Egypt under the regime of the Fatimite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sovereigns. The Shi'ites are extremely fanatical, refusing even to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eat in the society of persons of a different creed. As to the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sects, chiefly confined to Syria (Metdwileh, Isma'lllyeh, Nosairlyeh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Druses, etc.), see Baedeker s Syria and Palestine.{% endraw %}
