{% raw %}their flags contribute; water is distributed; and the riding-horse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a buffalo are led in the procession. The buffalo is slaughtered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the tomb and its flesh distributed among the poor.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Another custom peculiar to the Muslims is the separation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sexes even after death. In family-vaults one side is set apart{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the men, the other for the women. Between these vaults is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrance to the tomb, usually covered with a single large slab. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vaults are high enough to admit of the deceased sitting upright in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them when he is being examined by the angels Munkar and Nekir{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the first night after his interment (see p. lxiv).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Religious and Popular Festivals of the Mohammedans.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The dates of these festivals, which may all be seen to the best advan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tage at Cairo, cannot easily be given according to the European computa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of time, owing to the variable character of the Arabian lunaryearfcomp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. lxix). Calendars reducing the Mohammedan and Coptic reckoning of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time to the European system may, however, be obtained at any bookseller's.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first month of the Arabian year is the Moharrem, the first ten{% endraw %}
{% raw %}days of which f'ashr), and particularly the 10th (ydm 'ashura), are con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sidered holy. On these days alms are distributed, and amulets purchased.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mothers, even of the upper classes, carry their children on their should¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ers, or cause, them to be carried, through the streets, and sew into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}children's caps the copper coins presented to them by passers-by. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10th Moharrem, the highly revered 'Ashilra day, on which Adam and Eve{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are said first to have met after their expulsion from Paradise, on which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Noah is said to have left the ark, and on which Hosen, the grandson of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Prophet, fell as a martyr to his religion at the battle of Kerbela, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gami'a el-Hosen (p. 42) is visited by a vast concourse of religious de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}votees, whose riotous proceedings had better not be inspected except from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a carriage. Troops of Persians in long white robes parade the streets,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cutting themselves with swords in the forehead until the blood streams{% endraw %}
{% raw %}down and stains their snowy garments. Two hoys, representing Hasan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Hosen, are also led through the streets on horseback, with blood¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stained clothes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the end of Safar, the second month, or at the beginning of Rabi'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-awwel, the third', the Mecca Caeavan (p. lxix) returns home, its ap¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proach being announced by outriders. Some of the faithful who go to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}meet the procession proceed as far as three days' journey, but most of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them await its arrival at the Birket el-Hagg (p. 108), or pilgrims' lake.{% endraw %}
