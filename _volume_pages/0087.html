{% raw %}POPULAR FESTIVALS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lxxvii{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The month Ramadan is succeeded by that of Shawivdl, on the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}three days of which is celebrated the first and minor festival of rejoicing,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called by the Arabs El-'ld es-Sughayyir (the lesser feast), but better{% endraw %}
{% raw %}known by its Turkish name of''Beirdm. The object of the festival is to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}give expression to the general rejoicing at the termination ofthe fast;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and, as at our Christmas, parents give presents to their children, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masters to their servants at this festive season. Friends embrace each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other on meeting, and visits of ceremony are exchanged. During this fes¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tival the Khedive also receives his principal officials, ambassadors, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At this season the traveller may also pay a visit to the cemetery by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Bab en-Nasr, or to one of the others, where numerous Cairenes assemble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to place palm-branches or basilicum (rihdn) on the graves of their deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relatives, and to distribute dates, bread, and other gifts among the poor.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A few days after the Beiram, the pieces of the Kisweh, or covering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manufactured at Constantinople, at the cost of the Sultan, for the Ka'ba{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(the most sacred sanctuary in the interior of the temple at Mecca), whither{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it is annually carried by the pilgrims, are conveyed in procession to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the citadel, where they are sewn together and lined. The ceremonies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which take place on this occasion are repeated on a grander scale towards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the end of the month of Shawwdl (generally the 23rd), when there is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gay procession of the escort which accompanies the pilgrimage caravan to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mecca, and also takes charge of the Mahmal (p. lxxvi). On this occasion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every true believer in the prophet, if he possibly can, spends the whole day{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the streets. The women don their smartest attire. Many of the harem{% endraw %}
{% raw %}windows are opened, and the veiled inmates gaze into the streets. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief scene of the ceremonies is the Rumeleh (PI. F, 2), where a sumptuous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tent of red velvet and gold is pitched for the reception of the dignitaries.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The procession is headed by soldiers, who are followed by camels adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with gaily coloured trappings, and bearing on their humps bunches of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palm - branches with oranges attached. Each section of the cavalcade is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preceded by an Arabian band of music, the largest section being that which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accompanies the Takht Rawdn, or litter of the Emir el-Hagg, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}next in order that of the Delil el-Hagg, or leader of the pilgrims, with his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attendants. Next follow various detachments of pilgrims and dervishes with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banners, and lastly the Mahmal (see p. lxxvi). A picturesque appearance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is presented by the camp of the assembled pilgrims (Haggi) at the Birket{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Hagg (p. 108), whence the caravan finally starts for Mecca.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}' On the 10th of Dhil-higgeh, the twelfth month, begins the great fes¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tival of El-'ld el-Kebir, which resembles the lesser feast (el-'id es-sug-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hayyir) already mentioned. On this day, if on no other throughout the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}year, every faithful Muslim eats a piece of meat in memory of the sacri¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fice of Abraham, and the poor are presented with meat by the rich.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With the Rising of the Nile there are also connected several inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esting festivals, closely resembling those of the ancient period of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pharaohs, which even the Christian epoch was unable entirely to ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}literate. As, however, they take place in summer, few travellers will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have an opportunity of witnessing them. As these festivals have refer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ence to a regularly recurring phenomenon of nature, their dates are ne¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cessarily fixed in accordance with the Coptic solar reckoning of time, in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stead of the variable Arabian lunar year. — The night of the 11th of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Coptic month Ba'una (17th June) is called Lelet en-Nukta, i.e. the 'night{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the drop', as it is believed that a drop from heaven for a tear of Isis,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}according to the ancient Egyptian myth) falls into the Nile on this night{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and causes its rise. The astrologers profess to calculate precisely the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hour of the fall of the sacred drop. The Cairenes spend this night on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the banks of the Nile, either in the open air, or in the houses of friends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}near the river, and practise all kinds of superstitious customs. One of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these consists in the placing of a piece of dough by each member of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}family on the roof of the house; if the dough rises, happiness is in store{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the person who placed it there, while its failure to rise is regarded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a bad omen. On 21st June the river begins slowly to rise (comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 1). On the 27th of the Coptic month Ba'una (3rd July) the Munddi{% endraw %}
