{% raw %}XIII. and XIV. DYNASTIES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The monarchs of the 13th Dynasty, most of whom were named{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sebekhotep, maintained Egypt at the height of her power for some{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time, but a period of decline afterwards set in. There is no period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Egyptian history at which kings were more numerous, most of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them reigning but a short time. The South was probably ruled by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the descendants of the ancient Theban kings, while in the town of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Xois, in the W. Delta, another family raised themselves to power,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forming the 14th Dynasty.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5. Hyksos Period (ca. 1700-1575 B.C.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}XV. and XVI. DYNASTIES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt was now conquered by a Semitic people, known as Hyksos,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}i.e. 'Shepherd Kings' or, perhaps more correctly, 'princes of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shosu' or Syrian Beduins. Few of their monuments have been pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served (statue of Kheyan, see p. 86); but it is evident that they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conformed to the ancient culture of Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6. The New Empire (1600-1100B.C.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt became a great power during this period. At first the culture of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the New Empire differed little from that of the Middle Empire, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under Thutmosis III. political and social life as well as the art of Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}underwent a radical change, owing to the new relations with W. A«ia.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The tribute paid by foreign states caused an enormous flood of wealth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to pour into Egypt, and especially into Thebes, the capital. The earlier{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buildings, that had fallen into disrepair, were now replaced by imposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monuments, such as the temples at Karnak, Luxor, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}XVII. DYNASTY (ca. 1600-1545).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}While the Hyksos kings maintained themselves in the N., the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was ruled by Theban princes, who were at first vassals of the foreign{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intruders. The tombs of these princes lie near Drah Abu'l Negga.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sekenyen-Re I., II., III. The mummy of one of these was found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Der el-bahri (p. 278).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kemose. His queen was perhaps Ahhotep, whose jewels are now in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Museum at Gizeh (p. 83).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amosis (Ahmose, 1580-1555 B.C.) conquered Auaris, the chief fort¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ress of the Hyksos, and expelled the intruders from Egypt,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was reunited under one sceptre.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenophis I. (Amenhotep, 1555-1545 B.C.). This king and his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mother Nefret-ere were afterwards regarded as the patron-gods of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Necropolis of Thebes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*XVIII. DYNASTY (1545-1350 B.C.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thutmosis I. (Thutmose, 1545-1515 B.C.) re-conquered Nubia. His{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tomb at Biban el-Muluk (p. 277) was the first royal rock-tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}constructed under the Pharaohs. During his lifetime his children{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fought for the succession.{% endraw %}
