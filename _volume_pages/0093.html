{% raw %}Ramses II. (Ramesse, 1324-1258 B.C.), the most celebrated of all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian kings. He waged tedious wars against the Hittites{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(battle of Kadesh, p. 286), finally making a peace with them in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 21st year of his reign (p. 252), which left Palestine proper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the possession of the Egyptians, while N. Syria was acknow¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ledged to be tributary to the Hittites. Ramses developed an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extraordinary building activity in the course of his reign of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}67 years. Perhaps one-half of all the extant temples date from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this reign; and the name of Ramses is found in nearly every{% endraw %}
{% raw %}group of ruins in Egypt. His largest temples were those of Abu{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Simbel(p. 370), Karnak (p. 245), Luxor, theRamesseum, Abydos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 218), Memphis (p. 131), and Bubastis. His tomb is at Biban{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Muluk (p. 270); his mummy at Gizeh (p. 80). Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is frequently identified with the 'Pharaoh of the Oppression'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Exod.i, 11). Of his numerous sons only one survived him, viz.—{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenephthes (Merenptah), who carried on campaigns against the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Libyans and their allies, the peoples of the Mediterranean. His{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mortuary temple is at Thebes (p. 287); his grave at Biban el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Muluk (p. 267).{% endraw %}

{% raw %}*XX. DYNASTY (1200-1100 B.C.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Set-nakht succeeded in restoring peace.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses III. (Ramesse) conquered the Libyans and in two great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}battles repelled an invasion of barbarians who approached from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Asia Minor by land and by water, threatening Egypt. His reign{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of 33 years was thereafter an epoch of peace and quiet, in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several large buildings (e.g. the temple at Medinet Habu,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 299) were erected. The king presented great gifts to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods, especially to the Theban Amnion, who had been richly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}endowed by former kings also. The high-priest of Ammon gradu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ally became the greatest power in the state. The king's tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is at Biban el-Muluk (p. 268) ; his mummy at Gizeh (p. 80).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}His successors —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses IV.-Ramses XII. gradually fell more and more under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}control of the priests of Ammon. Their tombs are at Biban el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Muliik (pp. 264 et seq.). After the death of Ramses XII. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Herihor, high-priest of Ammon, seized the throne.{% endraw %}
