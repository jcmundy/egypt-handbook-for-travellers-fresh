{% raw %}Nektanebes (Nekht-Har-ehbet), of Sebennytos, built a temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Isis at Behbit (p. 158).{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Nektanehos (Nekhte-nebof) was a powerful monarch, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whose reign large temples (e.g. atPhila?, p. 338) were{% endraw %}

{% raw %}5. The Graeco-Roman Period (332 B.C.-640 A.D.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1. Alexander the Great and the Ptolemaic Period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Under the Ptolemies the lower valley of the Nile became{% endraw %}
{% raw %}once more for three centuries the seat of a brilliant kingdom,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at first under gifted rulers of the most prosperous, richest, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most powerful state in the world, but afterwards condemned to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shameful impotence under their vicious and degenerate posterity,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}torn by fratricidal wars, and existing only by the favour of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, until it was involved in the domestic struggles of Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and finally perished'. The customs and religious views of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians were respected by the Ptolemies, who represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}themselves to the native population as the descendants of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient Pharaohs. Large temples were built during this period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexander the Great tolerated the native religion and visited{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the oasis of Ammon in 331, where he was hailed by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priests as a son of Ammon. He founded Alexandria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 7), which soon became the centre of Greek culture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and of the commerce of the whole world. After his death{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 323 the Macedonian empire fell to pieces. Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}became the satrapy of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemy I. Soter I., son of Lagus, who carried on the govern¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment at first for Philippus Arrhidaeus and Alexander II.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}son of Alexander the Great, and then for the latter alone.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexander II. died in 311, and Ptolemy assumed the title{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of king in 305. The Museum at Alexandria (p. 9) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolema'is Hermiu (p. 217), in Upper Egypt, were founded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in this reign.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemy II. Philadelphus married first Arsinoe I., daughter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Lysimachus, then his sister Arsinoe II. Arsinoe II. was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named patron-goddess of the Fayum, which was entitled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 'Arsinoite nome' in her honour. Under Philadelphus{% endraw %}
