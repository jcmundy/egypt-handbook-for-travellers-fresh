{% raw %}VII. Religion of the Ancient Egytians.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By Prof. G. Steindorff.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In spite of the numerous religious inscriptions and represent¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ations that have come down to us from Egyptian antiquity our{% endraw %}
{% raw %}knowledge of the Egyptian religion is comparatively slight. "We are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indeed acquainted with the names and aspects of many deities and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we know in what temples they were worshipped, hut of the true{% endraw %}
{% raw %}essence of these deities, of the particular significance attributed to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them by priests and people, ofthe myths attached to the personality{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of each, we know very little. One thing, however, is certain; viz.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that the Egyptian religion was developed in prehistoric times in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}separate and independent communities. Each town, each village,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each hamlet had its own god, its own patron deity, to whom the in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}habitants prayed when need or danger threatened, whose favour{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they courted with gifts, and whose anger they sought to avert with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacrifices. The names of these local deities and the aspects assigned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to them by their various worshippers differentiate them very mark¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}edly. The god of the region of the cataracts, for example, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named Khnum, the god of Thebes Amon or Ammon, and the god of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Heliopolis Alum. Frequently they possessed no special name, hut{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were distinguished only in terms of the town where their worship{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was celebrated, as, e.g., He of Ombos, She of Bast (Bubastis), etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The actual form also of the deity was affected by the character of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the town and his worshippers. A locality in which the manufacture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of pottery was the chief industry represented its god as a great.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}potter, who had formed the entire world out of clay moulded on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}potter's wheel; in agricultural districts the deities were gods of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}harvest; places inhabited by flsherfolk worshipped water-gods.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}When a small town increased in power and extended its authority{% endraw %}
{% raw %}over an entire district or province, the jurisdiction of its god was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}likewise extended, and the 'town god' became a 'provincial god'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It probably often happened also that the inhabitants of a certain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town emigrated to new settlements; in such cases they doubt¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less carried their deities with them and erected new temples to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them in the new home. Sometimes the effective protection and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abundant benefits bestowed on his worshippers by some local deity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}might attract the attention of less fortunate neighbours and induce{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them also to rear a temple for him and worship him. Thus in various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ways gods became known in towns to which they were not indig¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enous and obtained circles of worshippers side by side with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purely local deities.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}be no doubt that the nation at the very earliest period had a certain{% endraw %}
