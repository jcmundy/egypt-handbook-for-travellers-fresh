{% raw %}animal-worship. It was probably only this excessive expansion of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}animal-worship that struck the Greeks in Egypt as remarkable. For{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traces of a similar worship were common to various Oriental peoples,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and even among the Greeks and Romans themselves certain animals{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were regarded as sacred to the gods, as, e.g., the lions of Cyhele, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}owl of Athena, and the eagle of Zeus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A stage beyond fetishism was reached when the Egyptians began{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to form an anthropomorphic conception of their deities. The gods{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had human forms and wore clothing like human beings. Like prin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ces, they wore on their heads helmets or crowns, and, like the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}primaeval rulers, they had lions' tails fastened to the back of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aprons. They bore the sceptre or the commander's baton as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}symbol of their might. The deities that were conceived of as animals{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now received human figures, with the heads of the animals sacred{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to them. Thus Sobk appears as a man with a crocodile's head,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khnum as a man with a goat's head, the ibis-headed Thout and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ram-headed Ammon have human bodies, etc. Though such a device{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cannot but appear both strange and repellant to us as it did to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greeks, it must be confessed that the Egyptian artists in their reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and statues of those animal-headed gods managed the transition from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the animal's head to the human body with remarkable skill. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}god frequently had a wife and a son, and in that case this so-called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Triad dwelt and was worshipped in one temple. Divine families{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of this kind are exemplified in Plah, god of Memphis, with his wife{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sekhmet and his son Nefertem, and by Osiris, Isis, and Horus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Atum, the local deity of Heliopolis, had as many as eight com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}panions assigned to him; and the worship of the nine gods became{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so popular that it was adopted in many different localities, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place of Atum being taken by the local god in each.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Human passions and virtues were attributed to the gods; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous tales were told by the faithful of the divine exploits and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adventures. Unfortunately most of these myths have perished; of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the few that have come down to us the best known is the story of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Osiris, which in antiquity also was one ofthe most widely spread.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Osiris ruled as king over Egypt and the country enjoyed the bless¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings of prosperity. But Set, his wicked brother, conspired against{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him, and at a banquet persuaded him to enter a cunningly wrought{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chest, which he and his seventy-two accomplices then closed and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}threw into the Nile. The river carried the chest down to the sea,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the waves at length washed it ashore near the Phoenician By-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blos. Meanwhile Isis roamed in distress throughout the country,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seeking her lost husband; and she at length succeeded in discovering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his coffin, which she carried to a sequestered spot and concealed.{% endraw %}
