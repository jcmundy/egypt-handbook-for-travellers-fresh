{% raw %}RELIGION.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cxxiii{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous deities had long been explained as in reality one with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the great sun-god (comp. p. 203). The representations and names{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ammon and his fellow-gods were everywhere obliterated. But{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after the death of Amenophis the partizans of Ammon speedily{% endraw %}
{% raw %}regained the upper hand; the new religion was abolished, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}earlier creed restored. The Egyptian religion remained in its former{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confusion; the process of amalgamating different gods became more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and more common; and religious belief gradually lost all living{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reality. Men clung anxiously to the ancient traditions, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}superstitious belief in amulets and magic as the only protection{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against harmful influences gained universal sway. But no fresh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious conceptions are to he found in the innumerable texts in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scribed upon the temples, tombs, and sarcophagi of the later period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A few Egyptian deities, however, such as Isis, Harpocrates, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Serapis (who was introduced into Egypt under the Ptolemies),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}retained sufficient influence to find their way into the Roman pan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theon, and to gather round them a considerable crowd of worship¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pers in the Roman empire. The old religion of Egypt was gradually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vanquished only by the power of Christianity.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Future Life. A considerable diversity of doctrine as to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fate of man after death prevailed amongst the Egyptians. Different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}localities held different views as to the future state and the life{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there led, and these various views were never reduced to a single{% endraw %}
{% raw %}authoritative creed. The only point that was common to the whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}people was the firm conviction that the life of man did not end at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}death, but that on the contrary men continued to live just as they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had lived upon earth, provided that the necessaries of existence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were assured to them. It thus seemed specially necessary that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}body should be carefully interred and protected from decay. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}next step was to build a house for the deceased, after the pattern{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his earthly abode, in which he might dwell, and which, according{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the popular belief, he could quit at pleasure during the day.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Statues, erected in a special room for the purpose, represented the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}owner of the house, his family, and his domestics. Sacrificial of¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferings provided the deceased with food, and pious endowments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ensured him against hunger and thirst even in the distant future.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nor was this all; representations of food, utensils, etc. were painted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or carved upon the walls of the tomb or the sides of the sarcophagus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and it was believed that through magic these representations could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}serve the deceased in place of the real things. Ornaments, cloth¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing, etc., also were placed in the tomb or depicted on the walls for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the same purpose. The occupations that engrossed the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while on earth, the pleasures that he delighted in, the dignities{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that he enjoyed, awaited him beyond the tomb, and these too were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represented on the walls in order that he might really possess them.{% endraw %}
