{% raw %}RELIGION.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cxxv{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In flat contradiction to these doctrines was the popular belief{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that man possessed not only a body but also a soul (ba) or spirit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(ekh), which lived after death. This was originally conceived of in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the shape of a bird; at a later period as a bird with a human head.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It was believed that the spirit left the body at death and flew freely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about, but could return to the body at pleasure, provided, of course,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that the latter remained whole and did not fall a prey to decay.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thus from ancient times everything was done in Egypt to prevent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the destruction of the body, and this object was so completely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attained by embalming that the features of numerous mummies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have remained perfectly recognizable to this day. Under the Ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire human bodies were preserved with the aid of bitumen and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rolled in linen bandages and wrappings. The process of embalming{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was more elaborate at later times. The brains were first removed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through the nostrils by means of an iron hook; the stomach was then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opened with a flint knife and the viscera removed (Herodotus II,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}86) and placed in four jars, known as Canopi. These were usually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closed with lids, bearing the heads of the four sons of Osiris, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whose protection the intestines were committed. The heart also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was removed from the body, and replaced by a stone scarabaeus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}placed upon the breast of the deceased, beneath the wrappings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Herodotus states that at a later period there were three methods of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}embalming, differing according to the expense involved.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A prominent place in the belief of the ancient Egyptians was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also taken by another immaterial part of mortals, distinct from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}soul. This was the Ka, a kind of guardian-spirit or genius, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was horn with the individual and accompanied him through life as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a 'double'. The Ka did not expire with its protege" but continued{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to live in order to protect the deceased against enemies in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}future world.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}List of the Chief Egyptian Deities and Sacred Animals.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ammon or Amon (Fig. 1), the god of Thebes, was made a sun-god{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the name Amon-Re and became the national god under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Middle Empire. For his persecution by Amenophis IV., see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 203. His sacred animal was the ram.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amset, one of the four guardian-deities of the dead, who protected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them from hunger and thirst, and to whom therefore the viscera{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the deceased were dedicated. The other three gods were Hapi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Twe-metf, and Kebh-snewf.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antjbus or Antaios, the Greek name for a peculiar Egyptian god,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worshipped at Antaeopolis (p. 215).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antjbis (Fig. 2), a god of the dead, whose function was connected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the interment. A later myth makes him a brother of Osiris.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The jackal was sacred to him.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Anuket (Greek Anukis), goddess of the district of the cataracts.{% endraw %}
