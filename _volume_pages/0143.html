{% raw %}VIII. Historical Notice of Egyptian Art,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By Professor G. Steindorff.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I. Architecture.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In Egypt, as elsewhere, the Pier and the Column are the most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}important of all architectural members. Their absence indicates a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}very elementary stage in the art of building, when artistic devel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opment has yet to begin. Their presence breaks the outline of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}different masses of the edifice and affords strength and support.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The simplest form of the weight-bearing member is the square{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pier, and this is common even in the tombs of the Ancient Empire.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}occupied by reliefs or inscriptions and their |{% endraw %}

{% raw %}ossal figures of Osiris (Fig. I) in the Rames- j{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seum (p. 285) and at Medinet Habu (p. 300).{% endraw %}

{% raw %}octagonal pillar by bevelling off the corners,{% endraw %}

{% raw %}at the top so as to blend with the roof; at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foot was a round, cushion-like base. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}next step was to convert, by a similar process,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the octagonal pillar into one with sixteen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sides, and in some cases the flat surfaces were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grooved or fluted, a sharp edge being left be¬{% endraw %}
