{% raw %}However different from each other the Temples of the New{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empibe appear at first sight, there is but little difficulty in refer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ring them all to two general fundamental forms. One of these,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vividly, hut probably quite accidentally, recalling the Greek Perip-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teros or temple surrounded by a colonnade, occurs only during the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}18th Dyn., the age of Thutmosis III. and his successors. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rectangular Cella (or Sanctuary), containing the sacred boat with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the image ofthe god and provided with doors at each end, rose upon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a basement of masonry, crowned with a concave cornice and ap¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proached by a flight of steps. On all four sides it was surrounded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a colonnade, the roof of which rested upon square pillars and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns (usually Proto-Doric). Occasionally, as (e.g.) at Medinet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Habu, this main structure was adjoined at the back by several smaller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apartments, also used for religious rites. Curiously enough this form{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of peripteros was revived in the Ptolemaic period, though with various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}modifications, being used in the so-called Birth Houses, which stood{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beside the principal temples and were dedicated to the worship of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the maternal deity (Isis or Hathor) and her child. The inner sanc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tuaries in these birth-houses also were surrounded with colonnades,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the roofs of which, however, were borne by remarkable plant-columns,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crowned with heads of Hathor or with figures of Bes.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}simply and clearly illustrated in the small temple built by Ram¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}of the great temple of Ammon at Karnak, p. 245). The approach{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the temple is formed by the Pylon, two large towers of masonry{% endraw %}
{% raw %}flanking the entrance-door. These towers are shaped like very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}steep truncated pyramids; the slightly inclining walls are framed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with round mouldings and offer the greatest available space for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs. The towers were imposing from their sheer size, and this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impression was heightened (from the Middle Empire onwards) by the{% endraw %}

{% raw %}flag-staffs which were fastened at the foot in the masonry of the{% endraw %}
