{% raw %}HISTORY OF ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemies that attention was once more directed to the S. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monarchs raised many large temples to the gods of the country{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually on the site of earlier ruined buildings. All these temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are built on one uniform plan, differing but slightly from the form{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prevalent under the New Empire (comp. the plan of the temple at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Edfu with that of the Ramesseum). There is a difference in only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two essential points. The colonnade (or terrace) at the back of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colonnaded court of the earlier temples has developed into a larger{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vestibule or Great Hypostyle Hall, supported by columns, of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an example had already occurred in the temple of Luxor. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns of the front row are united with each other by means of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Screen Walls or Balustrades (over 6 ft. in height) which separate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the court from this Vestibule Hall, called by the Greeks Pronaos. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Furthermore the Sanctuary, which formerly had a door at each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}end, is now closed at the back and retains only the front entrance.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Between the vestibule and the sanctuary a lesser hypostyle hall and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two smaller halls are placed, an arrangement, however, which also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occurs under the New Empire. The side-rooms are also numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at this period and among these special mention must be made of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}small Sacripcial Court situated on the right side (see plan of Edfu,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 314) and an elegant Kiosk adjoining it (ib.), which do not occur{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in any of the older temples f.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From a very early period all flat surfaces on pylons, interior{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls, column-shafts, and ceilings were covered with represen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tations and inscriptions. The external walls, the pylons, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls of the courts, i.e. those parts of the temple that were ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posed to the vulgar eye, commemorated the exploits of the king,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}campaigns, great festivals, or other important events of his reign;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the representations were intended to keep the power and nobility{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Pharaoh constantly before his people. On the other hand the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representations in the interior of the temple were exclusively{% endraw %}
{% raw %}devoted to the religious proceedings that took place there. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king, who theoretically was the only mortal who might have inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course with the gods, appears again and again, offering gifts and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}homage to the deities and receiving from them earthly blessings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the late period and especially under the Ptolemies the secular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representations on the external walls and the walls of the court gave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place to religious scenes. The variegated battle-scenes of the New{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire no longer appear on the pylons, but the typical figure of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pharaoh smiting his enemies in presence of the god; and on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}external walls the battle-scenes and triumphs of the ruler give place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to sacrificial and other sacred scenes depicted at tedious length. —{% endraw %}

{% raw %}of the Egyptian a type in small of the world. The roof corresponded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}t Unless, indeed, the open court at Der el-hahri corresponds to this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}court in the Ptolemaic temples.{% endraw %}
