{% raw %}It is difficult for the ordinary student to obtain a proper ap¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preciation of Egyptian Beliefs, owing to the peculiar style of draw¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing, which arose in prehistoric times and which was religiously{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adhered to as a kind of sacred tradition ever afterwards, with but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few exceptions. The principle adopted was that of representing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each part of the body in the clearest and most faithful manner,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ignoring, however, the necessity of combining them harmoniously{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as they appear to the eye in a complete figure. Thus we constantly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}see reliefs with the faces in profile, but with the eyes drawn from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the front; or the shoulders are shown nearly facing us and the feet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and legs in profile ; or, still more peculiarly, the back of the trunk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is drawn in full breadth and the front in profile. These peculiar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ities recur in the works of other Oriental nations and even in those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Greeks of the early period, who, however, soon overcame the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}defect. Another rule of Egyptian composition forbade the inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}section of the figure by an outstretched arm or similar line; thus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the case of a figure walking or stretching the hand to any ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ject it is invariably the foot or hand farthest from the spectator{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that is extended. Ignorance of perspective and foreshortening is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also apparent, all the more on account of the evident effort to re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present every separate object clearly and completely. Thus persons,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}animals, etc., supposed to be behind others are depicted in rows above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them, and objects intended to be lying upon tables are depicted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Standing above the tables. This primitive method was carried so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}far that, in the representations of vessels with interior decoration,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the interior decoration is placed above the vessels, without any at¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tention to perspective. The principal personages in a representation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are indicated by the primitive distinction of being delineated on a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}much larger scale than the other figures.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The art of drawing in Egypt was hampered from time immemor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ial by a number of designs that were mechanically copied again{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and again and that no one ventured to alter. Even the accompany¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing inscriptions were held sacred from all changes, although they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were composed in an old-fashioned language that was almost a dead{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tongue in the later periods. Among the subjects thus stereotyped{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were scenes relating to the intercourse of the king with the gods (in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prayers or sacrifices, etc.), those representing the king smiting his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enemies, the deceased receiving offerings from his different estates,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the slaughtering of cattle for sacrifice, and many others. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pictures of these subjects, therefore, we find the most rigid adherence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the above-mentioned rules. But beyond these the Egyptian artists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had a considerable field for the exercise of their invention, and they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}often displayed great brilliancy of imagination and observation.{% endraw %}
