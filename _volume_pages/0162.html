{% raw %}IX. Greek (Alexandrian) Art in Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By ^Professor Theodor Schreiber of Leipsic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Early Egyptian Art did not die out with the Pharaohs; both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the Ptolemies and, later still, under the Roman emperors,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temples in the old style were raised, restored, or enlarged, and were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned as before with statues, frescoes, and obelisks, while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worship of the old national gods was continued in them unchanged.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}But the new rulers and the Greeks who immigrated with them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}established Hellenic customs, art, and science in Egypt, as they had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been established in the other kingdoms of Alexander's successors.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexandria, the mighty creation of Alexander the Great, destined to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be the centre of his empire, became, as the residence of his success¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ors, the Ptolemies, who distinguished themselves as patrons of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}learning, the central point of the intellectual interests of the Hel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lenic world, though Athens, of course, still retained some of her old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supremacy, and Pergamum at a later date also entered the lists.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Museum of Alexandria investigations concerned with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}direct observation of men and things, were carried on side by side{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the literary and historical studies of scholars. Poets and artists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gathered in crowds at the brilliant court; and the royal passion for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}building undertook tasks undreamt of by earlier Greek art, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, indeed, with its limited resources, that art could never have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}achieved. East and West met in Egypt, and the most diverse peoples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Greeks, Egyptians, Jews, Negroes from the interior of Africa, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— mingled there in the closest association, in which the Greeks,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}though the dominant element, adapted themselves to many of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}native religious and social customs, and learned to keep their eyes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open to the good qualities of their neighbours. Thus there arose in{% endraw %}
