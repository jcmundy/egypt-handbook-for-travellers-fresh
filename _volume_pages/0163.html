{% raw %}The great buildings of the Greek period in Egypt have gradually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}disappeared almost without leaving a trace, though some lingered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}until last century, for the simple reason that most of them were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built of marble which could be used conveniently as material for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other buildings or for lime-burning. The majestic Alexandrian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Serapeum with its forest of pillars has disappeared, leaving as its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only relics the so-called 'Pompey's Pillar' which originally stood in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle of the temple-court, the recently excavated foundations,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a number of isolated blocks of stone scattered throughout the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city. Yet in size and conception this building was one of the greatest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}creations of ancient Architecture, and the first achievement of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new style originated by Deinocrates, the gifted architect of Alexander{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Great, which exhibited a combination of Oriental and ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek forms. The Serapeum, the common national sanctuary of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greeks and Egyptians, was elevated high above the city by means of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}huge substructure cut out of the rock. The platform on the top, reached{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the late-Roman period by a staircase of a hundred steps, formed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a spacious temple-court, which was surrounded with colonnades and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned at the entrance with propylaea and in the centre with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above-mentioned huge column, which was probably intended to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have a companion. The temple, with the colossal statue of Serapis,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupied the background. This colossus and the temple itself were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}experiments in the new style of incrustation just introduced from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the East. The statue of Serapis is said to have included six different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}metals, besides all kinds of gems, but the exact method of its con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}struction is still uncertain. It was apparently, however, related to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ihe new style of wall-decoration, which can be minutely traced in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its prototypes, beginning, and progress. The walls of Babylonian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Persian royal palaces were built of sun-dried bricks and then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}laced with slabs of alabaster, glazed tiles, or even with gilded metal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plates; in like manner the brick walls in the buildings of the Ptolemies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were covered — first of all, it is said, in the Serapeum — with slabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of marble, glass, or metal, and were adorned with friezes, reliefs, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaics. So late as the end of the 16th cent, the Italian traveller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Filippo Pigafetta (1533-1604) saw in Alexandria fragments of wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decoration 'of wonderful work', in houses that had survived from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}antiquity and were still inhabited; and even in the present day the{% endraw %}
