{% raw %}Not much more than the names have come down to us of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number of large buildings of the Ptolemies, each of which doubtless{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represented an architectural advance. Such were the Soma, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mausoleum of Alexander the Great, the harbour-works (Heptastadion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Diabaihra) which united the city and the island of Pharos, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Alexandrian Temple of Pan, which rose in the form of a hill.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The last relics of the Telesterium, a temple built by Ptolemy II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}outside the E. gate of the city for the celebration of the Eleusinian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mysteries, were destroyed about the middle of the nineteenth cen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tury, before scholars had found time to examine them carefully. All{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that remains of the Greek Serapeum, in the necropolis of Sakkara,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now lies buried again in the sands of the desert, except the sculp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures brought to light in the first excavation, some of which aie now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left to disintegrate in the open air (comp. pp. 135 et seq.). Suffi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient attention has not yet been paid to the position in architectural{% endraw %}
{% raw %}history of the technically unique Aqueduct of Alexandria, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was coaeyal with the foundation of the city, and stretched its sub¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terranean canals along all the principal streets, supplying the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cisterns which were found in every house and some of which are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still in existence. Of the palaces and villas of the Ptolemies, of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the celebrated lighthouse on the island of Pharos, of the Gymnasium{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated on the Dromos or chief street, and of a few other buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we learn from ancient authors enough at least to justify us in sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mising that they served as models to the Roman architects for sim¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ilar edifices. The building of Alexandria was the first example on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a grand scale of the systematic laying out of a city on a homo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}geneous plan, with its regular network of streets cutting each other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at right angles and bordered with colonnades, its long vistas, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its symmetrically disposed public buildings; and it was imitated in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}innumerable subsequent foundations. The latest of these was Antinou-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}polis (p. 201), founded by Hadrian on the Nile, the lingering rem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nants of which were not finally destroyed until last century. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rue de la Porte de Rosette in modern Alexandria marks the line of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient main street, which united the E. and W. gates of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fortifications; and numerous remains of the double colonnade with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which it once was bordered are seen in the fragments of columns in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the neighbouring houses and gardens.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}accompanied by a corresponding activity in the domain of Sculpture.{% endraw %}
