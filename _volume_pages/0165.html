{% raw %}Alexandrian sculpture was wonderfully prolific in genre composi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions and decorative carvings. The extant sculptures may be easily{% endraw %}
{% raw %}divided into three classes according to their style. The first class{% endraw %}
{% raw %}comprises the works of native artists affected by Greek influence,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who adhere in general to the traditional canon, but display a purer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taste in the treatment of the face and in the attitude and modelling{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the figure. Thus the colossal statue of a Macedonian king (said{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be Alexander II.), which was found in the temple-precincts at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Karnak and is now in the Museum of Gizeh, is executed, as regards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its general arrangement, in conformity with the ancient Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scheme of statues, while its face and hair are treated in a freer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manner. Two other specimens of this art are to be seen in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vestibule to the innermost tomb-chamber in the great tomb at Kom{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esh-Shukafa (p. 12); another is the colossal head of a Ptolemy,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}treated in the Egyptian style (K. in Room V of the Museum at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexandria). The Egyptian sculptor of the portrait-statue of a native{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scribe found at Alexandria (No. 294 in the Gizeh Museum, p. 90){% endraw %}
{% raw %}has gone still further. Not only has he abandoned the crouching{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posture traditional for such subjects, but he has also imitated as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely as possible the Greek style in the attitude, the shape of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}head, and the folds of the drapery; though at the same time the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stiffness of the position, and still more the pillar at the back with its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hieroglyphic inscription, clearly announce the origin of the artist.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A smaller group of statues and reliefs are apparently the works of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek immigrants, who brought with them, and practised unchanged,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an art acquired abroad, and mainly inspired by Athenian ideals.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Examples of this Alexandrian 'Ideal Style' are to be seen in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}splendid Head of a Gaul in the Gizeh Museum (Room XL, No. 291),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which deviates considerably in style from the well-known realistic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gauls' heads of the Pergamenian school; a sepulchral Stele in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same Museum (Room XL, No. 289); the fine seated Statue of Hercu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}les in the vestibule of the Alexandrian Museum; the Head of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hercules (Case J, Room XVI), and numerous other specimens in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the same museum. A curious weakening of this ideal style, due to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the new material, appears in sculptures carved in limestone quarried{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Meks near Alexandria or in the shell-limestone of the Mokattam{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills near Cairo. The most interesting specimen of this is a group{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a mourning woman and child in the Alexandria Museum (0;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Room IV). The most important achievement of this school was the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above-mentioned colossus of Serapis in the Serapeum at Alexandria,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the work of a sculptor named Bryaxis, who seems to have been an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}immigrant from Caria; and this may still be reconstructed in its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}details with the help of numerous larger or smaller copies. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colossus represented the mysterious deity sitting on a throne, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cerberus by his side, and crowned with the corn-measure, the em¬{% endraw %}
