{% raw %}Talent for decorative art is apparent even in these creations. But{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it achieves higher things in the mural frescoes and in the reliefs, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most developed form of which, the pictorial relief with landscape{% endraw %}
{% raw %}background, also took its rise under the influence of Ptolemaic art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Numerous mural paintings in the Greek style have recently come to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}light in the ancient cemeteries of Alexandria, and many of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are, significantly, found beside or beneath frescoes in the revived{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian style. When the graves were used a second time the Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings were covered with whitewash and the Egyptian frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were painted on the top. Examples of both styles may be seen in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}museum at Alexandria (Room XI, No. 8). The technical delicacy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and freshness of conception which was still characteristic of Alex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}andrian painting at the imperial epoch are apparent in the Portraits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attached to the heads of mummies, painted on thin wooden tablets,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in tempera, or in encaustic colours, or in a style uniting both these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}methods. The practice of furnishing the mummy-covering with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portrait reproducing the features of the deceased dates from early{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian times. In the Hellenistic period masks modelled in stucco{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and coloured, or paintings upon wood were chosen for this purpose,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in order that the features of the deceased might be portrayed as those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a living person. In most cases in fact, even in portraits of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rudest make, the lifelike expression is surprising. They look as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}though they were executed from the living model, so that the theory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that they were posthumous portraits is not very probable. At the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hack of some of these portraits a layer of mortar, about an inch in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thickness, has been found, and in others there are holes, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seem to have been meant for nails to fasten the picture to the wall;{% endraw %}

{% raw %}kind used to be painted as ornaments for rooms during the lifetime{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the persons represented, and that they were removed from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall and placed on the mummy-covering after death, except when{% endraw %}
