{% raw %}The most prominent characteristic peculiarities of Arabian archi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tecture are the following : —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1. The introduction of the pointed arch as the dominating{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aesthetic characteristic (Mosque of Ibn Tulun) and the employment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Byzantine stilted round arch, as well as of the round and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pointed horseshoe arch, the scalloped arch, the clover-leaf arch, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 'keel' arch. These (with the exception of the scolloped and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clover - leaf arches) were accompanied by corresponding forms of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}domes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2. The development of the form of tower known as the minaret.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}3. The refining of various forms of pinnacles that occur also in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early Egypt, Assyria, Phoenicia, and Persia.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4. The employment in facades of two colours, by alternate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}courses of red and white limestone or (in later examples) of black{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and white marble.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5. The invention of the elegant wooden balconies and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}system of closing window - openings with wooden gratings (mush-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rehiyehs) or with plaster or stone gratings in Arabian patterns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(kamariyehs).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6. The development of surface ornaments into geometrical{% endraw %}
{% raw %}patterns of every kind (entrelacs) or conventionalized foliage (arab¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esques strictly so-called); the use of Arabic ornamental inscriptions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on friezes and medallions; and the treatment of wall-surfaces and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceilings in rich polychrome hues, whether by painting, incrustation,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or mosaic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The chief monuments of Arabian architecture in Egypt are the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious edifices (mosques) and the tombs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The period within which these were built extends from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accession of the Tulunide sultans to the conquest of Egypt by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Turks. The earlier mosques have disappeared, leaving hardly a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trace behind, and our knowledge of them depends upon the ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}viously exaggerated and often confused descriptions of the Arabic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}writers. The later mosques are of little artistic value. Some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them display a union of Turkish-Arabic architectural forms with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian-Arabic ornamentation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The only existing building dating from the Tulunide Pebiod{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the mosque of Ahmed Ibn Tulun (p. 53). The oldest plaster de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corations in this mosque display a system of ornamentation, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}various elements in which remain, as in the antique, separate and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distinct though some of them are so unusual in form as to defy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}classification under any known style.{% endraw %}
