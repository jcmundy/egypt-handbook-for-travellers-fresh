{% raw %}corresponding to the atrium of a Byzantine basilica, lie four flat-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roofed colonnades (liwan), used for prayers. The Chief Liwan or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sanctuary, placed on the side next Mecca, has usually four or five{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aisles, the others never more than two. The Ceucifoem Mosque,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a new form invented in Persia, was introduced into Egypt about the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}end of the 12th cent, by the Eyyubide Saladin. This was developed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the previous sample form by the construction of additional{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chambers at the four corners of the liwans, in such a way that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liwans, now covered with massive waggon-vaults, formed the four{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arms of a cross. Comp. the plan of the mosque of Sultan Hasan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 50). These liwans were used as school-rooms, whence arose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the name Medreseh, or 'school-mosque'. — Towards the close of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Mameluke supremacy still another form arose, used, however,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only for small mosques. The side-liwans were shortened and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}central court so contracted that it could be roofed over and lighted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the top. The four arms of the cross were covered with flat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roofs, like the colonnades in the original form of mosque, while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}waggon-vaulting was represented merely by a transverse rib on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side next the court (comp. plan of the mosque of Kait-Bey, p. 67).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With the conquest of Egypt by the Turks under Selim I., the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Turkish-Byzantine style of architecture also made its appearance in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that country. The four liwans were superseded by a single sanc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tuary, consisting of a main building covered with domes and usually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preceded by a second court.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The smaller prayer-rooms, frequently added to private houses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and not unlike the Christian chapels, were known as Zdwiyeh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now turn to inspect the Internal Equipment of the mosque.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The centre of the court was originally occupied by a fountain, be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}neath a canopy supported upon columns. This was intended for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ornament only, for the prescribed ablutions were performed at a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}special basin (Meda) in an adjoining court. Under Turkish rule{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the fountain was frequently replaced by an apparatus provided{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with taps and known as the Hanefiyeh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The sanctuary contains the Kibla or Mihrdb, the prayer-niche{% endraw %}
{% raw %}turned towards Mecca. Here also we observe: (1) the Mimbar, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pulpit, to the right of the Kibla, usually embellished with orna¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mental panels and incrustation; (2) the Kursi (pi. Kerdsi), the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seat of the Imam, together with a desk, on which the Koran (which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is kept at other times in a cabinet of its own) lies open during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}divine service; (3) the Dikkeh, a podium borne by columns, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded by a low railing, from which the Moballain (assistants{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Khatib) repeat the words of the Koran, which is read at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kibla, for the benefit of the people at a distance; (4) the various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lamps and lanterns (Tanur, large chandelier; Thoraiya, lit. 'seven{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stars', small chandelier; Fdnus, lamp; Kandil, small oil-lamp).{% endraw %}
