{% raw %}Since the days of the Bahrite Mameluke sultans (1250 A.D.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}every mosque has possessed a Sebll, orpublic fountain, exceptin cases{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when a separate building is erected for this. The sebils are rooms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with bronze railings at which passers-by may obtain water, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supplied from cisterns placed beneath. The upper story of the sebil{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a kind of loggia, supported by columns and covered with a tent-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roof, frequently in elegant timber-architecture. This is the Maktab,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or elementary school. The detached columns that frequently em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bellish the exterior of these buildings differentiate them from all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the other parts of the mosque-facade, in which columns appear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only built into the angles or immured in the masonry.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tombs. — The tombs of sultans and emirs and of their families{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are invariably built in connection with mosques (p. clxii). On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other hand the Sh?kh Tombs, or tombs of saints, which are found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in all parts ofthe country, are independent structures, usually built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the spot on which the revered deceased ended his days. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely resemble the mortuary chapels of the mosques and are, like{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them, covered with domes. The ordinary tombs of the Muslims are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally situated on high ground, beyond the influence of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}moisture of the river, and preferably in the desert. The subterranean{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vaulted chambers are generally large enough for four or more bodies,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and are destitute of decoration. The corpse, wrapped in white{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cotton cloth, is placed upon a bed of sand, with the face turned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards Mecca. When both sexes aTe interred in the same vault a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}partition-wall is erected to separate them. Above the vault stands a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cenotaph (Tarklbeh) resting on a more or less decorated pedestal,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and bearing two upright columns of marble or other stone, one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, placed immediately over the head of the deceased, bears his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name and age, with texts from the Koran. At the top is represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the turban of the deceased, the form of which indicates his rank.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Over the cenotaphs of persons of distinction are frequently erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}canopies, resting on four columns or pilasters. Wealthy families{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded the tombs of their ancestors with extensive buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(known as H6sh), including rooms for mourners, sebil, school, stables,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custodian's residence, etc. The tombs of the Khalifs and Mamelukes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Cairo include many erections of this kind, which lend the cem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eteries the appearance of small half-deserted towns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The secular buildings are on the whole less interesting. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fortifications of the citadel of Cairo, dating from the time of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Saladin, recall the mediaeval castles of Europe. Some of the nu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merous gates in the walls of Cairo date from the Fatimite period;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they were probably built after Roman models and are distinguished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the skill with which they aTe constructed, especially for accuracy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the jointing of the stones.{% endraw %}
