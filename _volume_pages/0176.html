{% raw %}clxvi BUILDINGS OF THE MOHAMMEDANS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ordinary streets of Oriental towns are very narrow, so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that no very satisfactory view is to be had of the facades and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grated balconies of the houses. The groundfloor is built of solid{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masonry and its rooms are frequently vaulted. The upper stories{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually overhang and are supported, together with their balconies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or oriel-windows, by stone consoles of peculiar construction. An{% endraw %}
{% raw %}agreeable and effective contrast to the broad, flat surfaces of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house-front is offered by the elegantly shaped oriel-windows and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Mushrebtyeh, or wooden balcony-gratings, carved to resemble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interlaced strings of beads. The deep door-recesses (like those of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mosques) also serve to break the level uniformity ofthe facades.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The massive wooden doors are strengthened with iron bands or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(less frequently) studded with nails arranged in intricate inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}laced patterns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The entrance-passage (Dirkeh) admits to the Hdsh or court{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. I, 4), which corresponds to the atrium of Roman houses and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has no columns around it. Off this open the rooms of the Salamlik,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Mandara, Takhta Bosh, and Mak'ad. At the back is the Bab{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Hartm(P].I, 12), or door to the staircase to the upper floor, before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which hangs a brightly coloured curtain. The staircase is usually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}narrow and without ornament, though sometimes the ceiling and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}string-hoards are embellished with a black and white mosaic pattern.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the top is the vestibule of the Kd'a (p. clxv), the drawing-room{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the harim. The Ka'a is usually a long and narrow room with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lofty ceiling, and, strictly speaking, consists of three connected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portions, differentiated in shape and height of ceiling. The square{% endraw %}
{% raw %}central portion, known as the Durka'a, lies one step lower than the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Liwans on each side. These liwans are not always on the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}level; the broader one is regarded as the place of honour by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceremonious Orientals. The ceiling of the durka'a, always loftier{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than those of the liwans, is provided with a cupola or lantern, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coloured-glass windows of the kind known as Kamarlyehs. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kamariyehs are plaster-disks, about H/4 in thickness, perforated,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while still soft, with patterns representing vases of flowers, houses,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}geometrical figures, writing characters, etc., the openings being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}afterwards filled in with coloured glass. Owing to the above-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned difference in the height of the ceilings, two of the walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the durka'a rest upon supports which are based upon massive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brackets reaching far down on the main side-walls. This arrange¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment results in a curious kind of flat arch, against which some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the beams of the liwan-ceiling lean. The durka'a is paved with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coloured marbles and frequently has a fountain in the centre. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liwans are paved with ordinary stone slabs, which are concealed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rugs or carpets. On one wall of the durka'a there is always a Suffa,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a kind of stand on which are placed the dishes used in entertaining{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guests. The walls of the liwans are panelled to the height of 6 or{% endraw %}
