{% raw %}The Public Baths, usually of quite unpretending exterior, are{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The Okellas' (p. 38) were important edifices when the caravan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trade, especially the caravan-trade with the Red Sea, flourished.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Their often extensive facades exhibit peculiar carvings. The portals{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resemble those of the mosques, and the locks and fastenings of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}outer shops are sometimes carved. The central hypaethral court ac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}commodated the caravan, the goods brought by which were deposited{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in vaulted chambers on the groundfloor, while the rooms in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}upper stories, opening off galleries, were used as lodgings by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merchants. The centre of the court was probably invariably occu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pied by a Mosalla.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}When we come to analyse the impressions produced by a study{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Arabic buildings in Egypt, we find that our admiration of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}harmonious and tasteful ornamentation, unsurpassed by any school{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of architecture, is counterbalanced by a certain feeling of aesthetic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dissatisfaction, prompted by the numerous incongruities arising{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from unsystematic and unskilful treatment of architectonic details.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Part of this dissatisfaction is doubtless to be placed to the account{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the dilapidated condition of most of the buildings. But the real{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reason why Arabian art failed to reach a high level in technical{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ability as well as in ornamentation must be looked for in the early{% endraw %}
{% raw %}collapse of the great empire of the Khalifs ; in the uncertain and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vacillating political circumstances of the period that followed ; in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}climatic and geological conditions; in the influence of superstition;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in the characteristic Oriental tendency to adhere with obstinate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fidelity to ancient forms and to leave unaltered anything that has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}once been accomplished. However much admiration the arabesque{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may excite, however great an influence it may exert on industrial{% endraw %}
{% raw %}art, we still miss in it the reproduction of living beings, the con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}templation of which invites, as it were, an intelligent and active{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sympathy.{% endraw %}
