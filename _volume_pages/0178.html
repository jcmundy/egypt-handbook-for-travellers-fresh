{% raw %}ARABIC LANGUAGE.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the period of the Tulunides, when Persian influence made{% endraw %}
{% raw %}itself felt even in the religious conceptions of Egypt, portraits were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painted and coloured wooden statues erected in the palaces, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there was even a factory for figures of animals in Cairo. But no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}long period elapsed before the prohibition contained in the Sunna{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against the representation of any living being again came into force.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Representations of this kind are therefore very rare, and are now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be found preserved only in the low reliefs carved by Persian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sculptors of the Shi'ite sect. Statues and paintings have disappeared{% endraw %}
{% raw %}without leaving a trace. Painting and sculpture in modern Egyp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tian art have been reserved exclusively for the decoration of wall-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surfaces.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}XI. The Arabic Language.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rewritten by Prof. Hans Stumme.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Teansliteeation of Arabic vocal sounds, so intensely different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from our own, in the ordinary Latin alphabet is rendered additionally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}difficult by the varied international relations of Egypt. In maps and plans,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in railway time-tables, and in other publications we find the transliteration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}differing widely according as the French or the English view has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adopted. In this Handbook we have transliterated the consonantal sounds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so far as possible according to English usage (e.g., sh instead of the French{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ch), hut have deemed it advisable to follow the Italian or Continental{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sounds of the vowels (ah, eh, ee, o, oo). Thus: emir, which is pronounced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'aymeer ; shekh (or sheikh), pronounced 'shake' (with a guttural k); tulul,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pronounced 'toolooP; Abustr, pronounced 'Abooseer', etc. — The I ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}article is frequently miassimilated; e.g. el-rds instead of er-rds (comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}note t on p. clxxii).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arabic belongs to the Semitic group of languages, and has no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relationship with the tongues of Europe. A knowledge of Hebrew,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however , will materially facilitate the learning of Arabic. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}golden era of Arabic literature is coeval with the introduction of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}El-Islam, and the Koran in the dialect of the Kureish (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}family of Mohammed) is still regarded as an unrivalled model of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style and language. But by the side of this literary Arabic flourished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also various colloquial dialects, which were carried by the Arabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into the various provinces conquered for the Crescent, and there{% endraw %}
{% raw %}developed partly under the influence of the old local tongues. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this way arose the vulgar dialects of Arabic, of which that spoken{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Egypt is one. In writing, however, an attempt was made to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}retain the older forms, and the written language of the present day,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}known as Middle Arabic, occupies a position midway between the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}original classical tongue and the popular dialects.{% endraw %}
