{% raw %}A little farther to the S. we pass a large Arabian cemetery, lying{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the right, and soon reach an eminence covered with rubbish and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fragments of ruins, on which rises *Pompey's Pillar (PL F, G, 7).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The monument is composed of red granite from Assuan, and it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now the only important relic of antiquity in the city. The found¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ations, composed of several blocks (one with the name and figure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Sethos I., p. Ixxxii) which once belonged to other buildings, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}much damaged. The height of the column, together with the rectan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gular pedestal and the Corinthian capital, is 89 ft. ; the shaft is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}69 ft. high, and is about 9 ft. in diameter fhelow, and not quite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}8 ft. at the top. The pillar was raised by the Egyptian prefect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Maximus as a landmark for sailors, and afterwards bore a statue of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Emp. Diocletian erected in 302 A.D. by a Roman prefect named{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Posidius. The present name of the pillar is due to the mediaeval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belief that it marked the tomb of Pompey the Great.{% endraw %}
