{% raw %}The **Street Scenes presented by the city of the Khalifs afford{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an inexhaustible fund of amusement and delight, admirably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}illustrating the whole world of Oriental fiction, and producing an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indelible impression on the uninitiated denizen of the West. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Oriental life seems to feel the atmosphere of the newer quarters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}uncongenial, and it must therefore be sought for in the old Arabian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarters, which are still mostly inaccessible for carriages, in spite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the many new streets that have been constructed in Cairo of late{% endraw %}
{% raw %}years. Most of the streets in the old part of the town are still un-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paved, and they are too often excessively dirty. Many of them are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so narrow that there is hardly room for two riders to pass, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}projecting balconies of the harems with their gratings often nearly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}meet. The busy traffic in these streets presents an 'interminable,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ravelled, and twisted string of men, women, and animals, of walk¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ers, riders, and carts of every description. Add to this the cracking{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the drivers' whips, the jingling of money at the table of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}changers established at every corner of the street, the rattling of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the brazen vessels of the water-carriers, the moaning of the camels,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}braying of donkeys, and barking of dogs, and you have a perfect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pandemonium'. It is not, however, until the traveller has learned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to distinguish the various individuals who throng the streets, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}knows their different pursuits, that he can thoroughly appreciate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his walks or rides. We may therefore give a brief description of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some of the leading characteristics of the different members of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}community.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From a very early period it has been customary for the Arabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to distinguish their different sects, families, and dynasties by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colour of their Turbans. And the custom still prevails to a certain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extent. The 'Sherifs', or descendants of the prophet, now wear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}white turbans, though originally they wore green, the colour of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prophet. Green turbans are now frequently worn by the Mecca{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pilgrims. The 'Ulama, or clergy and scholars, usually wear a very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wide, evenly folded turban of light colour. The orthodox length of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a believer's turban is seven times that of his head, being equivalent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the whole length of his body, in order that the turban may after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards be used as the wearer's winding sheet, and that this cir¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cumstance may familiarise him with the thought of death. The{% endraw %}
