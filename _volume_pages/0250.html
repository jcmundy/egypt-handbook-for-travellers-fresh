{% raw %}These schools are maintained by the private enterprise of the school¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masters themselves, who exact 1-2 piastres per week from each pupil. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mere reading and recitation of verses from the Koran being in itself con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sidered a meritorious act, the great object of these schools is to teach the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pupils to recite the Koran hy heart. After learning the alphabet, the pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nunciation and the values of numbers, the pupil is taught the ninety-nine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'beautiful' names of Allah, a knowledge of which is necessary to enable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him to repeat the ninety-nine prayers of the Mohammedan rosary (sebha).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The boy is then made to write out the Fdtha, or first chapter (sureh){% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Koran, which he reads often enough" to impress it perfectly on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his memory, swaying his body to and fro the while, whereby, as he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imagines, his memory is rendered more pliant. After learning the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chapter, he next proceeds to learn the last, the last but one, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}others in the same inverted order, until he reaches the second, the rea¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}son being that the chapters gradually diminish in length from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}second to the last. Although the language is often difficult and obscure,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no explanations are given, so that the boy who knows the whole book{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by heart usually understands hut little of it. As soon as the boy has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}learned the whole of the Koran in this way, the completion of his studies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is commemorated by the celebration of the Khatmeh, a family festival, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which the schoolmaster is invited.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Bazaars f of Cairo, though inferior to those of Damascus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Constantinople, present to the European traveller many novel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}features and many interesting traits of Oriental character. As is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the universal custom in the East, shops of the same kind, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their workshops, are congregated together in the same quarter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named sometimes after a mosque, but more usually after the wares{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there sold, e.g. Suk en-Nahhdsln, bazaar of the coppersmiths, Suk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Khordaglyeh, bazaar of the ironmongers. Most of the bazaars con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sist of narrow, and often dirty, lanes, generally covered over with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an awning to shade them from the sun, and flanked with shops about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6 ft. wide. These shops (dukkan) are open towards the street, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in front of each is a masfaba or seat on which the customer takes his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place and on which the shopkeeper offers his prayers at the appointed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hours. The inscriptions above and in the shop do not consist ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dealer's name, but of various pious ejaculations, such as 'Oh Allah,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}locker ofthe gate of gain, oh, all-nourisher', 'the help of Allah and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a speedy victory', etc. These lanes usually enclose a massive store¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house of considerable size (khan), consisting of two stories. Several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these khans form a quarter of the city (hdra). These were for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merly closed at night by massive, iron-mounted gates, still in some{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cases preserved, though no longer used.{% endraw %}
