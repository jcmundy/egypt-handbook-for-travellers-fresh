{% raw %}On leaving the mosque we turn into the Mashdad el-Heseni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. E, 3), cross the Sikkeh el-Gedideh (p. 42) obliquely, and enter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Shabi'a el-Halwagi (PL E, 3), which is mainly occupied by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}score or more stalls of the Booksellers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the booksellers are also scholars, and their shops are the resort{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the learned world of Cairo. As the prices of books vary greatly in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accordance with the demand and other circumstances and there is no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}such thing as a fixed publishing price, purchasers should always endea¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vour to ascertain beforehand the true value of any work they wish to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buy. As in the case of many other wares, the line between new and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}second-hand books is not so strictly drawn in the East as in Europe.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The booksellers generally keep catalogues, several feet in length, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}refresh their memories regarding the state of their stock. The Koran,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is shown very reluctantly to non-Muslims, is kept separate' from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the other books. The books are not arranged side by side as in Eu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ropean shops, but piled up in a very inconvenient fashion. Many of them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are sold in loose sheets, in which case the purchaser should see that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the work is complete, as gaps are of frequent occurrence. The bindings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}usually consist of leather or pasteboard. Valuable books are often kept{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in cases of red sheepskin, out of which they are drawn by means of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}loop. — The workmanship of the bookbinders, who, like other Oriental{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artizans, work in the open street, is far inferior to European productions.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Red is their favourite colour.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The *Gami'a el-Azhar (PL E, 3, 4), the 'blooming', the most im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant monument of the Fatimite period, was completed in 973 A.D.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Gohar, the vizier of the Fatimite Sultan Mu'izz, and was converted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into a University in 988 by Khalif el-'Aziz (p. xciv). The rectangular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ground-plan of the original building is easily recognizable, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it has been so frequently restored that no part of it can be said{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to date actually from the Fatimite period except the central part{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the sanctuary, with its cupolas. Everything outside this rectangle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is known positively to be of later date. The characteristic old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ornamentation of the arcades and cupolas deserves special attention;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of the walls has been for the most part renewed after vanished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}patterns. The arcades of the court (sahn) were rebuilt under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khedive Tewfik with scrupulous reproduction ofthe old style and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the retention of the old columns. — The successive rulers of Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have emulated each other in maintaining and enlargingthis venerable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}huilding. In the 18th cent, the wealthy 'Abd er-Rahman added four{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aisles to the sanctury, and in more recent days Sa'id Pasha and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khedives Tewfik and 'Abbas II. have been notable benefactors of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mosque. 'Abbas II. erected a new building in place of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dilapidated N.W. side of the mosque, and his neo-Arab facade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is practically the only one the mosque boasts, the other sides being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}all quite unpretentious and concealed in narrow lanes.{% endraw %}
