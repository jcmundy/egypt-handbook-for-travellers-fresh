{% raw %}Immediately adjoining the mosque is the town-gate Bab ez-Zu-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weleh (PL E, 4), at the end of the street. This is built of solid{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blocks of stone and resembles the two other gates of the Fatimite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period, the Bab el-Futuh and the Bab-en-Nasr (p. 62). It was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected at the end of the 11th cent, by Greek builders from Edessa.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The S. side consists of two huge towers ; by that to the W. are a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number of stone and wooden balls, probably dating from the Mame¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}luke period. Tuman Bey, the last ofthe Circassian sultans of Egypt,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was hanged outside this gate by Sultan Selim I., on 15th April,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1517 (p. 33). This gate is also called Bdb el-Mitwelli, from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}old tradition that the most highly revered saint Kutb el-Mitwelli{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has his abode behind the western gate, where he sometimes makes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his presence known by a gleam of light. From the inner (E.) gate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hang bunches of hair, teeth, flowers, shreds of clothing, and other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}votive offerings, placed here by sick persons who hope thereby to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cured of their diseases. Opposite the outside of the gate is the sebil{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Sultan Farag, by the large grated window of which executions by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strangulation took place down to the middle of the 19th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Zuweleh Gate the Derb el-Ahmab, (PL E, 4) leads{% endraw %}
