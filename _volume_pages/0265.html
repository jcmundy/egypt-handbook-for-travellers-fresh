{% raw %}The Gami'a Ibn Kalaun (PL F, 6), situated to the N.E. of the Mosque{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Mohammed 'Ali, was erected in 1317 by Sultan en-Nasir. Long used{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a military magazine and storehouse, it has recently been cleared out,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and is willingly shown by the British military authorities. It exhibits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traces of the Romanesque taste on the exterior, particularly on the portals.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the interior are some marble columns with ancient capitals.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Immediately to the S.E. of the Mosque of Ibn Kalaun is the so-called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Well of Joseph (BirYtisuf; PL F, 6), a square shaft, sunk in the limestone rock{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to a depth of 290 ft. Within the shaft, at a depth of about 155 ft., is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}platform on which the oxen stood that brought the water to the surface{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by means of a sakiyeh. The well formerly provided the citadel with water,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but has lost its importance since the completion of the new water-works{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 63). When the citadel was constructed here in the 12th cent., the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}builders discovered an ancient shaft filled with sand, which Saldheddin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ytisuf (p. 32) caused to be re-opened and named after himself Yusuf's, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Joseph's, Well. This circumstance gave rise to the tradition, which was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly current among the Jews, that this was the well into which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Joseph of Scripture was put hy his brethren.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Gami'a Suleman Pasha (PL F, 6), also called Sdryat, on the N.E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side of the citadel, was erected in 1526 by Suleman, the Mameluke, after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards Sultan Selim. The architecture is a mixture of Arabian and Turkish.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The mosque is small, but carefully executed. It contains Cufic inscriptions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble mosaics, a decorated prayer-recess, a pulpit in marble, and in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teresting door-mounts.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the tombs of the Khalifs and Mamelukes, see p. 64.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Bdb el-Gebel ('mountain-gate'), to the E. of the citadel, a road{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads straight to the Mokattam (p. 108). A road diverging to the right a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}little farther on leads to a Dervish Monastery (visitors admitted), situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on a mountain-slope. (The monastery may also be reached from the Place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mehemet Ali via the narrow lanes between the Tombs of the Mamelukes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the citadel.) An easy staircase ascends to an attractive court, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which are situated the residences of the monks. Coffee is frequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offered to travellers, all recompense being declined. From the court a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dark cave (probably an old quarry) enters the mountain-side, with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graves of deceased dervishes. At the end is a chamber containing the tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the founder of the order of dervishes, where worshippers are frequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}observed. The remains of a female relative of the Khedive also rest here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under an elaborate gilt tomb.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We return to the Place Rumeleh (p. 51) and follow the Shari'a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mohammed 'Ali to its intersection with the Shdri'a el-Hilmlyeh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL D, 5, 6; the donkey-drivers know short-cuts hither from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}citadel). We follow the latter street and its continuation es-Siyu-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}flyeh. Three minarets soon come into sight, the two most distant{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belonging to the Gami'a Shekhun (PL D, 6). At the corner opposite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mosque is the Sebil of the Mother of 'Abbas I. (PI. D, 6), in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble, rich and effective in general appearance, but lacking finish{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in its details. The street now takes the name of Shdri'a er-Rukblyeh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL D, 6, 7). We follow it for about 300 yds., and turn down the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shdri'a Ibn TulUn to the right, in which, after about 70 yds. more,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we observe on the right a lane leading to the E. entrance of the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Gami'a Ibn Tulun (PL D, 7; also pronounced Talun). This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosque, the oldest in Cairo, was erected by Ahmed Ibn Tulun, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}founder of the dynasty of the Tulunides (p. xciii), in the year 879.{% endraw %}
