{% raw %}tinuation of that described in Section 2. — The N.W. portion of Cairo{% endraw %}

{% raw %}From the Sikkeh el-Gedideh (Rue Neuve), opposite the Garni a{% endraw %}

{% raw %}To the right lies the entrance to the Khan el-Khalil (p. 42),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nearly opposite which is the Suk es-S&igh (pi. Siyagh), or bazaar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the gold and silver smiths, which consists of several crooked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lanes, barely a yard in width. The occupants of these crowded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alleys keep their wares in glass-cases or under glass shades. Their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stalls present a very poor appearance, but their filigree-work is{% endraw %}

{% raw %}frequently sold as genuine. The silver manufactured at the shops{% endraw %}

{% raw %}el-Kadi (PL E, 3), or 'House of the Judge', originally a palace of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Emir Mamay, a general of Kait Bey. In the large court on the{% endraw %}

{% raw %}capitals. This is the entrance to the building in which the kadi{% endraw %}

{% raw %}wife. — We. now return to the main street.{% endraw %}
