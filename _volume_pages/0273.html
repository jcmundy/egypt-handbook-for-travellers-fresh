{% raw %}Khalifs (entr. from the Derb Kirmez). — About 200 yds. farther{% endraw %}

{% raw %}the Mosque of Abu Bekr Mazhar el-Ansari (PL E, 2).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This mosque was built in 1497 by Abu Bekr, director of the chancery{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Sultan Kait Bey, in the style of the small mosques ofthe later Mameluke{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period, and has of late been thoroughly restored. It is full of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}characteristic decoration of its date. Especially noteworthy is the treat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment of the E. wall of the interior, where red and black plaster has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pressed into the white marble background as a substitute for the more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}costly marble mosaic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther on the Shari'a en-Nahhasin assumes the name Shdri'a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bdb el-Futuh. On the left, about 50 paces farther on, we reach the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrance of the ruinous —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gami'a el-Hakim (PL E, 2), begun in 990, on the plan of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gami'a Ibn Tulun (p. 53), by Khalif el-'Aziz, of the Fatimite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dynasty, and completed by his son El-Hakim in 1012. It was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seriously damaged by the earthquake of 1302, but was restored by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bibars el-Gashankir. It now lies again in ruins, with the exception{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the sanctuary, which has been provided with a modern ceiling.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The two minarets are not unlike that of Ibn Tulun.{% endraw %}
