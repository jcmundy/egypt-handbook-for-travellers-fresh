{% raw %}5. The Immediate Environs of Cairo.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most interesting points in the immediate neighbourhood of Cairo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may all be reached by Carriage (p. 27); but travellers who use Donkeys{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 27) are more independent among the Tombs of the Khalifs and elsewhere.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1. The Tombs of the Khalifs and the Mamelukes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A visit to the Tombs of the Khalifs (and to a certain degree to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tombs of the Mamelukes also) is exceedingly interesting, especially to¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards sunset, owing to the very novel and curious picture they present. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tickets of Admission, see p. 30.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The large and superb mausolea of the 13-16th cent., which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the names Tombs of the Khalifs and Tombs of the Mamelukes,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stretch along the entire E. side of the city, were erected by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bahrite and Circassian Mameluke sultans. The name 'Tombs of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Khalifs', applied to the northernmost group, is historically a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}misnomer, for the tombs have no connection with the 'Abbaside{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khalifs then resident in Egypt (p. xciii) and treated as mere titled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}puppets. All these mosque-tombs were once richly endowed, each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being provided with a numerous staff of shekhs and attendants, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with their families resided within their precincts. The revenues of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mosques having been confiscated at the beginning of the pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sent century, the tombs gradually fell to ruin. Now, however, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Committee mentioned at p. 30 has taken them into its keeping.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The usual route (very dusty) to the *Tombs of the Khalifs leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through the Bdb en-Nasr (PL E, 2; p. 62), past the Mohammedan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cemetery and the reservoir of the water-works (p. 63). To the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Windmill Hill (p. 67). Beyond the unimportant tomb of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shikh Zaldl we have one of the finest *Views of the city of the dead.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We may also proceed from S. to N. through the Bdb el-Atabeg (el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alldba) or the Bdb el-Wezir, both of which are to the N. of the citadel.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In this case we cross the small cemetery of Kardfet Bdb el- Wezir and first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reach the Tomb Mosque of Kait Bey. We finish our inspection with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N. group of tombs (see below) and return to the town hy the Bdb en-Nasr.{% endraw %}
