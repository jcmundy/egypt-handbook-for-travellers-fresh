{% raw %}is protected by a roof. The okella, the sebil, and the mosque all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belonged to the burial-place of Kait Bey, which covered an area{% endraw %}
{% raw %}330 yds. long. Its exact limits cannot now be determined, and a{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The *Tomb Mosque of Kait Bey (p. xcvii), built in 1463 and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restored in 1898, is the finest edifice among the Tombs of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khalifs. It is distinguished from all the others by its lofty dome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with bands of sculpture, its slender minaret (135 ft. high),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its harmonious proportions, and its elaborate stalactite and other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ornamentation. In the interior we notice the beautiful marble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaic, the tasteful ceilings, the pulpit, and the kamariyehs (partly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new). Within the mausoleum (PL 8) are shown two stones, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are said to have been brought from Mecca by Kait Bey, and to bear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impressions of the feet of the prophet; a finely carved desk; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beautiful ivory carvings in the canopy over the tomb.{% endraw %}
