{% raw %}**19. Wooden Statue from Sakkara, known as the Shikh el-Beled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(village-chief), a name given to it by the Arabs on account of its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resemblance to a well-fed specimen of that modern functionary.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The feet, which had been broken off, are restored, but the rest of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the figure is in its original condition. The arms are separately worked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and attached to the body. The upper part of the body and the legs are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bare, while from the hips hangs a kind of apron folded in front. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hand is the long rod of office. The round head with its short hair, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the portrait-like, good-natured face are remarkably lifelike. The eyes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were put in. They consist of pieces of opaque white quartz with pupils{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formed of rock-crystal, and they are framed with thin plates of bronze,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the edges of which form the eyelids.{% endraw %}
