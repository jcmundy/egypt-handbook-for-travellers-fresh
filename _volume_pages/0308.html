{% raw %}*290. The famous Decree of Canopus, in three languages, found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Tanis (p. 156).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The decree appears above in hieroglyphics, or the Ancient Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}written language, below in Greek, and on the margins in the popular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dialect written in the Demotic character. The decree was pronounced by an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}assembly of the priests in the temple of Canopus on 7th March (17th Tybi),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}B.C. 238, in the reign of Ptolemy III. Euergetes I. It praises the king for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}having brought back the images of the gods from Asia, gained many{% endraw %}
{% raw %}victories, preserved peace in the land, and saved it from imminent famine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by his forethought in remitting taxes and importing corn. In token of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gratitude a resolution is passed to institute new festivals in honour of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the king and queen and their ancestors, to call all priests also 'priests of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the divine Euergetfe', to found a new sacerdotal caste to be named after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Euergetes, and introduce an improvement in the popular calendar so that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the festival of Euergetes may always be celebrated on the first day of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}year as in the year of the decree. It is also resolved to pay permanent hon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}our to the Princess Berenice, who died young and unmarried, and to celebrate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}certain yearly festivals to her memory. The inscriptions lastly declare{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that the decree is to be inscribed in the holy (hieroglyphic), the Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(demotic), and the Greek languages, and to be exhibited in the temples.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}**291. Marble head of a Gaul, an original Greek work of great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beauty, dating from the beginning of the Ptolemaic period (p. civ).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This head may be compared with those of the Dying Gaul in the Ca-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pitoline Museum and the Group of Gauls in the Museo Boncompagni at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, though it is executed in an entirely different style. The work¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manship is much freer, fresher, and less careful; the back of the head{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and neck are but partly worked. Only the head, throat, and part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}drapery are preserved. They appear to have belonged to a statue of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man represented in the most energetic movement, probably fighting. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}head is thrown back towards the right shoulder and the eyes look up¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards in keen excitement.{% endraw %}
