{% raw %}Room LXXXIV. Royal Mummies and Objects found with them.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Towards the close of the New Empire the power of the Egyptian state{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gradually declined, until finally not even the last resting-places of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dead could be secured from robbers. Not only the necropolis at Drah{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abu'l-Negga (p. 262), but even the secluded tombs in the 'Valley of Kings'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 262) were plundered. The authorities recognized their impotence and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contented themselves with rescuing the mummies of the ancient Pharaohs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The mummy of Ramses II. was therefore transferred from its tomb at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Biban el-Muluk (p. 267) to that of Sethos I., and when that refuge ceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be deemed secure, both bodies were removed to the tomb of Amenophis I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Finally, under the 21st Dyn., it was resolved to protect the royal mummies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from farther profanation by interring them all together in a rocky cleft{% endraw %}
{% raw %}near Der el-bahri (p. 283), which was artificially widened for the purpose.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The corpses of' the ruling dynasty were also placed here. Thus at last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the remains of the great monarchs of the New Empire — Amosis I.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thutmosis III., Sethos I., and Ramses II. — were left in peace until in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1875 the fellahin once more discovered their secret resting-place and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plundering of the mummies began again. The modern thieves succeeded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in keeping their secret to themselves for a considerable period, but in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1881 they were traced, and the mummies were deposited in the museum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Cairo to save them from the hands of the robbers.{% endraw %}
