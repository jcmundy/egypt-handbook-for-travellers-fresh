{% raw %}About 3/4 M. beyond the garden are situated the ruins of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}famous ancient Heliopolis, or city of the sun, called On by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians. The latter name frequently occurs in the Bible. Thus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Genesis (xli, 45), we are informed that Pharaoh gave Joseph 'to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wife Asenath, the daughter of Potipherah (Egypt. Pete-pre, "he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whom the sun-god Re has given'), priest of On'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Heliopolis-On was one of the most ancient Egyptian cities and was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the chief town of a separate province, included in Lower Egypt. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deities of the place were the hawk-headed Re-Harmachis (the sun-god,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whence the Greek name Heliopolis) and the human-headed Atum, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was incarnated in the sacred Mnevis Bull. To these was dedicated the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}famous temple, 'the House of Re', built on the site of an earlier edifice{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Amenemhet /., first king of the 12th Dyn., in front of which his son{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and successor TJsertesen I. erected two great obelisks (see below) in cel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ebration of an important anniversary. A large section of the Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious literature was due to the priests of Heliopolis, and their doctrines{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were widely disseminated throughout the country at a very early period,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so that Re-Harmachis was one of the most highly venerated deities in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt. — Even during the Greek period these priests enjoyed a high{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reputation for wisdom; Herodotus conversed with them and Plato is said{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to have spent thirteen years with them, in order to learn some at least{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of their doctrines. — Under the New Empire the temple of Heliopolis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the largest and most richly endowed in all Egypt, next to the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ammon at Thebes. — When Strabo (b. about 60 B.C.) visited Egypt,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the city had been destroyed, but the temple was still intact, except for some{% endraw %}
{% raw %}minor injuries attributed to Cambyses; even the houses of the priests{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the apartments of Plato and his friend Eudoxus were shown to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller. The priestly school, however, had ceased to exist, and only a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few officiating priests and guides for foreigners resided there.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The outer walls are now the only vestiges of the city, while of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temple nothing is left but a few scanty ruins and the *Obelisk.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The latter, the oldest Egyptian obelisk known, is of red granite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Syene (Assuan), and is 66 ft. high; but the ground on which it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stands has been so raised by deposits of mud, that a great part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obelisk is now buried. Each of the four sides bears the same in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scription in bold hieroglyphics; that on the N. side, the only one{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely legible, records that Usertesen I., King of Upper and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lower Egypt, lord of the diadems and son of the sun, whom the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(divine) spirits of On (Heliopolis) love, etc., founded the obelisk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the first festival of Set (a kind of jubilee celebration). The in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scriptions on the other three sides are more or less covered by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cells of wild bees, besides being otherwise damaged. The pyr-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amidium at the top and the hawks which begin the inscriptions on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each side were once covered with metal. The companion obelisk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(for these monuments were always erected in pairs) stood down to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 12th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the W. of the obelisk the remains of the temple may be recog¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nized in a few blocks of granite, bearing inscriptions by Ramses II. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one Ramses II. appears offering a libation to Atum. — The necropolis of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Heliopolis, where coffins and tombstones of a late period have recently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been found, does not repay a visit.{% endraw %}
