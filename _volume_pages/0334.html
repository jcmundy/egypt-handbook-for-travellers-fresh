{% raw %}110 Route 7. SPRING OF MOSES. Environs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This hill stands at the mouth of the narrow, winding valley, 3/i M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in length, through which the path to the Spring of Moses ascends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}over large blocks of stone and rubble. The ravine terminates in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lofty amphitheatre of rock. Here is a cleft in the rock from which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trickle a few drops of bitter and brackish water, quite arbitrarily{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named the Spring of Moses ('Ain Musa). The chief attractions of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the gorge consist in the numerous desert plants and the fossils it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains and in the sense of perfect solitude which it conveys.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In order to reach the smaller Petrified Forest, we return to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mouth of the gorge and proceed towards the S.E., skirting the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slopes of the Mokattam, which are here more precipitous. We first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pass a black projecting rock, which has a glazed appearance, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then a square gap in the rock, beyond which we observe opposite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to us gently sloping hills, consisting of limestone, marl, and beds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of fossil oysters. The route ascends, a little to the right, between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these hills, and soon reaches the plateau of the Gebel el-Khashab,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where the scattered fragments of fossil wood indicate the begin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ning of the Little Petriped Forest. Almost all these trunks and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fragments have been referred by Unger to an extinct tree, which he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named the Nicolia ^Egyptiaca and regarded as akin to the bombacese.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The petrifaction is now generally supposed to have occurred during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the later tertiary period under the action of silicious geysers, such{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as are to be seen to-day in the Yellowstone Park of North America.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The silicated trunks lie in a secondary stratum, the overlying strata{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in which they were originally embedded having disappeared in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course of the desert denudation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Crossing the plateau of the Petrified Forest for about 20 min.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more towards the S., we suddenly reach the S. slopes of the Mo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kattam, through a gap in which a path descends into the Wadi et-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ti'h, or 'valley of wanderings' (more correctly Wadi Digld). This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}valley stretches to the W.N.W. towards the valley of the Nile, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}begins at the hills of Gharabun, like the parallel Wadi Hof (p. 155),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which debouches to the N. of Helwan. On the S. horizon rise the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills of Turra (p. 155), recognisable by the old fortress on their right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spur and by two heights exactly opposite to us, of which that to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left somewhat resembles a coffin in shape, while that to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is hemispherical. Crossing the bottom of the valley in this direction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(S.), we perceive in the Turra hills the entrance to a desert gorge,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bounded by lofty and precipitous slopes. This valley extends for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}many miles in various windings, communicates with the ravines{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the desert which begin in the Gebel Hof near Helwan, and is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abundantly stocked with the plants peculiar to the desert.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We may return to Cairo from the Little Petrified Forest through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 'Valley of Wanderings', skirting the S. and W. slopes of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mokattam, and passing the Tombs of the Mamelukes. Another in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teresting return-route leads across the Mokattam hills. If we choose{% endraw %}
