{% raw %}under the Romans and under the Arabs, renewed attempts were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made to penetrate to the treasures supposed to lie in the interior.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first modern traveller who carefully and successfully examined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Pyramids was Nicholas Shaw in 1721; but he still entertained the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}notion that the Sphinx had a subterranean connection with the Great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pyramid. He was followed by Norden in 1737; Pococke in 1743, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gives a plan and dimensions; Fourmont in 1755; Carsten Niebuhr in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1761; Davison in 1763; Bruce in 1768; Volney in 1783; Browne in 1792-98;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Denon, Coutelle, Jomard, and other savants of the French expedition{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under Bonaparte in 1799-1801. Jomard in particular has the merit of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}having taken very accurate measurements. Hamilton, in 1801, was a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dispassionate and critical observer. In 1817, Caviglia, a bold, but illiterate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and fanciful seaman, was fortunate in eliciting new facts regarding the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interior ofthe Great Pyramid, and excavated the Sphinx. In 1817, Belzoni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thoroughly explored the interior of the Second Pyramid. Belzoni, an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intelligent explorer and accurate draughtsman, was originally a monk at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, but when the French occupied that city he retired to London,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where he devoted himself to study in spite of many hardships. In 1815 he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arrived in Egypt, where, besides exploring the pyramid, he discovered the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tomb of Sethos I. at Thebes, etc. The next eminent explorer was Sir Gardner{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wilkinson in 1831. In 1837 and 1838 Col. Howard Vyse and 3Ir. Perring{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made very thorough investigations and took careful measurements which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}will always be considered authoritative. In 1842-45 Prof. Lepsius, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distinguished German Egyptologist, made several very important discov¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eries, and furnished us with much valuable information. He found no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fewer than thirty pyramids which had been quite unknown to previous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travellers. G. Maspero opened the small pyramids of Sakkara in 1880{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and discovered important inscriptions. W. M. Flinders Petrie subjected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Pyramids of Gizeh to a new and thorough investigation in 1881-82.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Pyramids of Dahshur were examined in 1894-95 by De Morgan; those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Lisht in 1895 by Gautier and Je'quier.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The pyramids of Gizeh stand upon a plateau, which extends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 1600 yds. from E. to W. and about 1300 yds. from N. to S..{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the E. and N. margins being very precipitous at places. The pyr¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amids are built exactly facing the four cardinal points. The diagonal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the largest pyramid from N.E. to S.W. is exactly in a line with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the diagonal of the second pyramid.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ** Great Pyramid is called by the Egyptians 'Yekhwet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khufu' or the 'Gloriow Place of Khufii. No trace is now left of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its original outermost covering, which probably consisted of slabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of limestone and granite. The length of each side (PL A A) is now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}750 ft., but was formerly (PL B B) about 768 ft.; the present per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pendicular height (PL E C) is 451 ft., while originally (PL E E),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}including the nucleus of rock (PL FF) at the bottom, and the apex{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL CE), which has now disappeared, it is said to have been 482 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The height of each sloping side (A C) is now 568 ft., and was for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merly (PL B E) 610ft. The angle at which the sides rise is 51°50'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The cubic content of the masonry, deducting the foundation of rock{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the interior, as well as the hollow chambers, was formerly no less{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than 3,277,000 cubic yards, and it still amounts to 3,057,000 cubic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yards. In round numbers, the stupendous structure covers an area{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of nearly thirteen acres. The material of which it is constructed is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone from the Mokattam and from Turra, containing numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fossils, chiefly nummulites (p. 108").{% endraw %}
