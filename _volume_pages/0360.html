{% raw %}1 28 Route S. TOMB OF NUMBERS. Environs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We may complete our circuit of the plateau of the Pyramids by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visiting the Sphinx (p. 123) and the Granite Temple, (p. 124), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then return to the Great Pyramid and the three small Pyramids{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lying in front of it to the E. — At the foot of the E. side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}southernmost of these small pyramids lies a small Sanctuary of Isis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL o), 'Mistress of the Pyramids') which was rebuilt by Psusennes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(21st Dyn.). Only a few stumps of columns now remain.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We may now proceed to the E. to the verge ofthe desert plateau,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the direction of the Arab village, in order the inspect the Tombs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofthe Ancient Empire, hollowed out in the rocky slope. Several of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these are now used as dwellings or stables. The best known is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb of Numbers (PL n), which belonged to a certain Khafra-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}onkh, a courtier of Khafre. On the left entrance-wall appear the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased and his brother, accompanied by a dog, inspecting the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cattle that are driven before them by peasants. The peasants are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arranged in several rows, headed by a standard-bearer. Writers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are engaged in recording the number of cattle of each kind, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numbers being placed above the herds (whence the name ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tomb). Thus we are informed that Khafra-onkh had 853 bulls, 220{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cows and calves, 2235 goats, 760 asses, and 974 rams. On the left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(S.) wall are the deceased and his wife at table. On the rear (W.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall are five door-shaped steles with a full-face portrait in high{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relief of the deceased, to the left. The mural reliefs, which are in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}poor preservation, are in the clumsy and undeveloped style of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4th Dynasty. — On the S. horizon rise the pyramids of Abusir{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 129) and the step-pyramid of Sakkara (p. 134).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beside the village of Kerddseh, about 5 M. to the N. of the Pyramids{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Gizeh, rise the Pyramids of Abu Roash, which were erected towards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the end of the 4th Dynasty. They are most conveniently reached on camels{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hired from the Beduins (enquire at the Mena House Hotel). The route{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads through the desert, skirting the edge of the cultivated land. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}largest pyramid built by Tetf-Re (p. 127) and called El-Kd'a by the natives,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is situated upon a steep rock, almost inaccessible from the plain, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reached by an approach, still nearly 1 JI. long, from the N.E. Nothing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is left of the pyramid but its rocky core, and the same is true of a smaller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone-pyramid to the S.W. The plateau commands a fine view of the Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}valley and of the gorges of the Libyan desert. — There is a third pyramid,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built of brick, in the plain to the N. of the village of Abu Roash. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brick superstructure, which was 55 ft. in height when Lepsius saw it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1842, has since been entirely demolished, and nothing now remains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but the rock-core with the tomb-chamber.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Excursion to the Pyramids of Abusir, to the S. of Gizeh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is interesting. We take the electric tramway to the Mena House Hotel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 112), and ride thence in l*/2-2 hrs. on a donkey (there and back{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10 pias.) or camel. Or a donkey may be taken direct from Cairo in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2'/2 nrs., via, the villages of Gizeh and Sbobrement; or the excursion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be combined with that to Sakkara (p. 130).{% endraw %}
