{% raw %}Shobrement. After about 1 hr. we have the large cemetery of Shobre-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment on our right. A low mound of rubbish on the summit of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert-plateau marks the site of the stone-pyramid of Zdwiyet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-'Aryan. In ll/% hr. we reach the rubbish heaps of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abu Gurab, formerly also called the Pyramid ofRigh, the explora¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of which was carried on in 1898-1901, on behalf of Berlin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Museum, by Drs. Borchardt and Schsefer. The building was a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sanctuary of the Sun God, erected by King Ra-en-woser (5th Dyn.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the occasion of the jubilee of his accession.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The sanctuary stands upon an artificial platform and consists of an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}uncovered court, 330 ft. long by 250 ft. broad, with its entrance on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E. side, while in the posterior (W.) half of it rose the large Obelisk of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sun. From the entrance-gate a covered passage, ornamented with fine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs, led to the left along the E. and S. sides of the court, and then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}turned to the right (N.) to reach the obelisk. The obelisk itself has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}totally vanished, but part of the platform of masonry on which it stood{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is still extant; and the top of this, reached by an internal staircase, com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mands a fine view. In the front half of the court was the place for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slaughtering the sacrificial bulls; the channels or gutters in the pavement{% endraw %}
{% raw %}empty themselves into nine alabaster basins (originally ten). In front of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the platform of the obelisk stands the altar, 19 ft. long, 18 ft. broad, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4 ft. high, built of five massive blocks of alabaster. On the S. side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obelisk is a ruined chapel, which was embellished with admirable reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(now partly in Cairo, partly in Berlin). On the N. side of the obelisk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was another sacrificial court. The N. side of the court was flanked by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}treasure-houses, reached from the entrance-gate by a passage (to the right){% endraw %}
{% raw %}similar to that described above. — The town connected with the sanctuary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lay in the valley to the E. of the hill.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The three largest Pyramids of Abusir, erected by kings of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5th Dyn., stand close together about 3/4 M. to the S.W. of the sanc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tuary of Abu Gurab. The masonry of these monuments, having orig¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inally been constructed with no great care, is now much damaged,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and their bases are covered with sand. The entrances are on the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sides, and the interior chambers are almost completely in ruins.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The northernmost of the pyramids belonged to King Sehure, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was connected by a still traceable path with a building (probably a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple) situated in the plain. Its perpendicular height was 163'/2 ft-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(now 118 ft.), its sides were 25872 ft- (now 217V4 ft.) in length,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and they were inclined at the angle of 51° 42'39". The central{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramid, to the S. of the last, belonged to King Ra-en-woser{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(5th Dyn.). The builder of the largest pyramid (sides 108, formerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}120'/2 yds.; perpendicular height 165, formerly 229 ft.), situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a little to the S.W., was King Nefer-er-ke-re (5th Dyn.). The other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramids, some of which were certainly also sanctuaries of the sun,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are mere heaps of ruins, and one of them (to the S.W. ofthe largest){% endraw %}
{% raw %}seems never to have been completed.{% endraw %}
