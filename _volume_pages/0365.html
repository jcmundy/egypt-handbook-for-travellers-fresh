{% raw %}MEMPHIS. 9. Route. 131{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At (20 M.) Bedrashen visitors bound for Memphis and Sakkara{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leave the train, which goes on to Upper Egypt. The station lies to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right of the line. We ride along the railway, turn to the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cross a bridge, and follow the embankment towards the village of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bedrashen and a conspicuous grove of palms on the W. We pass{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through the bazaar of the village, where candles may be bought at a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek 'bakkai'. On each side of the embankment lie green fields in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spring and summer, and large expanses of water in autumn and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the first half of winter. The embankment ends near the first trees{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the palm-groves, 20 min. from the station. The large mounds of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rubbish before us, shaded by palms, and strewn with blocks of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}granite, broken pottery, and fragments of brick, mark the ancient —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Site of Memphis.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Were it not for the vast Necropolis to the W. of the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city, no one would imagine that one of the most famous and pop¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ulous capitals of antiquity had once stood here. The Egyptians,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the earliest period down to the Boman imperial epoch, built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their edifices, with the exception of palaces and temples, of large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sun-dried bricks of Nile-mud; but even the public buildings of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Memphis have entirely disappeared, as the stones were carried off in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}former centuries to build other edifices on the right bank of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile. The narrow streets, which are said to have been half-a-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}day's journey in length even down to the 12th cent., extended{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between the Nile and the Girzeh Canal, to the N. as far as Gizeh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and to the S. about as far as the latitude of the Pyramids of Dahshur.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most important quarters and buildings of the city appear to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood in the fields of the villages of Bedrashen and Mit Rahineh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Histoby. Memphis, the capital of the nome of 'the White Wall',{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belonged to Lower Egypt and from a very early period played an im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant strategic and political role, owing to its position on the borders{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between the two ancient kingdoms of the 'North' and the 'South' (p. lxxv).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A later tradition ascribes its foundation to Menes, the first historical ruler{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Egypt, who is said to have built also the temple of Ptah (Hephsestos),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the patron-god of the city. Memphis consisted of several quarters, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most important of which were the 'White Wall' (in later times the citadel),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 'South Wall', with a temple of Ptah, and the 'House of the Spirit of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptah', or great temple-precincts of that god. Under the 6th Dyn. a new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarter was founded, in which King Pepi fixed the residence of his court{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and near which the sepulchral pyramid of the ruler was situated. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarter, and also the pyramid, were called Merire-men-nofer, i.e. King{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Merire (Pepi) remains beautiful', and this name (in the later form Menfe,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Greek Memphis) was afterwards applied to the whole city. Memphis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attained its greatest prosperity under the monarehs of the Ancient Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who resided here or in the vicinity (near Gizeh). When Thebes became{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the centre of Egypt and the Theban Ammon the most revered among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the gods, under the Middle and New Empires, Memphis appears to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}retrograded. But even in the time of the 20th Dyn. the temple of Ptah{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the largest in the country but two. In the course of the contests{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the possession of Egypt, which raged after the 22nd Dyn., the city was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}captured by the Ethiopian Piankhi and by the Assyrians, and on several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other occasions.{% endraw %}
