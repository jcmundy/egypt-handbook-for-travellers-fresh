{% raw %}1 32 Route 9. MEMPHIS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cambyses, the first monarch of the Persian dynasty, took the city by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}storm after his victory at Pelusium (B.C. 525) over Psammetikh III.; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even after the foundation of Alexandria (B.C. 332) it still retained some{% endraw %}
{% raw %}importance. Under Augustus it was a large and populous city, though{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its palaces, elevated on an eminence, lay ruined and deserted. Among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temples that still existed were those of Ptah, of Apis (p. 135), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a female deity, who was identified with the Aphrodite of the Greeks.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In consequence of the edict of Theodosius (A.D. 379-395; comp. p. xcii){% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temples and statues were destroyed, and under the later Byzantine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monarchs the heretical Monophysites (p. xciii) seem to have been very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous here. Mukaukis, the leader of the Copts, was established at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Memphis while negotiating with 'Amr Ibn el-'As, the general of 'Omar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 32). The Mohammedan conquerors transferred their residence to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right hank of the Nile, opposite the northernmost part of Memphis, using{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the well-hewn blocks, which had once composed the venerable palaces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and temples of the ancient city of Menes, for the construction of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palaces, castles, and mosques at Cairo. Memphis, however, was so vast,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that it was long before its plunderers succeeded in entirely destroying it.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Down to a late period the ruins of Memphis excited the admiration of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}all visitors. Thus 'Abdellatif (at the end of the 12th cent.) assures us{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that even in his time the ruins contained a profusion of wonders which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bewildered the mind and baffled description. — After his time the rapidly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dwindling ruins of Memphis are rarely mentioned.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The path diverging to the left from the Bedrashen embankment,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and leading through the palm-grove to the village of Mlt RaMneh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brings us to the *Colossal Statues of Eamses II., which once{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marked the entrance to the temple. The first of these, discovered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1888, is made of granite, and lies on its back on a slight eminence,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so that to see the face it is necessary to climb on to its breast. Its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length is 25 ft., not including the crown, which is 6^/2 ft- in length.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The square hole in the head of the colossus was for the insertion of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the crown, which now lies on the ground beside it. On both shoulders,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}breast, girdle, and bracelet occurs the name of the king; and on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pillar at the back is an inscription. On the left of the statue is an incised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relief of Princess Bent-Anat. — A stele of Apries (26th Dyn.) and the upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of a double statue of Ptah and Ramses II. (?) have also been found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on this spot. In the rounded pediment of the stele appear Ptah, on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left, and the hawk-headed Sokaris, on the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A few min. farther on we reach the mud-hut that conceals the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Second Colossus (adm. 4 pias. for those without official admission{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ticket, see p. 130), discovered by Messrs. Caviglia and Sloane{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 116) in 1820. A wooden flight of steps ascends to a platform{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from which the statue is inspected. It consists of remarkably hard{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and fine-grained limestone, and before it was injured was about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}42 ft. in height, corresponding to the measurement given by Hero¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dotus (30 cubits of l1^ ft- each). The workmanship is excellent.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}produced. An artificial beard is attached to the chin. In the girdle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a dagger adorned with two hawk's heads. On the right shoulder,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the breast, and the girdle appear the prsenomina of Ramses II. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In front of the hut are several fragments of monuments, showing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the name of Ramses II.{% endraw %}
