{% raw %}SAKKARA. ,9. Route. 133{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From Mir Rahineh (Memphis) to Sakkara. We ride towards the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. from the statues of Ramses, leaving the village of Mit Rahineh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at a little distance to the right. On quitting the palm-grove we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obtain an attractive view; immediately to the right, shaded by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palm-trees and lebbeks, is the small House of Tigran-Bey, a nephew{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Nubar Pasha. About 1 M. to the W. is another long palm-grove{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounding Sakkara and bordering the desert; beyond this, on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yellow sand of the desert, rise eleven pyramids. The first of these,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left, is the S. brick-pyramid, beyond which are the blunted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramid, the N. brick-pyramid, and the great pyramid, all belong¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing to the group of Dahshur (p. 153). Not far from these we next{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perceive the Mastaba Fir'aun, with the pyramid of Pepi II.; then,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exactly above the houses of Sakkara, two pyramids, the lesser of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is that of Pepi I.; and, lastly, to the right, the pyramid of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Onnos, the great step-pyramid, and two smaller ones (to the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of Teti). These last eight pyramids belong to the group of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sakkara. — Having nearly reached (% hr. from the statues of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses) Sakkara, we leave the village to the left, passing an open{% endraw %}
{% raw %}space with a pond, turn towards the N., and skirt the palm-groves.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the end of these, on the left, is a beautiful, shady sycamore,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}close to a spring of good water.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A different route must be followed during the period of the inundation,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when the low ground between Mit Rahineh and Sakkara is under water.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the statues of Ramses we return to the (5 min.) end of the embank¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment (see p. 131), which leads back to Bedrashen, and then turn to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N., traverse the whole of the palm-grove, and ride through the brick{% endraw %}
{% raw %}houses of ancient Memphis, until we reach another embankment which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}winds across the plain towards the W., and is interrupted by a bridge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with sluices. In 20 min. more we reach the margin of the desert beside{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some brick ruins (marked 'Remains of a house in crude brick' on the map){% endraw %}
{% raw %}where we join the above route.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The united two routes ascend to the plateau , and bring us in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sight of the vast **Necropolis of Sakkara, which extends about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}41/3 M. in length from N. to S., and 1/4-I M. in width from E. to "W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It contains sepulchral monuments of every kind, dating both from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient and the later empire. Loose heaps of light-coloured{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sand mark the position of recent excavations. The whole of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Necropolis has been repeatedly explored both by the Byzantines and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Khalifs, as well as by modern explorers. The Arab name Sakkara{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has probably arisen from the corruption of some earlier Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name connected with Sokar, the god of the dead of Memphis.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Two routes to Mariette's House, which is 20 min. distant, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shown on the map. That to the right passes several unimportant{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramids, with the so-called Cemetery of the Cats, where numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mummies of cats and ibises were found, to the extreme right. No¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thing is to be seen here, as the tombs are now closed, being considered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dangerous. The whole of the soil of the Necropolis is indeed so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}honeycombed with tombs that great caution should be used in tra¬{% endraw %}
