{% raw %}Beyond the Step Pyramid, as we continue on our way to Ma¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}riette's House, a striking view opens towards the N. In the fore¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ground lies the green valley of the Nile, bordered by palm-trees,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and framed on both sides with the yellowish-grey desert; and we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also observe the alabaster mosque of Mohammed 'Ali at Cairo. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left tower the three pyramids of Gizeh, and the three nearer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramids of Abusir. The path turns to the right beyond the next{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heap of rubbish (N.W.), crosses the hollow, and soon reaches —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mariette's House. Mariette, the famous French Egyptologist,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first rose into notice by his discovery of the Apis Tombs in 1851;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and from 1858 till his death in 1881 was director of the official{% endraw %}
{% raw %}excavations in Egypt. No charge is made for admission to the terrace,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but it is usual to give a fee of 2'/2 pias. or more, according to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number of the party, to the 'Ghafirs' who take charge of the house.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A guide to the tombs must be taken at Mariette's House, as visitors{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are not admitted to them unattended.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A trodden path leads from Mariette's House, to the W., in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few min., to the **Apis Tombs, the subterranean part of the Egypt¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ian Serapeum, hewn in the rock.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apis, the sacred hull of the god Ptah (p. 132), which was worshipped{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a special temple at Memphis, was after death embalmed like a human{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being and interred with great, pomp in the necropolis of Memphis. As{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early as the reign of Amenophis III., and probably still earlier, the Apis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tombs consisted of a subterranean tomb-chamber, reached by a sloping{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shaft, over which a chapel was erected in honour of the bull. Under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses II. a large common grave was prepared for the Apis bulls by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Prince Kha-em-weset; a subterranean gallery, over 100yds. in length,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was hewn in the rock and flanked with chambers, which were walled up{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after receiving the wooden coffin containing the sacred remains. Psam¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}metikh I. caused a similar gallery with side-vaults to be constructed at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right angles to the first one. These vaults, which were added to at inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vals down to the Ptolemaic period, were much larger and more carefully{% endraw %}
{% raw %}constructed than the previous series. They have an aggregate length of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 380 yds. and are about 10 ft. in width and 17!/2 ft. in height. Above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them rose a large temple for the cult of the dead god. — The ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians believed that like the soul of men (p. cxxiii) the spirit of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased bull was united with Osiris, and became the 'Osiris-Apis' (Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Oser-hape; Gr. Osorapis). He thus became a kind of god of the dead and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was called, like Osiris, 'Lord of the western land'; pilgrims crowded to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the tomb to pay their devotions and to present votive offerings. The last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were usually small memorial tablets, which were inserted in the walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the subterranean galleries. The worship of the foreign god Serapis or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sarapis, introduced under Ptolemy I., rapidly spread in Egypt, and it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}easy to understand how the new Sarapis was confounded with Osorapis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and worshipped along with the latter in the ancient temple in the necro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}polis of Memphis. The temple itself came to be commonly known as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Serapeum or Sarapeum. Within the extensive chambers of the Serapeum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there was established a colony of hermits, who lived in the strictest se¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clusion in small cells, receiving even their food through narrow windows{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or air-holes. There is no doubt that these were the prototypes of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christian monks and ascetics of a later period. — A second temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Osorapis, built by Nektanebos, once stood opposite the temple covering{% endraw %}
