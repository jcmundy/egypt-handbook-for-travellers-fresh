{% raw %}DAHSHUR. 9. Route 153{% endraw %}
{% raw %}If time permit, the traveller may now proceed to the S. Group{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Sakkara, a ride of l'^hr. to the S. from Mariette's House. We{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pass near a space of ground about 440 yds. square, enclosed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}broad and massive, but now very dilapidated walls on the E., N.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and W. sides, while the S. side is bounded by the natural hills of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the desert. — The route now leads straight to the Mastaba el-Fir'aun,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the most interesting monument in the S. group. To the left are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the dilapidated Pyramids of Pepi I. and Merenre and a pyramid{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called by the Arabs Haram esh-Showwdf. On the N.W. side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mastaba el-Fir'aun is the Pyramid of Pepi II. All these pyramids{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(now inaccessible) are constructed and adorned exactly in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same manner as that of King Onnos (p. 134). The Mastaba el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fir'aun resembles in its shape some of the mastabas of the Middle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire. The passages in the interior resemble those in the pyr¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amids of Onnos and his successors, so that this monument may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably enough be a king's grave, as its name implies ('grave of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pharaoh'). The top, which is easily reached, commands a fine view.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the S. and E. slopes of the plateau of Sakkara are numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rock Tombs. They are far simpler than the mastabas both in point{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of construction and of internal decoration.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About IV2 M. to the S. of the Mastaba el-Fir'aun lie the Necropolis and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pyramids of Dahshur. Here rise two large pyramids and a smaller one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}limestone, and two of brick (the 'Black Pyramids'), together with remains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of others, all of which are at a considerable distance from each other.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The N. Brick Pyramid, which was once covered with slabs of stone, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably the tomb of King Usertesen III. (12th Dyn.). The length of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the side at the base is 345 ft., while the height is now only about 90 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N. of this pyramid, but within the girdle-wall that formerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enclosed it, are two subterranean galleries with tomb-chambers in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which female members of the royal family were interred. In the lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these galleries were found the jewels of the princesses Sit-Hathor and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Merit (now in the Gizeh Museum, p. 82). — At the E. base of the pyramid{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a house belonging to the Service des Antiquiiis, in which the night may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be spent by permission previously obtained at the Museum in Gizeh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the S.W. of the N. Brick Pyramid lies a larger Stone Pyramid.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This is still 326 ft. in height and 234 yds. in width, being nearly as large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as the Great Pyramid of Gizeh, and in its solitude presents a very imposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appearance, even to an accustomed eye. It was probably (like the Pyr¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amid of Meidum, p. 192) built by Snofru, and is thus the most ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}royal tomb in the form of a pyramid.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the E. and S. are remains of several other pyramids. Still farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the S. rises a pyramid of peculiar form, sometimes called the Blunted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pyramid, the lower part rising at an angle of 54° 41'. while the sides of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the apex form an angle of 42° 59'. This pyramid is 206'/a yds. square and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}321 ft. in height. The exterior coating is in good preservation. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interior was explored as early as the year 1660 by an English traveller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named Melton. In 1860 M. Le Brun found a small chamber in the interior.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}No clue to the name of the builder has been discovered.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the E. of the Blunted Pyramid and a few kilometres to the S. of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N. Brick Pyramid, not far from the village of Menshiyeh, which lies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the edge of the desert to the N. of Dahshur, rises the S. Brick Pyramid.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This, which was originally covered with limestone slabs, was perhaps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built by King Amenemhet III. (12th Dyn.). To the N. of it, but enclosed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the former girdle-wall, are the graves of King Hor and Princess Neb-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hetepti-khrot (p. 82). Between the N. and S. Brick Pyramids is the ruined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pyramid of Amenemhet II. (12th Dyn.).{% endraw %}
