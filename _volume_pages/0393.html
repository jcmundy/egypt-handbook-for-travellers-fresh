{% raw %}The dry and warm climate is specially adapted for rheumatic,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pulmonary, and kidney diseases, and for all cases in which cold{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and damp should be avoided (comp. also p. xxvii). Between Nov.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Feb. rain falls for a few hours only, and even during the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inundation, when the lower-lying river-valley is covered with mist{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the morning and evening, the atmosphere at Helwan is free{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from all perceptible moisture. The mean temperature in winter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is 61° 5'Fahr., with a daily range of 21° (from 51 to 72°). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amount of daily sunshine averages 8 hrs., and the fall of tem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perature after sunset is very slight. In consequence of the rocky{% endraw %}
{% raw %}soil and the powerful rays of the sun, the air is extraordinarily{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pure, dustless, and bracing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Helwan is within easy reach of Cairo. Excursions may be made{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the gorge-like valleys of the desert, and to Sakkara, while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banks of the Nile afford good wild-fowl shooting, but the desert{% endraw %}
{% raw %}game is shy and not easily reached.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The quarries of Ma'sara and Turra are reached from Helwan in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vfe hr's. ride; candles and matches should not be forgotten. These quarries,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which are still worked, yielded fome of the stone used in the construction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Pyramids and Mastabas and other ancient buildings down to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemaic period. The stone is transported to the hank of the Nile by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}means of tramways, carts, camels, and mules. The Arabs of the present{% endraw %}
{% raw %}day quarry the stone on the outside of the rocky slopes only, while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarrymen of the Pharaohs penetrated into the interior of the mountain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and excavated large chambers, when they came to serviceable stone. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apparently endless dark rocky halls can scarcely be trodden without a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}feeling of awe. The roofs are supported hy pillars of rock. A few in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scriptions, recording the opening of new halls in the quarries, some de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}motic inscriptions, and reliefs (Kings Amenemhet, Amosis, Amenophis III.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nekht-Har-ehbet) are still preserved. The Egyptians named these the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarries of Royu (or T-royu), which the Greeks corrupted into Troja, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Strabo relates that the village beside the quarries was 'an ancient residence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of captive Trojans who had followed Menelaus to Egypt and remained there'.{% endraw %}
