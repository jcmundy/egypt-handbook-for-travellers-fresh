{% raw %}Damietta, Arabic Dumydt, situated between the Damietta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}branch of the Nile and Lake Menzaleh, about 4 M. from the sea,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains 43,750 inhab. (few Europeans). Seen from the railway-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}station, situated near the harbour, Damietta presents an imposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appearance, with its lofty houses flanking the river. The interior{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the town, however, by no means fulfils the traveller's expecta¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions. On every side lie ruinous old buildings and walls. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}harbour is considerable, but its entrance is much silted up. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}insignificant trade of the place is chiefly in the hands of native{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merchants (Arabs and Levantines). The industries to which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town was indebted for its former prosperity (see below) still exist{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to some extent, and the traveller will find it interesting to visit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of the streets inhabited by the silk and cotton weavers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}History. Little or nothing is known of the early history of Damietta.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}During the Arabian era Damietta attained a great reputation on account{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the resistance it offered to the Crusaders ; but the town of that period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood farther to the N. than its modern successor (see below). In 1218 it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was besieged by King John of Jerusalem. With the aid of an ingenious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}double boat, constructed and fortified in accordance with a design by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Oliverius, an engineer of Cologne, the Frisians, Germans, and others{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the besiegers succeeded after a fight of twenty-five hours in capturing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the tower to which the chain stretched across the river was attached.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The success of the Christians was, however, considerably marred by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interference of the ambitious, though energetic Pelagius Galvani, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}papal legate, and by the vigilance of the Egyptian prince Melik el-Kamil.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At length, after various vicissitudes, the Christians captured the place.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}They obtained valuable spoil, sold the surviving townspeople as slaves,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and converted the mosques into churches, but in 1221 they were compelled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a treaty to evacuate the town. In 1249, when Louis IX. landed near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Damietta, it was abandoned by its inhabitants. Without striking a blow,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Crusaders marched into the deserted streets of the fortress, but in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course of the following year they were obliged to restore it to the Saracens{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as part of the ransom of Louis IX., who had been taken prisoner at Mansura{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 158). During the same year, by a resolution of the Emirs, the town{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was destroyed, and re-erected on the E. bank of the river, farther to the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The new town soon became an important manufacturing and commer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cial place. Its staple products were leather-wares, cloth, and essence of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sesame, for which it was famous, and its harbour was visited by ships of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}many different nations. By the construction of the Mahmudiyeh Canal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Damietta lost most of its trade, and its decline was farther accelerated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the foundation of the ports on the Suez Canal.{% endraw %}
