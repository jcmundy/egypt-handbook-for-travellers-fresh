{% raw %}Zakazik, a thriving, semi-European town, lies on a branch of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fresh Water Canal (see below) and on the Mu'izz (Moez) Canal (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient Tanite arm of the Nile, p. 156). It is the capital of the E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}province of Sherklyeh and seat of a Mudir, and contains 35,700 in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}habitants. The situation of Zakazik, in the midst of a fertile tract{% endraw %}
{% raw %}watered by several canals, and connected with the richest districts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Delta, is extremely favourable, and it is a rapidly improving{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place. Zakazik forms the chief centre of the Egyptian cotton and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grain trade. Many European merchants have offices here, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large cotton-factories give the place an almost European appearance.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}An Arab market is held here every Tuesday.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Zakazik is the junction for branch-railways to Kalyub (p. 23){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and to Abu Keblr-Mansura, etc. (see p. 156). Narrow-gauge rail¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}way to Sinbeldwln (p. 157).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the vicinity, near Tell Basta, 1/i hr. to the S., lie the ruins of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient Bubastis (Egyptian Per'-Baste; the Pibeseth of Ezekiel xxx. 17),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the capital of the Bubastite nome. The remains of the temple of Bastet,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the patron-goddess of the town, were excavated in 1887-89 by the Egypt¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ologist Naville, at the expense of the Egypt Exploration Fund; but they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are in too ruinous a state to repay a visit. The temple, begun by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pyramid-builders Kheops and Khephren, underwent frequent alterations{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the hands of Ramses II. and other later kings, but owed its final{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form to the monarchs of the 22nd Dyn., who resided at Bubastis, and to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nektanebes (Nekht-Har-ehbet). At this period it consisted of four large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}halls, of an aggregate length of 6U0 feet; and in these were celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the joyous and licentious festivals in honour of Bastet. 'When the Egypt¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ians travel to Bubastis', says Herodotus, 'they do so in this manner. Men{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and women sail together, and in each boat there are many persons of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both sexes. Some of the women make a noise with rattles, and some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the men blow pipes during the whole journey, while the other men and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}women sing and clap their hands. If they pass a town on the way, they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lay to, and some of the women land and shout and mock at the women{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the place, while others dance and make a disturbance. They do this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at every town that lies on the Nile; and when they arrive at Bubastis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they begin the festival with great sacrifices, and on this occasion more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wine is consumed than during the whole of the rest of the year. All the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}people of both sexes, except the children, make a pilgrimage thither,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 700,000 persons in all, as the Egyptians assert.'{% endraw %}
