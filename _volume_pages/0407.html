{% raw %}attempt to connect the Red Sea with the Nile (and thereby with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Mediterranean) was made by Nekho (p. lxxxv). His plan was to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extend towards the S., from Lake Timsah to the Red Sea, an earlier{% endraw %}
{% raw %}canal, in existence even under Ramses II., which diverged from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile near Bubastis and flowed through the Wadi Tumilat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 163). Herodotus informs us that no fewer than 120,000 Egypt¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ians perished while engaged in the work, and the king afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abandoned the undertaking, as he was informed by the oracle that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the barbarians (i.e. the Persians) alone would profit by it. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}canal was completed a century later by Darius. Its course roughly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corresponded to that of the present Fresh Water Canal. It was wide{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enough to permit two triremes to row abreast, and it was four days'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}journey in length. Darius commemorated the completion of the great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work by various monuments on its banks, of which the remains have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been found at different spots (e.g. to the S. of Tell el-Maskhiita;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the W. of the Serapeum station; to the N. of Shaluf near Kabret;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and to the N. of Suez). — Under the Ptolemies the canal system{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was extended, and locks were erected at its efflux into the Red Sea.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Canal fell into disrepair during the first Christian century,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Trajan (98-117 A.D.) seems to have restored it. At all events,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a canal, beginning near Cairo, and terminating in the Gulf of Suez,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the precise course of which, probably following the earlier channel,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is nowhere described, was called the Amnis Trajanus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}After the Arabs had conquered Egypt, they must have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desirous of connecting the Lower Egyptian part of^the Nile as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}directly as possible with the Red Sea. 'Amr ibn el-'As (p. xciii){% endraw %}
{% raw %}accordingly restored the ancient canal (of which the Khalig at Cairo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is said to be a portion), and used it for the transport of grain from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fostat (p. 32) to Kolzum (Suez), whence it was exported by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Red Sea to Arabia. The bed of the ancient canal is said to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been pointed out to 'Amr by a Copt. The canal again became un¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}serviceable after the 8th century. At a later period the Venetians{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequently thought of constructing a canal through the Isthmus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a view to recover the trade which they had lost owing to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}discovery of the route round the Cape of Good Hope, and several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travellers advocated the scheme; but no one seriously attempted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to carry it out. Leibnitz, too, in his proposal regarding an expe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dition to Egypt, made in 1671 to Louis XIV., the greatest monarch{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his age, strongly recommends the construction of such a canal.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sultan Mustafa III., the admirer of Frederick the Great, 'Ali Bey,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the enterprising Mameluke prince, and Bonaparte all revived the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scheme, and the latter on his expedition to Egypt in 1798 (p. cxv){% endraw %}
{% raw %}even caused the preliminary works to be undertaken, but the actual{% endraw %}
{% raw %}execution of the project seemed almost as distant as ever. Lepere,{% endraw %}

{% raw %}to a serious miscalculation he threw great doubt on the feasibility{% endraw %}
