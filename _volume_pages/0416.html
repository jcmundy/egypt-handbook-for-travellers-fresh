{% raw %}15. Suez and its Environs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Railway Stations. 1. Gare; 2. Rue Colmar (both for the town of Suez);{% endraw %}
{% raw %}3. Terre-Plein, for Port Tewfik; 4. Docks Station, for the docks.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hotels. — Hotel Bel Air (lessees, MM. Pelletier), opposite the English{% endraw %}
{% raw %}telegraph-office, well managed, good table; Hotel Bachet (lessee, Mme.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bachet), at Port Tewfik (p. 173), the property of the Canal Co., well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spoken of. There are also a few smaller hotels- — Beer. Cafi Olympia,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rue Colmar; Suez Bar, Rue Colmar (kept by a Greek).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Post and Telegraph Offices (Egyptian) at the station. Telegrams to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foreign countries should be despatched by the wires of the Eastern Tele¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graph Company (English). Branch post-office at Port Tewfik, with branch{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offices for both telegraph companies and a sub-office of customs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}English Physician: Dr. J. Creswell.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vice-Consuls. British, J. R. Norrish; American (Consular Agent), Alfred{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. Haydn; German, Th. Meyer; French, M. Allemer; Austrian, <?. Tomicich;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Russian, N. Costa (p. 174).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Junction Railway between the town and the harbour-island (p. 173),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in '/4 hr. (fare^ 2, 1 pias., return-ticket 3, l1/* pias.); trains hourly all day.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rowing Boats. A charge of 8-10 pias. is usually made for a rowing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boat for an hour. The boatmen are apt to he extortionate in their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}demands, as travellers on their way to or from India, and making a short{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stay only, are often too lavish in their payments. Comp. pp. 173 174.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Disposition of Time. The afternoon of the day of arrival may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}devoted to a visit to the Harbour and Canal Entrance, by rowing-boat or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the junction-railway. The next forenoon (early start necessary; comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 173) may be spent in an excursion to the Springs of Moses. A visit to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Coral Formations (p. 174) practically involves another day's stay at Suez.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shells and coral from the Red Sea may be bought from the boatmen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the harbour; bargaining necessary.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Suez lies at the head of the gulf of that name, one of the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extremities of the Red Sea, and to the S.W. of the mouth of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Suez Canal. Before the construction of the great work of M. de Les¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seps, it was a miserable Arabian village, with 1500 inhab. at most,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while in 1897 it contained 17,457, including 2774 Europeans. Its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trade, however, in spite of the opening of the canal and the construc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of large docks, has not materially increased. Neither the Arab¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ian quarter, with its seven mosques and unimportant bazaar, nor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the European quarter, which contains several buildings and ware¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}houses of considerable size, presents any attraction. The streets{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and squares are kept clean, and the climate is excellent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On a mound of de'bris to the N. of the town, not far from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}station and the magazines of the 'Khediviyeh Company', is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kiosque of the Khedive, commanding a fine view of the mountains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the peninsula of Sinai, the sea, the harbour, and the town. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hill is called by the Arabs Kom el-Kolzum, and was probably the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}site of the Ptolemaic fortress Klysma; relics of the period of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pharaohs have also been discovered here. Beyond the railway, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W., are the mud-huts of an Arab sailors' quarter. The small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eminence to the N.W. is named the Beduins' Hill. — A little{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther to the N. is the mouth of the Fresh Water Canal (p. 162),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the flow of which into the conduits, as well as its discharge into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sea, is regulated by means of a large lock. The level of the{% endraw %}
