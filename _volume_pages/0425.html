{% raw %}FAYUM. 16. Route. 175{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Railway from Cairo to Medinet el-Fayum, 75 M., in about 4 hrs. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trains (two daily in each direction) are often late. — From Medinet el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fayum the line goes on to Abuksa (Abouxah), but for a visit to the Birket{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Karun horses must be brought from Medineh (comp. p. 181).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Situation and History of the Fayum. In the great plateau of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Libyan Desert, which rises 300-400 ft. above the sea-level, is situated the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}province of the Fayum (from the ancient Egyptian 'Phiom', i.e. lake), the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first of the oases, which is usually considered to belong to the valley of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile, and is justly celebrated for its extraordinary fertility. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tract is in the form of an oval basin and is enclosed by the Libyan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills, which are here of moderate height, and lies about three-fifths of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}degree to the S. of Cairo. It enjoys a remarkably fine climate. This'land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of roses' is still one of the most beautiful parts of Egypt. The oranges{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and mandarins, peaches, olives, figs, cactus fruit, pomegranates, and grapes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grown here are much esteemed, and the fields, which are watered by means{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of wheels of peculiar construction, yield rice, sugar, cotton, flax, and hemp,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}besides the usual cereals. The beginning of November is probably the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}season at which the traveller will obtain the most distinct idea of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fertile character of the district. Even at the period of the Ptolemies and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Romans the products of the Fayum were much extolled. 'The Arsinoite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nome', says Strabo, 'is the most remarkable of all, both on account of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its scenery and its fertility and cultivation. For it alone is planted with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large and richly productive olive-trees, and the oil is good when the olives{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are carefully gathered; those who are neglectful may indeed obtain oil in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abundance, but it has a bad smell. In the rest of Egypt the olive-tree{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is never seen, except in the gardens of Alexandria, where under favour¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}able circumstances it yields olives, but no oil. Vines, corn, podded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plants, and many other products also thrive in this district in no small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abundance'. The Fayum is entirely indebted for its fertility to the Bahr{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Yusuf ('Canal of Joseph'), a channel 207 M. in length, which diverges from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile to the N. of Assiut, and flows through a narrow opening in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Libyan chain into the Fayum, where it divides into numerous ramifications,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abundantly watering the whole district. At the point where the Bahr{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Yusuf enters the Fayum, the district forms a plateau of moderate height,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descending towards the W. in three gradations towards the Birket Karun{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 181). Method of irrigation, see p. Iv.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In antiquity the Fayum was known as Te-she or 'lake-land' (Gr. Limne,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lake), from the great inland lake frequently mentioned and described by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek travellers and geographers under the name of Lake Moeris (from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt. me(r)-wer, muer, great canal), of which the last trace must be re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cognized in the present Birket Karun. At the most remote period the lake{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had a circumference of about 14UM. (Herodotus says 3600 stadia, i.e. 335 M.),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with an area of about 770 sq. M., and lay, according to recent calculations,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}70 ft. above the level of the Mediterranean; whereas the present lake is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}140 ft. below sea-level. The ancient Lake Moeris thus occupied almost the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entire basin of the Fayum, leaving uncovered only a narrow strip of fertile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land on the S., known as the 'Lake-land', on which stood the capital{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shetet (Crocodilopolis, p. 176), protected by embankments against inundation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Of the actual boundaries of the lake we can only say that on the N. it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stretched as far as the eminence with the desert temple discovered by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Schweinfurth, while on the S. the waters probably reached the neigh¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bourhood of Edwa, Bihamu, and Fidmin. As early as the Greek period,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly in the reign of Ptolemy Philadelphus, the lake-area was reduced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by means of embankments, until its total size approximated to that of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}modern Birket Karun. Attempts were made to reclaim land for agricultural{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purposes by draining the marshes; and the success that has attended{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these efforts is attested by the fertile fields and prosperous villages that{% endraw %}
