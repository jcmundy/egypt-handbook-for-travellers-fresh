{% raw %}or 'crocodile-town'. It never attained to any political importance. Ptol¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emy II. Philadelphus seems to have converted it into an essentially{% endraw %}

{% raw %}time of its greatest prosperity it had over 100,000 inhabitants.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}For a visit to the ruins, we. proceed due N. from the mosque of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kait Bey, following the trodden path through the extensive modern burial-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place. The embankments, like ramparts, to the right and left, are re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mains of Saltpetre Pans, such as were formerly used to extract the sebakh-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}salts from the rubbish-heaps of ruined towns. To the N. of that on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left lies a mound of rubbish known as K6m el-Kharydna, the highest part{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which, es-Sdga, encloses the ruins of some large building. During the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last twenty years important discoveries have repeatedly been made here of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}papyri, most of which are now in the Archduke Rainer's collection in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vienna, while others are in the museums of Berlin and other towns- Most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofthe papyri are Greek business documents (records, receipts, letters, etc.),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but some literary specimens have also been found, containing fragments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Homer, Euripides, Thucydides, etc. Others are written in Coptic,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Persian, Syriac, or Arabic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the railway-embankment, to the left, we now see the highest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mound, the K6m Fdris (65 ft.), the top of which commands a survey of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the whole of the Fayum. To the right (W.) are the black mound known{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as K6m en-Numshi, and the long K6m et-Taydra, where papyri have also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been discovered. More to theN., on the W. verge of the ruins, rises the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}K6m el-'Adddmeh ('bone-mound'), which was used in the 5-6th cent, after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christ as the burial-place of the poorer inhabitants. In the tombs here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous toys, well-preserved textile fabrics, etc., have been found.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We follow the path to the N. and presently find ourselves in a section{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the ruins, which, in Dr. Schweinfurth's word, 'to this day produces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the impression of a city but recently destroyed. The walls of the houses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still stand far and wide, but the narrow interlacing lanes, bewildering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in their ramifications and interrupted hy countless trenches and holes in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ground, render it impossible to obtain any clear idea of the general{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plan'. On the extreme N. edge of the ruins, partly projecting into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivated land, we may detect traces of the ancient principal temple,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was dedicated to Sobk. This existed as early as the 12th Dyn.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and was afterwards rebuilt by Ramses II. Within the rectangular girdle-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall, which was built of sun-dried bricks, lie fragments of an architrave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of red granite and isolated blocks of limestone bearing the names of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses II. and other kings. Most of the temple, however, was removed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the later inhabitants and incorporated in new buildings. Beside the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple lay originally the sacred pond in which the crocodile of Sobk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was kept.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now turn to the W. and reach the Bahr Tirsa Canal, on which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are several water-wheels. The large mounds of rubbish at the village of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Menshiyet 'Abdullah probably also belonged to the ancient city. We return{% endraw %}
{% raw %}southwards, skirting the canal and the edge of the cultivated ground. To{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right rises the large mound of K6m el-Bultiyeh ('mound of the Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carp'), on the S. side of which are the walls of an apparently fortified{% endraw %}
{% raw %}building, probably dating from the Arab period. Farther to the S. are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the K6m et-Taydra (see above), and the above-mentioned Saltpetre Pans.{% endraw %}
