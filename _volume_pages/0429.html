{% raw %}Fayum. HAWARA. 16. Route. 179{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esh-Sherki, i.e. river of the East. Skirting the E. bank of the stream{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for 1/2 hr. towards the N., we reach the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pyramid of Hawara, the tomb of Amenemhet III. This consists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of unburnt bricks of Nile mud mixed with straw, and, when its sides{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were perfect, covered an area about 115 yds. square. The limestone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}incrustation, however, had disappeared even in the Roman period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The nucleus of the structure is a natural mass of rock, 39 ft. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}height. The dilapidated summit is easily reached in a few minutes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a flight of well-worn steps. The entrance to the pyramid, on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the S. side, was discovered in 1889 by Prof. Flinders Petrie. An{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intricate series of passages in the interior leads to the tomb-chamber,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is 22 ft. long, 8 ft. wide, and 6 ft. high. Amenemhet III.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his daughter Ptahnofru were interred here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the S. the pyramid was adjoined by a large temple, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}main portion of which was probably dedicated to the manes of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenemhet. This edifice, however, which served as a quarry for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centuries after the Roman period, has completely vanished, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the exception of an extensive space strewn with small splinters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of stone and fragments of fine granite columns. Traces of its walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may also be recognized in the sloping banks of the Bahr Sela. There{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is no doubt that this edifice was the famous Labyrinth, of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient travellers speak with such unbounded admiration.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}For the best description we are indebted to Strabo, who visited the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Labyrinth in person. He says: 'There is also the Labyrinth here, a work{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as important as the Pyramids, adjoining which is the tomb of the king{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who built the Labyrinth. After advancing about 30-40 stadia beyond the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first entrance of the canal, there is a table-shaped surface, on which rise{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a small town and a vast palace, consisting of as many royal dwellings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as there were formerly nomes. There is also an equal number of halls,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bordered with columns and adjoining each other, all being in the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}row, and forming one building, like a long wall having the halls in front{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of it. The entrances to the halls are opposite the wall. In front of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrances are numerous long covered passages, intersecting each other and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thus forming such a winding path, that the ingress and egress to each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hall is not practicable to a stranger without a guide. It is a marvellous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fact that each of the ceilings of the chambers consists of a single stone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and also that the passages are covered in the same way with single slabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of extraordinary size, neither wood nor other building material having{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been employed. On ascending the roof, the height of which is incon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}siderable, as there is only one story, we observe a stone surface con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sisting of large slabs. Descending again, and looking into the halls, we may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}observe the whole series borne by twenty-seven monolithic columns. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls also are constructed of stones of similar size. At the end of this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structure, which is more than a stadium in length, is the tomb, consist¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing of a square pyramid, each side of which is four plethra (400 ft.) in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length, and of equal height. The deceased, who is buried here, is called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Imandes. It is also asserted that so many palaces were built, because it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the custom for all the nomes, represented by their magnates, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their priests and victims, to assemble here to offer sacrifice and gifts to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods, and to deliberate on the most important concerns. Each nome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then took possession of the hall destined for it.'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The remains of brick buildings, which still linger on this site and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were erroneously regarded hy Lepsius as remains ot the Labyrinth, date{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from a village of the Roman period.{% endraw %}
