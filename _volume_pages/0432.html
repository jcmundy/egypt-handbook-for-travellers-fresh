{% raw %}182 Route 16. KASR KARUN.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the desert, to the E. from the E. bank of the lake and to the N.W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Tamiyeh, rises the mound of K6m Ushim, covering the ruins of Ka-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ranis, a Greek town frequently mentioned in local history, with a temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Pnepheros and Petesuchos. — The mound Umm el-All, 7 M. to the E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofKaranis, marks the site of Bacchias, with a ruined Greek temple. Both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mounds were explored in 1896 by Messrs. Hogarth and Grenfell, at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}expense of the Egypt Exploration Fund.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ruins marking the site of Kasr Karun lie at the S.W. end of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Birket Karun. We land on the promontory of Khashm Khalil,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is overgrown with tamarisks and reeds. Ascending thence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}across the desert for about an hour, we reach the temple, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now 2'/4 M. from the lake, though it originally stood on its bank.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The fishermen object to pass the night here, being afraid ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beduins and the 'Afrit' (evil spirits).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kasr Karun is a fairly well preserved temple, of the late Pto¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lemaic period. The numerous traces of an ancient town that sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}round it are probably those of Dionysias, which was situated on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extreme W. verge of the Roman province of Egypt, at the beginning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the caravan route to the 'Small Oasis' (Bahrlyeh, p. 195). A cir¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cular foundation-wall indicates the site of an ancient cistern. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls of the temple consist of carefully hewn blocks of hard lime¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone. This temple, like almost all the shrines in the oases, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dedicated to the ram-headed Ammon-Khnum, as is proved by two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures of this deity standing at the highest part of the posterior wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the upper story of the open roof. The winged sun-disk occurs over{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each gateway in the building. There are no ancient inscriptions.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The temple is 20 yds. in width across the facade, and 29 yds. in length.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The entrance, facing the E., is approached by a lofty and carefully con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structed platform, 14 yds. in length, forming a fore-court. On the facade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the temple, to the right fN.) of the entrance-door, is a massive, semi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}circular projection, resembling the half of a huge column. On the lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}floor are the apartments of the temple which were dedicated to worship.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the first three Anterooms the ground slopes down towards the Sanctuary,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was divided into three small rooms at the back. The sanctuary is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}flanked by two narrow passages, each of which is adjoined by three rooms.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The anterooms also have adjacent chambers from which we may enter the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cellars, or ascend hy two flights of step' to the upper floor with its different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apartments, and thence to the roof, whence we obtain an extensive view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the remains of the ancient city, of the lake, and the desert. Over the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}doors leading into the second and third anterooms and into the sanctuary,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}instead of the ordinary concave cornice, there is a series of Uraeus snakes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the E. of the large temple are situated two smaller Roman temples,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in tolerable preservation, the larger of which, situated 300 paces from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the smaller, is not without interest. Its walls (18 ft. by 19 ft.) consist of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}good burnt bricks, and its substructures of solid stone. The cella ter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}minates in a niche resembling an apse; on each of the side-walls are two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}half-columns, which, as the fragments lying on the ground show, belong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Ionic order.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 8V2 M. to the E. of Kasr Karun are the ruins of Kasr el-Benat,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient Euhemeria, including the remains of a temple of Sukhos and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Isis. — Beside the village of Harit, 13/4 M. to the S.E. of this point, lie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ruins and the necropolis of the ancient Theadelphia; and 5 M. to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N.W. of Harit, almost due W. from Kasr Karun, are the ruins of the an¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient Philoteris, now known as Wadfa. All these places, which were founded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the early Ptolemies in the reclaimed bed of Lake Moeris, have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recently explored by the English travellers Grenfell and Hunt.{% endraw %}
