{% raw %}UPPER EGYPT.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Visitors to the temples and tombs of Upper Egypt and Nubia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}should be provided with a Genbhal Admission Ticket, which may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be obtained (price /IE) at the Museum of Gizeh (p. 78) or at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}any of the tourist-agents' offices in Cairo. Travellers who intend to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visit Luxor only may obtain tickets for 10s., valid for the ruins at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thebes alone.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ordinary traveller, desiring to visit only the principal points{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of interest, may ascend the valley of the Nile either by railway or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by steamboat. By Railway not less than 8 or 10 days are required{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for a visit to Abydos, Dendera, Thebes, Edfu, and Assuan. Those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who use the Steamboats, which spend 3 or 4 weeks on the voyage,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enjoy a much more thorough survey of these points; while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}voyage on the Nile has so many attractions of its own that even the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most hurried traveller should make a point of proceeding by river{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at least between Luxor and Assuan. All are strongly recommended{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to time their journey so as to arrive at Luxor 3 or 4 days before full{% endraw %}
{% raw %}moon; for moonlight adds a peculiar charm to a visit to the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there and at Assuan. It is as well for the independent traveller to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}avoid as far as possible coming into contact with the large parties{% endraw %}
{% raw %}organized by the tourist-agents, for circumstances are apt to arise in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which he is pushed to the wall, without any redress.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most attractive, but also the most expensive, method of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ascending the Nile is by Dahabiyeh (p. 187). Vessels with 4-25{% endraw %}
{% raw %}berths may be hired at Cairo. The steam-dahabiyehs or steam-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}launches are not much slower than the large tourist-steamers, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a sailing dahabiyeh, which has to be towed upstream when the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wind is unfavourable, not less than 2 or 3 months must be allowed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the voyage to Assuan and back.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Railway. Quitting Cairo by the night-express (sleeping-cars),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we reach Belianeh (p. 217) next morning, and ride thence on donkey-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}back to Abydos (p. 218). Provisions for this expedition must be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brought from Cairo. We have sufficient time to explore the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Abydos before catching the evening-train for Luxor (p. 233). At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}least four days should be devoted to an inspection of the temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and tombs at Thebes. On leaving Luxor we may either stop over a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}train at Edfu (p. 314) or proceed straight through to Assuan (p. 327),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where not less than two days should be spent. Edfu, however, may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}equally well be visited on the return-journey, as a night must in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}any case be spent at Luxor. We leave Luxor on our return by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fiTst morning-train and break the journey at Keneh (p. 225), whence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we ride to the temple of Dendera (p. 226). Provisions must be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brought from Luxor for this expedition. From Keneh we go on to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cairo by the North express.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The railway may be utilized for other excursions by travellers who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are to some extent familiar with the customs and language of the country.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The nights may be spent in the station waiting-rooms (with permission{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the station-master) or at the houses of the local prefects ('Omdeh).{% endraw %}
