{% raw %}The Mastabas of Meidum lie to the N. and E. of the pyramid. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were the tombs of the courtiers and officials of Snofru, and are among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the oldest monuments of the kind in the world. The two most important{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(to the N.) are those of the judge and vizier Nefermaat and his wife Yetet,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and of Prince Rahotep and his wife Nofrel. Their inner chambers, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were inaccessible in antiquity and have been again built up after explor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ation, were embellished with admirably executed representations and in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scriptions. The tomb of Rahotep is decorated with painted bas-reliefs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while in that of Nefermaat some of the scenes are painted upon stucco{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(e.g. the geese, now in the Museum at Gizeh, p. 79), while others are en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graved in outline and the inner surfaces filled up with colours. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Numerous graves of the humbler ranks of society have also been dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}covered at Meidum. In these the bodies were not interred in the usual{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manner, at full length, but lying on their left side, facing the E , and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with knees drawn up.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right bank, opposite Rikka and about iy2 M. from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river, lies the hamlet of Atfih, with some mounds of earth and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}de'bris representing the ancient Aphroditopolis, named after Hathor-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Aphrodite who was worshipped here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Egyptian name of the town was Tep-ye or Per Hathor nebt Tep-ye,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}i.e. 'House of Hathor, mistress of Tep-ye', whence the shortened Coptic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Petpeh and Arabic Atfih. Strabo states that a white cow, sacred to Hathor,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was worshipped here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Christian period (ca. 310 A.D.) Aphroditopolis gained some cel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ebrity from St. Anthony, who fixed his hermitage in the mountains to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E. of the town, beside a well and a group of palms. So many pilgrims{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of every class, age, and sex sought out the holy man, that a regular post¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing route, with relays of camels, was laid out across the desert.. St. An¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thony, however, fled from his admirers and buried himself deeper in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mountains. But while he thus shook off his earthly visitants, he could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not so easily escape those extraordinary tempters from spirit-land, at which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Callot has taught us to smile, though to St. Anthony himself, as well as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to St. Hilarion and other similarly persecuted anchorites, the contest was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of bitter earnest.{% endraw %}
