{% raw %}Benihasan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Donkeys (with good saddles) are in waiting at the landing-place of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the steamers, for the excursion to the Speos Artemidos and the Rock Tombs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(there and hack 3-4 hrs.; ls.-is. 6d.). — Travellers ascending the river in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a dahabiyeh should visit the Rock Tombs first, those descending should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visit the Speos first, in each ease sending the dahabiyeh on to meet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them. — For travellers by railway the most convenient stations are, Minyeh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Abu Kerkds (p. 190).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The village of Benihasan was founded towards the end of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}18th cent, by the inhabitants of the present Old Benihasan (see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}below), who wished a wider space for cultivation near their abode. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The route to the Speos Artemidos (V2 hr's. ride) leads to the E., at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first through fields, then along an embankment on the edge of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert, in which is an Arab cemetery. In the vicinity is the cats'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graveyard, in which the cats sacred to Pakht, patron-goddess of this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}region, were interred. Farther to the E. we reach the mouth of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wadi or ravine, with quarries of ancient date. On the right (S.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}side of the ravine, about 600 ft. from its mouth, lies the temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The rock-temple of the goddess Pakht, called Speos Artemidos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Greeks, is known to the Arabs as Stabl 'Antar (Antar's stable),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after an ancient hero. It consists of a vestibule and of an inner{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chamber connected with the vestibule by a corridor. It was built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the joint reign of Queen Makere and King Thutmosis III.; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}latter afterwards erased the names and representations of his sister{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 278), and Sethos I. inserted his own names in the blanks.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Over the Entrance to the temple is a long inscription in praise of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reign of Makere. Of the eight pillars which supported the Vestibule only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}three now remain; these hear on their sides the names of Thutmosis III.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Sethos I. (originally Makere). Rear Wall. To the left of the door,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sethos I. between Ammon-Re (enthroned) and the lion-headed Pakht;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thoth delivering a speech to the nine great gods of Karnak and to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods of "Upper and Lower Egypt. To the right of the door are three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs: Sethos sacrificing to Pakht; Sethos receiving from Pakht the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hieroglyphics of the word 'life', hanging from two sceptres ; Sethos blessed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Thout. To the left in the Coeeidoe the king is represented offering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wine to Hathor; to the right, he offers her a cynoeephalus. In the rear-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall of the Innee Chamber is a niche intended for a statue ofthe goddess.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the W. (left) is a second grotto, on the outside of which are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the cartouches of Alexander II., son of Roxana, and six scenes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representing the king in the company ofthe gods. The interior, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was supported by pillars, is now in ruins; perhaps it was never com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pleted. In the vicinity are several rock-tombs of the New Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the form of rectangular chambers, with deep shafts.{% endraw %}
