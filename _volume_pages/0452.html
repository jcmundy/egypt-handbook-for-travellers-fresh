{% raw %}198 Route 18. BENIHASAN. From Cairo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt, not only on account of their remarkable architectural fea¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures, but also for the manifold representations of scenes from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}domestic life of the early Egyptians.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The tombs, 39 in all, are arranged in a row in the rocks, and are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now distinguished by conspicuous red numbers. The best examples are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}secured by iron doors against the vandalism of the natives, from which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they had previously suffered. The tombs were most recently carefully{% endraw %}
{% raw %}examined and cleared of rubbish by Mr. Percy E. Newberry, at the cost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Egypt Exploration Fund. Travellers whose time is limited may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}content themselves with a visit to the four chief tombs (Nos. 17, 15, 3, 2).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}For remarks on the construction of the tombs, see pp. 287, 288.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The path that ascends to the tombs brings us first to No. 32.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Here we turn to the N. and proceed to —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb 17, which belonged to Kheti, son of Baket and nomarch{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the gazelle-nome (11th Dyn.). The facade is simple. We enter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Rock Chamber, the roof of which was originally borne by six{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lotus-columns with closed bud-capitals, though only two, with well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preserved colouring, are now standing. Left Wall (N.). In the top{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rows is a hunt in the desert, in the lower rows, male and female{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dancers; the statue of the deceased being borne to its place, car¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}penters, etc. Rear Wall (E.). Above are wrestlers in various atti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tudes; below, military scenes, attack on a fortress. Right WallfS.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From left to right: the deceased and his wife ; the deceased accom¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}panied by his fan-bearer, sandal-bearer, two dwarfs, etc.; the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}receiving offerings (notice the barn on the right). The scenes on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrance-wall are in poor preservation. — Farther to the N., at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}end of an ancient path ascending from the plain, is —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb 15, belonging to Baket, nomarch of the gazelle-nome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(11th Dyn.). The two columns which supported the roof of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rectangular chamber have been destroyed. In the S.E. angle is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}small serdab (p. cxliv). Left Wall (N.). Above, Hunting in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert; barbers, washermen, painters, etc. Below, the deceased and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his wife, with four rows of women spinning and weaving, female{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dancers, girls playing at ball; herdsmen bringing animals for sacri¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fice to the dead; goldsmiths ; fishing; various birds, with their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}names inscribed beside them. Rear Wall (EJ. Above, wrestlers;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}below, military scenes (resembling those in Tomb 17). Right Wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(S.). The deceased, in front of whom are several rows of men draw¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing a shrine containing a statue of the dead; in front are female{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dancers and attendants bearing ornaments, etc., for the statue;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}peasants bringing their flocks and herds; peasants forcibly brought{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to testify as to taxes, while scribes note down the amounts; potters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with wheels; men carrying slaughtered birds; men gambling.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb 3 is that of Khnemhotep, the son of Neheri, a scion of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}princely family with hereditary jurisdiction over the gazelle-nome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and over the E. districts, the capital of which was Monet-Khufu.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khnemhotep was invested by King Amenemhet II. with the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}districts and married a daughter of the governor of the dog-norne{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Cynopolis, p. 195), which was inherited by a son of this marriage.{% endraw %}
