{% raw %}200 Route 18. BENIHASAN. From Cairo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb 2, which belonged to Ameni-em-het, also a nomarch of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the gazelle-nome in the reign of Usertesen I. In the Vestibule are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two fine octagonal columns, bearing a fiat vault hewn out of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rock. On the door-posts and lintel are prayers for the dead and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the titles of Ameni. Inside the door, to the right and left, is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}long inscription dated in the 43rd year of Usertesen I., extolling{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the deeds of Ameni in several military campaigns and the benefits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conferred by him upon his province. — Foursixteen-edged columns,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with narrow fluting (so-calledProto-Doric columns, p. cxxxiii),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}support the roof of the Tomb Chamber, which has three sections.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The wall-paintings closely resemble those in the tomb of Khnem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hotep, but are still more faded. On the Entrance Wall, to the left, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shoemakers, carpenters, goldsmiths, potters, and other handicrafts¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}men, and agricultural scenes. Left Wall(N.). At the top, hunting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the desert; in the second row, transporting the statue and cer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emonial dances; below, to the right, the deceased receiving tribute{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from his estates ; in the two lowest rows, Ameni's estate-office. Rear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wall. Wrestlers and military scenes; in the lower row, the corpse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being conveyed to the sacred tomb at Abydos. In the Tear-wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opens a recess containing the statues (much dilapidated) of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased and his wife and mother. Right Wall (S.). To the left the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased is seated at table with sacrificial gifts heaped before him ;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priests and servants bring food and other offerings for the dead;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}below, the slaughtering and cutting up of sacrificial animals. To{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right is Hetpet, wife of Ameni, also seated at table and receiv¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing sacrificial gifts.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}If time permit, some of the other tombs also should be visited, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most interesting being the following: Tomb 4, that of Khnemhotep, son of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Khnemhotep buried in Tomb 3. In the vestibule stands a 'Proto-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Doric' column; the tomb-chamber was unfinished. — Tomb 5, unfinished.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Tomb 14, of Khnemhotep, governor of a province under Amenemhet I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the tomb-chamber are two plant-columns (unfortunately broken); the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall-paintings are interesting but sadly faded. On the rear-wall appear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}soldiers and a caravan of Libyans, with their wives and children and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}herds, who visited the province of the deceased ; the men are distinguished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the ostrich-feathers in their hair, the women carry their children in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}baskets on their backs. — Tomb 18, though unfinished, is interesting as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the process of hollowing out the tomb-chamber may be traced. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pavement in the front of the chamber is not fully excavated; and at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the back are ten lotus-columns with closed bud-capitals, of which five{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still unfinished remain. — Tomb 21, of Nakht, governor of the gazelle-nome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the 12th Dyn., resembles No. 15 (p. 198) in its arrangement. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb 23, of Neternakht, governor of the E. districts, with uninteresting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall-paintings ; on the E. wall is a Coptic inscription. — Tomb 27, of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Remushenti, governor of the gazelle-nome. — Tomb 28, with two columns,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was converted into a church in the Christian period. —■ Tomb 29, of Baket,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nomarch of the gazelle-nome. The door to this tomb and the entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Tomb 30 were broken by the Copts. The wall-paintings are in com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paratively good preservation, but offer no novel point of interest; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dwarfs following the deceased, on the W. half ofthe S wall, may perhaps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be mentioned. — Tomb 33, of Baket, prince of the gazelle-nome, son of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Baket interred in No. 29; several wall-paintings. — Tombs 34-39 were left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfinished.{% endraw %}
