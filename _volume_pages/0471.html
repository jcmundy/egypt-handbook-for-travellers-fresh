{% raw %}At the foot of the hill, beside the neat slaughter-house, we dismount{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and follow the good path which leads to the most interesting tombs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The tombs are closed with iron gates; the keeper lives beside the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slaughter-house.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We first reach a Large Rock Tomb, which belonged to Hap-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}zefa'i, prince of the nome in the reign of Usertesen I. The Arabs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}call it Stabl 'Antar, or the stable of Antar, a hero of tradition{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(comp. their name for the Speos Artemidos at Benihasan, p. 197).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Entering the tomb we first find ourselves in a vaulted Passage, on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left wall of which is the deceased, with a long and now scarcely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}legible inscription in front of him. A doorway, with a figure of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased holding a staff, on each side, leads hence to the Main Chamber.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right half of 1he Entrance Wall is a long inscription containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the text of Ten Contracts concluded between the deceased and various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priesthoods of his native city to secure the proper sacrificial offerings to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself and to his statues in his tomb and in the temple, and to provide{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the performance of other ceremonies. The corresponding inscription{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the left side of the same wall contains addresses to visitors to the tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and an account of the merits of the deceased. A door between two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recesses in the rear-wall admits us to a second vaulted passage, leading{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to a Second Room with three recesses. On the rear-wall of the central{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recess appears the deceased, with three women carrying lotus - flowers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before him; on the side-wall he is shown at table, while three rows of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priests and servants bring gifts to him or perform sacred ceremonies.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The left recess leads to the mummy-shaft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *View from this tomb is very fine. The fertile land and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile enclosed by the limestone hills of Libya and the Arabian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mountains in the distance form a quiet but by no means mono¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tonous setting for the beautiful town of Assiut, with its eleven mina¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rets and its environment of palm-gardens. The view is still grander{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the higher tombs. Here there is a row of three tombs close to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each other, dating from the obscure period before the Middle Em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pire. The northernmost has been destroyed. The second is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kahf el-'Asdkir, or Soldiers' Tomb, so named from the rows of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}warriors armed with spears and large shields on its S. wall. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right side of the vestibule appear Kheti, the owner of the tomb,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his wife Tef-yeb, with a long and partly effaced inscription,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}referring to the otherwise little-known King Meri-ke-re. Only a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}single column is left standing in the Main Chamber, in the Tear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall of which is a recess for the statue ofthe deceased. — A passage{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been made from this tomb to that adjoining it on the S., which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belonged to Tef-yeb, a prince of the nome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The large Arab Cemetery, which stretches across the plain to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N. of the hill of tombs, contains tasteful modern tombs, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its palms presents a much less gloomy impression than most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other bare Egyptian cemeteries.{% endraw %}
