{% raw %}The voyage from Assiut to Akhmim leads through an extremely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fertile and well-cultivated district. Well-tilled fields, broader on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. than on the E., adjoin both banks of the river, and are shaded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by fine palms and Nile acacias, especially near the riverside villages.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Here, as in most of Egypt, large quantities of pigeons are kept by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the peasants, chiefly for the sake of their droppings, which form the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only manure used in the fields, the dung of the cattle being dried{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and used as fuel. Large pigeon-houses, not unlike pylons, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visible in all the villages, and huge flocks of pigeons are seen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wheeling in the air or settling like a dark cloud on the fields. Most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these pigeons are of the common grey species, and attain a con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}siderable size, but many pretty little reddish-grey turtle-doves are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also seen. The pigeons really consume more than they produce, so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that their encouragement by the fellahin is rightly regarded as a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}serious mistake in their husbandry.{% endraw %}
