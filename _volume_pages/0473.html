{% raw %}31'/2M. Kau el-Gharb(W. bank) is opposite Kau el-Kebir, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lies in the plain on the E. bank and is surrounded by a ring of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills, containing grottoes with sculptures and quarries with demotic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscriptions. Stamped bricks found in the mounds of dc'bris be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}longed to buildings of the 18th Dynasty. The name Kau recalls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient Egyptian name of the town Tu-Kow (Coptic Tkow);{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Greeks named it Antaeopolis, in honour of the remarkable deity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worshipped here, whom they identified with Antaeus (p. cxxv).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}According to the myth, Antaeus was a Libyan king of immense strength,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who was in the habit of wrestling with all visitors to his dominions and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of slaying thuse whom he vanquished, in order to build a temple to his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}father Poseidon with their skulls. Hercules came to try conclusions with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him, and after overthrowing him in a wrestling-match, slew him. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}According to Diodorus the final struggle betwixt Horus and Typhon (Set){% endraw %}
{% raw %}took place here. In the Roman period Antaeopolis was the capital of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Antseopolitan nome. The last remains of an imposing temple, dedicated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here by Ptolemy Philometor to Antaeus and restored by Marcus Aurelius{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his colleague Verus (164 A.D.), were swept away by the Nile in 1821. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In a deep grotto-like rmarry in the N.E. angle of the hill behind Kau are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two pillars bearing two remarkable paintings of the god Antaeus and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}goddess Nephthys.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}mountains. The convent, also named Der Anba ShenUda after its founder,{% endraw %}
