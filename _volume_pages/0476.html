{% raw %}20. Abydos.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ordinary traveller, especially when he has at his disposal only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 8 hrs. allowed by the steamer, will confine himself to the Temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sethos I. and the sadly dilapidated Temple of Ramses II., with possibly a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visit to the old fortress of ShUnet ez-Zebib. The other antiquities are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}uninteresting. — Fair donkeys, with European saddles, may be obtained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the railway-station of Belianeh (p. 191; 3-4«.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abydos lies about 8^2 M. from Belianeh, a ride of li/2 hr. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}track crosses several canals, passes through the hamlet of el-Hegs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traverses a fertile district dotted with numerous villages, and finally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads over part of the Libyan Desert. Fine view of the mountain-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chain running towards the Nile. The ancient Abydos, the sacred{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city of the Egyptians, lay on the verge of the arable land, and ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tended from'Ara&a or 'Ardbat el-Madfuneh (i.e. 'buried 'Araba') to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Kherbeh (p. 224).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abydos (Egypt. Abotu) was one of the most ancient cities in Egypt and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}played an important role in the religion of the country. For here was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the famous grave of Osiris, in which the head of the god was traditionally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}believed to be buried in a casket. Just as the Shi'ite Mohammedan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cherishes no dearer wish than to be buried near the tomb of Hosen of Ker-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bela(p. 42), so the pious Egyptian, from the days of the Ancient Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desired no better fortune than to have his corpse carried to Abydos, there{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to find its last abode beside the tomb of Osiris. Those who were unable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to do this, or who had built tombs elsewhere, often caused their mummies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be brought temporarily to Abydos, to receive the desired consecration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and to spend some time at least with Osiris. Many contented themselves{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with merely erecting a memorial stone in the necropolis, thereby assuring{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to themselves the favour of Osiris, the lord of the underworld. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief god of Abydos was of course Osiris; but his wife Isis, his son Horus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and, under the New Empire, Ptah, Harmachis, and Ammon, with other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deities, were also worshipped there. — Strabo gives an interesting account{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Abydos: 'Above it (Ptolema'is) lies Abydos, the site of the Memnonium,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a wonderful palace of stone, built in the manner of the Labyrinth, only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat less elaborate in its complexity. Below the Memnonium is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spring, reached by passages with low vaults consisting of a single stone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and distinguished for their extent and mode of construction. This spring{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is connected with the Nile by a canal, which flows through a grove of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian thorn-acacias, sacred to Apollo. Abydos seems once to have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a large city, second only to Thebes, but now it is a small place, etc.'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This spring may perhaps have been a Nilometer. Ammianus Marcellinus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}speaks of the oracle of the god Bes, which flourished here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most important part of ancient Abydos was its extensive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Necropolis, situated in the desert. Four distinct sections are clearly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traceable. In the southernmost, beside 'Araba, are tombs of the New{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire and the temples of Sethos and Ramses. To the N. of this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rises a hill, with graves dating from the close of the Ancient Empire.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Still farther to the N., between the shrine of Osiris and the fortress{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Shimet ez-Zebib (p. 224), are the tombs of the Middle Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}many in the form of small brick-pyramids. Here also are found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graves of the 18-20th Dyn. and of the later period. Finally, in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hill of Umm el-Ga'ab, to the W., are the tombs of the kings of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}earliest dynasties. — The chief centre of interest is the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Temple of Sethos I., the Memnonium of Strabo. This won¬{% endraw %}
