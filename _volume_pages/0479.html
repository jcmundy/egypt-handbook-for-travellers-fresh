{% raw %}while a harder variety has been selected for the columns, archi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traves, door-posts, and other burden-bearing portions. The *Reliefs,{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The ground-plan differs materially from that of other great Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temples. Instead of one sanctuary, it has seven, dedicated to Osiris, Isis,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Horus, Ptah, Harmachis, Ammon, and the deified king; and as each of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these had a special cult, the entire front portion of the temple is divided{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into seven parts, each with its separate gateway and portals. The chambers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}behind the sanctuaries are not arranged behind each other as in other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temples, but side by side. Another remarkable peculiarity consists in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wing, containing various halls, chambers, etc., which stands at right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}angles with the main building (p. 222).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We enter the temple from the N.E. The first pylon is in ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the first court is occupied by modern huts. The Second Court,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which opens to the S. on the temple proper, is in better preservation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The sons and daughters of Ramses II. were represented on the wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the inner side of the pylon, but the figures and inscriptions have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been almost effaced. On the right and left walls appears Ramses II.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacrificing to different gods; on each side are steles of Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the back of the court a shallow flight of steps ascends to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}original vestibule of the temple proper, which is supported by 12{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns of limestone and originally had seven doors in its rear-wall.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the wall, to the left of the main entrance, is a Large Inscription{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 95 vertical lines, in which Ramses II. describes in florid language{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the completion of the temple and his filial piety towards Sethos.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the adjoining relief Ramses is shown presenting an image of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}goddess Maat to a triad consisting of Osiris, Isis, and his father{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sethos I., who takes the place of Horus. On the wall are other re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presentations of Ramses in presence of the gods. — The seven orig¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inal doors corresponded to the seven sanctuaries of the temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Processions in honour of the king seem to have entered by the door{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the extreme left; the next served for processions to Ptah, the third{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for Harmachis, the fourth for Ammon, the fifth for Osiris, the.sixth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for Isis, and the seventh for Horus. Ramses, however, walled up{% endraw %}
{% raw %}six of these doors, leaving the central one alone as the main entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The present entrance is by the door to the right of the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}main entrance. We first enter the First Hypostyle Hall, which is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 54 yds. wide by 12 deep. The roof, part of which has fallen in, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supported by 24 columns, with capitals in the form of papyrns buds.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The columns are so arranged that two pairs stand on each side of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the five central processional aisles, while the two outermost aisles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are each flanked on one side by the walls of the temple. The re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presentations on the shafts of the columns represent the king before{% endraw %}
