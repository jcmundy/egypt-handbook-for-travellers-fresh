{% raw %}Near the village of El-Kherbeh, about i/4 M. to the N. ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Ramses II., are the ruins of the ancient city and of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shrine of Osiris, dating back to the 12th Dynasty. — To the S. of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the latter are scanty traces of a second temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther to the W. lies a small fort, surrounded by two walls (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}outer wall the lower), named Shunet ez-Zeblb, which probably served{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a place of defence against the incursions of the Beduins from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A Coptic Convent (more like a village) to the N.E. of this point, dating{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the year 1306 of the Coptic era, scarcely repays a visit.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The rubbish-mounds at the foot of the hills, 1 M. to the S.W. of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of Ramses II., called by the Arabs Umm el-Ga'ab ('mother of pots'),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contain tombs of kings of the 1st and 2nd Egyptian dynasties, including{% endraw %}
{% raw %}those of Vsaphais and Miebis (1st Dyn.). They were explored by Amelineau{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Flinders Petrie, but there is now practically nothing to be seen.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Near the village of Ghabat, to the S. of Abydos, is an ancient quarry.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Above Belianeh the course of the Nile lies almost due E. and W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Dum Palm (Hyphaena thebaica) becomes more and more com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mon and increases in size and beauty as we travel southwards (comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. lix). About 4 M. from the S. bank lies Samhiid, on ancient rubbish-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mounds. The Arabian Mts. approach close to the river.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}28 M. Nag' Hamadi, on the W. bank, a town of 4400 inhab., is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a railway-station (p. 191). A large railway-bridge crosses the river{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here (see p. 191).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}31 M. Hou (W. bank), at one of the sharpest bends in the stream,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a large but miserable-looking village. It was the home of Shekh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Selim, who died about 30 years ago, at a very advanced age, after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sitting stark naked for 53 years on the bank of the Nile at the spot{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now marked by his tomb. He was regarded by pious Moslems with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great honour and was deemed to possess great powers in helping{% endraw %}
{% raw %}navigation. In the neighbourhood are the scanty ruins of the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Diospolis Parva.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}34 M. Kasr es-Sayad (E. bank), a steamboat-station, is probably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient Khenoboskion. Close to the bank is a steam-engine for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}raising water.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 4!/2 M. to the E., near the village of Isba, among the Arabian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills, are the Tombs of Princes of the Seventh Nome of Upper Egypt under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 6th Dynasty. Donkeys may be obtained through the Shekh el-heled.{% endraw %}
