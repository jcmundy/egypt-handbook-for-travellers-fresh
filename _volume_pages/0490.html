{% raw %}22. Dendera.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A hasty visit to the temple at Dendera may be accomplished in 3 hrs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Well-equipped donkeys stand in readiness on the hank opposite Keneh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(there and back 4 pias., fee 1 pias.). The visitor should not fail to he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}provided with candles or (better still) a magnesium lamp for exploring the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crypts and other parts of the temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The distance from the landing-place to the temple (about 2i/2M.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}is easily accomplished on donkey-back in i/2 hr- The route follows{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile to the N. through palm-trees, then proceeds to the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through well-tilled fields to the conspicuous mound of rubbish,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marking the site of the ruins.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dendera, the Tentyra of the Greeks, is one of the most ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and most famous cities of Egypt, and was the capital of the sixth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nome of Upper Egypt. Its ancient name was Enet, or in its fuller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form, Enet-te-ntore, 'Enet of the Goddess' (i.e. of Hathor), of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Greek and modern Arabic names are corruptions. Enet was the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief seat of the worship of Hathor (the Egyptian Aphrodite), god¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dess of love and joy. Along with her husband, the hawk-headed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Horus of Edfu, and her son, the youthful EM or Har-sem-tewe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}('Horus, uniter of both lands'; Gr. Harsomtus), she was worshipped{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the magnificent temple, which remains here to this day. Her{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief fe-tival synchronized with the great Feast of the New Year.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The gate, to which our road leads and where the cards of ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mission (see p. 184) are shown, forms the N. termination of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall of Nile bricks enclosing the temple. The total enclosure is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}317 yds. long and 306 yds. wide, and besides the large Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Hathor contains a small Sanctuary of Isis and a so-called Birth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}House (p. 231). The N. Gate was built under the Emp. Domitian,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who is here named Germanicus. On the side next the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appear the names of Nerva and Trajanus, also with the epithet of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Germanicus (Trajan is here also called Dacicus). There is another{% endraw %}
{% raw %}similar gate on the E. side ; while beyond the temple-precincts, also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the E., is a third gate, dating from the Roman imperial epoch.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the N. gate a passage between modern brick walls leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}**Temple of Hathor, which, though now deeply sunk in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accumulated rubbish of centuries, is in better preservation than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}any other large ancient Egyptian temple except those of Edfu and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Philae. It was built in the first century B.C. during the reigns of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the later Ptolemies and of Augustus; but it occupies the site of an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}older edifice, going back traditionally to the earliest period of Egyp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tian history, and added to or altered not only by the kings of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}12th Dyn., but also by the great monarchs of the New Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}such as Thutmosis III., Ramses II., and Ramses III. The decoration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the temple-walls with reliefs took place at a still more recent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}date. In accordance with the plan of other temples (comp. PI. of{% endraw %}
