{% raw %}232 Route 23. KUS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman period was one of the chief emporia for the wares of Arabia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and India. The town stood under the protection of the ithyphallic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}harvest-god Min (Pan), who was also the patron of travellers in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desert. During the great rebellion in Upper Egypt under Diocle¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tian (292 A.D.) Koptos was besieged and destroyed, but quickly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recovered from the blow. Down to the time of the Khalifs it re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mained a populous and thriving trading-town. To the S.E. of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}modern town lay the ancient city, with a temple of Min, the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which were excavated in 1894 by Flinders Petrie.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At Koptos the great caravan-routes through the Arabian Desert to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the seaports on the Red Sea quitted the Nile valley. The chief goals of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the caravans were the Sinaitic peninsula and the land of Punt (Pwenet),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated about the modern Somali Coast, which yielded incense, ivory,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ebony, panther-skins, etc., and was regarded by the Egyptians as a land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of fabulous wonders, like India. Other caravans made for the Wddi Ham-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indmdt in the desert, which produced a hard stone much prized by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptians, who used it largely for sculptures (comp. p. 346). At a later{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period the caravan-trade was diverted to the routes via Kus and finally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to those via, Keneh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1972 M. (E. bank) Kus (railway-station, p. 192), a town of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}14,200 inhab., occupies the site of the ancientApollonopoiis Parva,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where the god Haroeris was worshipped. According to Abulfeda{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1331) this town, now entirely vanished, was second in size only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Fostat (Cairo), and was the chief centre ofthe Arabian trade.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A few stones with fragmentary inscriptions have been built into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}houses of the town; and the mosque contains a basin formed of a single{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone, with the name of Ptolemy Philadelphus upon it. A pylon, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood here 30 years ago, has now disappeared.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the W bank, opposite Kus, lies Tukh (et-Ttik), inland from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, on the edge ofthe desert, are the ruins of Ombos (excavated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Flinders Petrie in 1895), not to be confounded with the town of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that name to the S. of Gebel Silsileh (p. 323). Set was the guardian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deity of this town. In the neighbourhood are extensive cemeteries{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dating from the period of the first Egyptian dynasties. — Near Shenhur{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(E. bank), 3 M. to the S. of Kus, Prisse d'Avennes discovered the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ruins of a small temple of Isis. To the E. passes the canal of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shenhur, which begins above Thebes and extends N. to Keneh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}22i/2m. (W. bank) Nakadeh, a town of 6800inhab. (4500 Copts),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with post and telegraph offices, a Coptic and a Roman Catholic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church, presents a picturesque appearance from the river. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller who lands here near sunset on a Sunday or festival will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be pleasantly surprised to hear the sound of church-bells.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N. of Nakadeh the Tomb of Menes, the first Egyptian King{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. lxxv), was discovered by De Morgan in lb97; it is a large mastaba of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brick, now much damaged.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the edge of the desert, between Nakadeh and Kamuleh (see p. 233),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lie four ancient Coptic Convents, said to date from the time of the Em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}press Helena. Der el-Meldk, the largest, is built of crude bricks and con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tains four connected churches, of which the largest is dedicated to St.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Michael. The convent, which has 28 domes, is now unoccupied, and is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}used for divine service only on certain festivals by the clergy of Nakadeh. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The other convents are those of Es-Salib (near Denfik), Mari Girgis, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mari Bokli (St. Victor). The last, with frescoes in its domes, is the oldest.{% endraw %}
