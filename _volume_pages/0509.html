{% raw %}el-Medineh. — 3rd day. Ramesseum; Tombs of Shekh 'Abd el-Kurna{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terrace-temple of Der el-bahri; el-Asasif; Drah Abu'l Neggah. — 4th day.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Sethos I. at Kurna; Tombs of the Kings, returning over the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ridge. — 5th day. Second visit to Karnak; visit to the various side-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temples and pylons; excursion to Medamut (p. 259), if desired.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sport. Sportsmen may have an opportunity of shooting a jackal, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}best time and place being at and after sunset near Biban el-Muluk or the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramesseum. An experienced hunter is to be found at the Luxor Hotel.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hyaenas are sometimes shot on the Karnak side. In March numerous quail{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are found here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On each side of the Nile, here interrupted by three islands,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stretches a wide belt of fertile land, bounded both on the E. and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. by ranges of hills, overtopped by finely shaped peaks, especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the E., where the ridge retires farther from the stream than on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W. The verdant crops and palms which everywhere cheer the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller as soon as he has quitted the desert, the splendid hues that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinge the valley every morning and evening, the brilliant, unclouded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sunshine that bathes every object even in the winter-season, lend{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the site of ancient Thebes the appearance of a wonderland, richly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}endowed with the gifts of never-failing fertility. Most of the ruined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temples are situated in the level district and are reached by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}waters of the Nile when the inundations are at their highest: while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the tombs are hewn in the flanks of the hills, where their dark open¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ings are so numerous, that the E. slope of the Libyan range might{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be aptly compared to a piece of cork or to a honeycomb.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the right (E.) bank lies Luxor, now a town of 11,000 inhab.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(over 2600 Copts), the chief tourist-centre in Upper Egypt. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name is derived from the Arabic el-Kusilr (pi. of el-Kaif) and means{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'the castles', having reference to the extensive temple within which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of the place was built. Near the temple traces of an antique{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structure on the river-bank may still be seen, though it is gradually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being washed away by the annual inundations. To the N. of Luxor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extend the immense ruins of Karnak, formerly connected with it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the streets of the city. Even under the Pharaohs the Temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ammon, the chief god of Thebes, was considered the most striking{% endraw %}
{% raw %}creation of an age peculiarly famous for architectural achievements.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To this day no other building in the world can match its dimensions.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther to the N. is another extensive temple-site at Medamut{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 260), which must be regarded as occupying the site of a suburb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Thebes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the left (W.) bank was the Necropolis, with vaults in the rock{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and tombs on the desert-soil, adjoined by many mortuary temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and dwelling-houses. Even in antiquity the Tombs of the Kings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 262) were reckoned among the chief sights of ancient Thebes.{% endraw %}
