{% raw %}240 Route 24. THEBES (E. BANK). 1. Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The exterior walls of the pylons are adorned with representations{% endraw %}
{% raw %}referring to the campaign against the Hittites, which Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carried on in Syria in the 5th year of his reign. The Reliefs en creux{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have suffered severely from the hand of time and at several places{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are almost obliterated. On the Right ( W.) Tower we see the life and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}business of the Egyptian camp; to the left the king on his throne{% endraw %}
{% raw %}holds a council of war with his princes, in the middle is the camp,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fortified by the shields of the soldiers arranged side by side, to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right the king in his chariot dashes into the fray. The scenes on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Left (E.) Tower plunge us into the battle; the king in his chariot{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dashes against his foes who have surrounded him, and launches his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arrows against them. The field is covered with dead and wounded,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while the Hittites flee in wild confusion to the fortress of Kadesh,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whence fresh troops issue. Farther to the left Kadesh, girt with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}water, appears, with the defenders on the battlements. Remote{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the battle-field, to the extreme left, the prince of the Hittites{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stands in his chariot, surrounded by his guards, and 'fears before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his majesty'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Below the reliefs on the W. tower is a long poetical description{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the battle of Kadesh, inscribed in vertical lines. This is now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally.known as the 'Epic of Pentaur'. It is continued on the E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tower, where, however, the text is still partly concealed by rubbish.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the front of each tower of the pylon are two large vertical{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grooves for the reception of flag-staffs, and above them are large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}square apertures, which served both to receive the braces securing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the flag-staffs and to admit light and air to the interior.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The portal between the towers is in a very ruinous condition.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The reliefs represent Ramses II. in presence of the chief deities of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temple. The carvings, in tolerably high relief, in the doorway{% endraw %}
{% raw %}date from the reign ofthe Ethiopian king Shabako.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the principal pylon (though not accessible from this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side) lies the great Court of Ramses II. (PI. A), which was entirely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded by a double tow of papyrus-columns (74 in all), with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bud-capitals and smooth shafts. It measures 185 ft. in length and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}167 ft. in breadth, but owing to the presence of a mosque (p. 238),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only the W. side has been Md bare. In the N.W. corner, adjoining{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W. tower of the pylon, lies an ancient Chapel (PI. Th), built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Thutmosis III. and restored by Ramses II. It is raised upon a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}platform above the pavement of the court, and contains three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chambers, of which that in the centre was dedicated to Ammon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that on the W. to Mut, and that on the E. to Khons. On the side{% endraw %}
{% raw %}facing the court it had a small colonnade of four clustered papyrus-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns in red granite.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Walls of the court are covered with reliefs and inscriptions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}including sacrificial scenes, hymns to the gods, representations of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conquered nations, etc., most of which date from the reign of Ram¬{% endraw %}
