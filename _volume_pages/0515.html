{% raw %}On the S. side this court is adjoined by a Colonnade (PI. B), which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was originally intended as the beginning of an immense hypostyle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hall (comp. p. 238). The colonnade is in fairly good preservation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and contributes essentially to the dignified appearance of the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Luxor when viewed from the river. The marvellous play of colour{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shown by the deep, heavy shadows of this colonnade at sunset is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nowhere excelled. Seven couples of columns, nearly 42 ft. in height,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with calyx-capitals, still support a heavy architrave above a lofty{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abacus. The whole was built by Amenophis III., but Tut-enkh-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amon (p. 238), Haremheb, Sethos I., Ramses II., and Sethos II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have also recorded their names upon it. The walls of the colonnade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were embellished by Tut-enkh-Amon with reliefs, representing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great festival celebrated at Luxor on New Year's Day. On that day{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sacred boats of the gods were brought by the Nile from Karnak{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Luxor, borne into the temple at Luxor, and returned to Karnak{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the evening. The procession is here depicted in all its details,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}though unfortunately a large part of the reliefs has perished with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ruined upper part of the walls. The series begins at the N.E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corner of the colonnade and ends at the N.W. corner.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}columns on three sides. The columns are clustered papyrus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns with bud-capitals; the E. and W. rows, with the archi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trave, are in excellent preservation, and are specially effective as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seen from the river-bank.{% endraw %}
