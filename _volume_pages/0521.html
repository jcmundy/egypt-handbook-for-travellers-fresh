{% raw %}We now proceed to inspect the Pylon (p. 239). On the way thither{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the traveller should not omit to cast a glance on the exterior W. walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Court and Colonnade of Amenophis III. and the Court of Ram¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ses II. (PI. b, c, d). These walls were embellished by Ramses II. with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs of scenes from his Asiatic campaigns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A paved street, flanked on both sides with figures of recumbent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rams, each with a small image of Amenophis III. in front of it, led{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the N. from this temple to the temple of Karnak. This street{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corresponded to the present main street of Luxor, with the bazaar,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and numerous traces of it may be identified; e.g. beside the market{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the N. of the village (PI. C, 2) and beside the temple of Khons at{% endraw %}

{% raw %}to the right extends a new embanked road leading to the Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Mut. Farther on we reach the village of Karnak. On the way{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we pass the pedestals of numerous Krio-Sphinxes, which also flank{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the old road to the Temple of Mut that diverges here. We follow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the main route, the W. Sphinx Avenue, to the Temple of Khons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a. Temple of Khons and surrounding Buildings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A handsome Portai, erected by Euergetes I., witha winged sun-disk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the hollow cornice, forms the S.W. entrance to the temple-preeincts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Karnak, which were enclosed by a brick-wall. The reliefs represent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Euergetes praying and sacrificing to the Theban deities. Beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this portal another avenue of sphinxes, erected by Ramses XII., the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last of the Ramessides, brings us to the beautiful and interesting —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Khons. This temple was dedicated to the Theban{% endraw %}
{% raw %}moon-god Khons, son of Ammon and Mut, and, like Ramses III.'s{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple to Ammon (p. 247), may be regarded as a characteristic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example of an Egyptian sanctuary under the New Empire. Ram¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ses III. reared the walls of this temple, but placed reliefs only in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}innermost chambers; the completion of the decoration was left for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his successors Ramses IV., Ramses XII., and the priest-king Herihor.{% endraw %}
