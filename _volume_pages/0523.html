{% raw %}Adjoining the temple of Khons on the S.W. is a Temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Osiris, built upon a basis about 12 ft. in height, crowned with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hollow cornice. The keeper of the temple of Khons will open it ou{% endraw %}
{% raw %}request. The entrance is on the W. side; on the jambs appears the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king before Osiris and other deities. We enter first a Rectangular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hall, with a well-preserved ceiling resting upon two columns, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}calyx-capitals and heads of Hathor on the abaci. The grated win¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dows at the top of the S. wall should be noted. To the right lie three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side-rooms with crypts; the door on the left is built up. — We{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proceed through the central door into a Second Hall, flanked by two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side-rooms, of which that to the left contains a representation of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dead Osiris upon the bier. We then pass through a door, on the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}jamb of which appears Opet, the hippopotamus-goddess, and enter{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Euergetes II.) before the gods. From the Sanctuary a flight of steps{% endraw %}

{% raw %}b. The Great Temple op Ammon.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Proceeding through the lanes of Karnak in the direction of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile, we soon reach the first pylon of the great temple of Ammon.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The rectangular terrace in front of this was even in antiquity covered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the waters of the inundation, as is indicated by marks on its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}front, recording the height of the inundations under the 21st-26th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dynasties. On the terrace rises an Obelisk of Sethos II., beside which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the base of its companion. A row of Krio-Sphinxes, erected by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ramses IL, extended from this point to the portal of the temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ammon. The fragments of building to the S.W. of the right pylon-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tower belong to a Chapel, erected about 390 B.C. by King Akhoris{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his successor Psammuthis. In the interior Psammuthis appears{% endraw %}
{% raw %}offering incense to the boat of Ammon.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The great **Temple of Ammon, the 'Throne of the World' as it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was officially styled, was not built on any single uniform plan, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}owes its present form to the building activity of many successive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rulers of Egypt. From the Middle Empire to the Ptolemaic period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most of the Pharaohs took some share in adding to or adorning this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shrine, rivalling each other in the magnitude of their designs. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foundation of the temple must be dated not later than the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monarchs of the 12th Dynasty. When Thutmosis I. ascended the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}throne and made Thebes his capital, the original modest shrine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seemed no longer worthy of the dignity of the god, and the king{% endraw %}
{% raw %}therefore added two colonnades and two pylons (Nos. V and IV) in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}front of the temple. In the reign of Makere alterations and additions{% endraw %}
