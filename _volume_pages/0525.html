{% raw %}Farther on, in the central axis of the court, are two large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pedestals for statues (that on the right retains its base only). Behind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was a Colonnaded Structure; of the original ten columns there still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stand five broken shafts on the left side, and one complete column{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(with calyx-capital and abacus) on the right. This structure had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a door on each of its four sides; in front of the W. door is a re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cumbent sphinx.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Upon the complete column Psammetikh II., of the 26th Dyn , has placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his name over that of the Ethiopian Taharka, of the 25th Dyn.; beside{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it is the name of Ptolemy IV. Philopator, which also appears on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abacus. The ehaft is composed of 36 courses of carefully hewn stone, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital of 5 courses. The height is 69 ft.; the greatest breadth of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital 16 ft., the circumference at the top 49 ft. The columns were united{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by means of balustrades, dating from the reign of Philopator.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A door in the N. walk of the colonnade surrounding the great court{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads out of the temple. In its vicinity are a number of Krio-Sphinxes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}placed close together, which were stored here in antiquity.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right, facing the great court, is the Temple of Ramses III.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was exhumed and cleared of rubbish in 1896-97 by the public{% endraw %}
{% raw %}department in charge ofthe antiquities. It is dedicated to Ammon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and is perhaps the best extant specimen of a simple Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple, built throughout on a single homogeneous plan. Its total{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length is 170 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Pylon with the entrance-door is much injured, especially at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the top. On the exterior of the left tower (E.) Ramses III., wearing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the double crown, holds a band of prisoners by the hair and raises{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his club for a blow. Ammon, standing in front of him, hands him{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sword of victory, and delivers to him chained together the re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presentatives of the vanquished peoples, who appear in three rows.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the two upper rows are the conquered nations of the south, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the third row those of the north. On the right wing are similar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representations, the king here wearing the crown of Lower Egypt.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the doorway, Ramses III. receives from Ammon the symbol of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}life, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the pylon is an open Court, surrounded on three sides{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by covered passages. The roofs of the passages at the sides are each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supported by eight Osiris-pillars (p. cxxxiii), while on the terrace{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the back are four similar pillars and four columns with bud-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capitals. The pillars are united by balustrades adorned with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs.{% endraw %}
