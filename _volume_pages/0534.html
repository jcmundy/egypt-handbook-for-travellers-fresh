{% raw %}256 Route 24. THEBES (E. BANK). 2. Karnak:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}c. The Northern Buildings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ruins to the N. of the Temple of Ammon are in such bad preser¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vation that the traveller who has little time at his disposal may content{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself with a glance at the great N. girdle-wall of Nile-bricks, and at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N. gateway.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beginning at the E. gateway (see p. 255), we skirt the girdle-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall ofthe great temple of Ammon, first to the N. and then to the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(left), until we see upon the right or N. side of our path another{% endraw %}
{% raw %}girdle-wall of Nile-bricks. Passing through a gateway in the latter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with the cartouche of Nekht-Har-ehbet (p. lxxxvii), we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reach the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Mont, which, though not inferior in size to the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Khons (p. 243), is now so ruined that its ground-plan can scarcely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be made out. The temple was built for the war-god Mont or Mentu{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Amenophis III. (18th Dyn.), though it was subsequently several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}times enlarged between the reign of Ramses II. and the epoch ofthe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemies, especially by Euergetes I. The temple stood upon a plat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form of masonry, traces of which still remain. The earlier sculptures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and architectural fragments are of great beauty; and columns with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hathor capitals were also employed. Two obelisks of red granite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}once stood in front of the N. entrance, upon bases that are still in situ.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The N. gateway, built of granite, dates from Ptolemy Philadelphos.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the granite gateway we proceed to the S.W., passing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains of a Ptolemaic Temple, of which the staircase is still to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be seen, to Six Small Chapels, each of which is entered by a sand¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone gateway (without inscription) in the girdle-wall. Only the two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chapels farthest to the W. have left any considerable remains. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}second from the W. contains the name of Amenertai's, with that of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}her brother Shabako. The fine alabaster statue of the queen, now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the museum at Gizeh (p. 87), was found here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Re-entering the precinct of the temple of Ammon by a gateway{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in its S. girdle-wall, we notice immediately to our left a Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ptah, patron-god of Memphis. It was built by Thutmosis III.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and enlarged and restored by Shabako the Ethiopian and by several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Ptolemies.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As we approach from the W., we reach five successive Gateways, of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which the second and fourth (PI. a and b) were built by Shabako, whose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}names have been scratched out, the others by a Ptolemy. Farther on is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Corridor, running between two columns (with rich foliage capitals) con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nected by a balustrade. The Pylon at the end of the corridor has a portal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bearing the name of Thutmosis III., restored in the Ptolemaic period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This admits to a Court (PI. c), embellished at the back with a portico{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supported by two sixteen-sided columns. Two altars stand here, dedicated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hy Amenemhet I. and Thutmosis lit. In the walls are ancient recesses.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A staircase ascends to the upper story. A doorway (with restored reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Thutmosis III.), in the main axis of the temple, leads into the Sanctuary,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which retains the original reliefs of Thutmosis III. To the right is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mom with a statue of the lion-headed goddess Sekhmet; to the left, another{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a well-preserved ceiling and ancient reliefs of Thutmosis III. — It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is instructive to observe the difference in style between the ancient and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the restored reliefs.{% endraw %}
