{% raw %}The reliefs on the W. Wall of the court, which also date from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Haremheb, are in poor preservation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The wings of Pylon X, the last pylon, forming the S. entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the precincts of the great temple of Ammon, have collapsed, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the central doorway is still standing. Haremheb was the builder of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this pylon also, for which he used the stones of a building raised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Amenophis IV. in Karnak to his new deity (p. 203). The reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the doorway exhibit Haremheb sacrificing and performing other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious rites. — In front of the N. side of the pylon stand two head¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less statues of Ramses II.; and before the S. side are the remains of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colossal statues of Amenophis III. (E.) and Haremheb (W.), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lower part of a colossus of Osiris.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the pylon the E. Avenue of Sphinxes, erected by Harem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heb, leads to the S. Temple Precincts.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the E. of the avenue is a Chapel of Osiris-Ptah (closed), with well-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preserved painted reliefs. The chapel was built by the Ethiopian Kings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tanutamon and Taharka (25th Dyn.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The avenue leads straight to a Gate, built by Philadelphus and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}embellished with reliefs and long inscriptions, in the N. side of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}girdle-wall enclosing the temple of Mut. Thence we proceed amidst{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures of recumbent rams, sphinxes, and fragments of statues to the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Mut, built by Amenophis III., which has recently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been cleared of rubbish under the directions of Miss Benson, an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}English lady.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A Gateway (PI. A) admits us to a large Court, in the middle of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was a colonnade. Outside the gateway are several pillars with figures of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the god Bes. On the gateway are lengthy inscriptions of the Ptolemaic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period (hymns to Mut) and in the entrance is an inscription of Ramses III.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who restored the temple. The court contains several seated figures of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lion-headed goddess Sekhmet, which were dedicated in this temple by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenophis III. On several of these Sheshonk I. has placed his own name{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in place of that of Amenophis. — Farther on is a second Colonnade, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statues of Sekhmet and a colossal granite figure of Amenophis III. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The inner rooms of the temple are in a very ruinous condition.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Behind the temple of Mut lies a Sacred Lake, in the shape of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}horseshoe. Farther to the W. are the remains of a small Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ramses III.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}being counted; 3. Battle with the Libyans; 4. Triumphal procession of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the king, and train of Libyan captives; 5. The king inspecting the captives;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6. Train of captives; 7. Dedication of the spoil to Ammon and his fellow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deities.{% endraw %}
