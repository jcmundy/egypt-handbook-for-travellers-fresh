{% raw %}Temple of Sethos I. THEBES (W. BANK). 24. Route. 261{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is visible at a considerable distance. This temple was founded in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}honour of Ammon by Sethos I., and at the same time was devoted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the worship of the king's father, Ramses I. Sethos left it un¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}finished and Ramses II. 'renewed' it, i.e. added the reliefs and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscriptions that were wanting. The beautiful execution of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscriptions and reliefs vividly recall the contemporary sculp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures in the great temple at Abydos (p. 218). The original build¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing (see the annexed plan) was 518 ft. in length, but of this only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the actual sanctuary with its halls and chambers, 153 ft. in depth,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains, while there are only scanty relics of the former courts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and pylons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Colonnade on the front of the temple originally displayed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10 clustered columns with papyrus-bud capitals, but only 8 are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now left. On the architrave is the dedicatory inscription of Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In its inner wall are three doors (PI. a, b, c), which lead into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}three divisions of the temple. On this wall, to the left of PI. 6, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representations of the provinces of Upper Egypt (a man and woman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alternately), bearing flower-decked dishes with cakes and wine; to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right, similar reliefs of the provinces of Lower Egypt. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}former have lilies on their heads, the latter papyri ■— the floral{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emblems of the two regions. Above the former the king offers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}incense to the bark of Amnion carried by priests; above the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the king appears before various deities.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passing through the middle door (PI. 6), we enter a Hypostyle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hall with 6 papyrus-bud columns, flanked on each side by three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chambers (PI. d-i). On the slabs of the roof of the middle aisle appear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the winged sun-disc, flying vultures, and the names of Sethos I.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between two vertical rows of hieroglyphics. The low reliefs on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls show Sethos I. and Ramses II. before various deities. Those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at PL a and at PI. (3 represent respectively Mut and Hathor of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dendera nourishing Sethos. — Side Chambers. Chamber g is ruined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and inaccessible; the ceilings in Chambers d, f, and h are in good{% endraw %}
{% raw %}condition. The finely executed reliefs in Chambers e, f, h, and i{% endraw %}
{% raw %}depict Sethos I. offering incense or performing sacred ceremonies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in presence of various deities. In Chamber i: on the left wall, Thout{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before the sacred bark of the king; on the right wall, the king seated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the banquet; on the rear-wall, the king as the god Osiris, seated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a chapel, surrounded by other gods. In Chamber d are sunk{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs of Ramses IL, showing (right) the king entering the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before Ammon and Amonet, and (left) the king offering incense to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ammon, Khons, and Mut. — The Sanctuary has four unadorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}square pillars. On the right and left walls Sethos appears offering-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}incense before the boat of Ammon.{% endraw %}
