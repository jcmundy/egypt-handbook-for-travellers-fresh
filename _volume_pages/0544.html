{% raw %}Farther to the N., among the spurs of the Libyan mountains, is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Necropolis of Drah Abu'l Negga.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Drah Abu'l Negga is one of the oldest cemeteries of Thebes, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}treasures discovered here by Mariette's excavations were of extraordinary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}value. Tombs of the 11th, 13th, and 17th Dynasties were discovered here,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but they have all been destroyed. The Rock Tombs on the hill-slope of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Drah Abu'l Negga are more interesting. Immediately behind the last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}houses in the village of Kurna is the Tomb of Neb-Amon, dating from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beginning of the New Empire, with tasteful stucco reliefs of vintage and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}funeral scenes. A hole in this tomb is the only access to the adjacent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tomb of Ment-her-khopshef, royal fan-bearer. Of the two chambers here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one has been filled up by a landslip. The very fine reliefs of the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represent burial-scenes and funeral sacrifices. — Farther to the N. is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfinished Tomb of Ramose, an architect.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4. Biban el-Muluk. Tombs of the Kings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Tombs of the Kings at Biban el-Muluk belong to the 18-20th Dyn¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}asties, and, in contrast to the pyramids that mark the graves of kings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up to the beginning of the New Empire, consist of series of passages and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chambers hewn in the rock. Like the corridors within the pyramids they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were intended only for the reception of the sarcophagus; the temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dedicated to the manes of the deceased, in which the offerings to the dead{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were made, were built in the plain.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Structure of the tombs is practically the same in all. Three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Corridors, placed one beyond the other, led into the innermost recesses.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Small side-chambers sometimes opened off the first corridor; oblong recesses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were made at the top of the sides of the second; and small recesses for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the reception of the furniture of the dead were provided at the end of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the third. A door led from the third corridor into an Anteroom, beyond{% endraw %}
