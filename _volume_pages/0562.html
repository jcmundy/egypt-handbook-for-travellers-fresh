{% raw %}5. From Biban el-Muluk to Der el-bahri and El-Asasif.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To return from Biban el-Muluk to the plain we should take the moun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tain-path via Der el-bahri. It is possible to ride to the top of the hill,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but better to go on foot all the way. Riding down the hill is by no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}means to be recommended. The donkey-boys usually lead the animals by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a narrow path over the ridge, and await the travellers at the foot. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}afternoon is the best time to visit the temple of Der el-bahri, for during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the hotter hours of the day the oppressive rays of the sun are reflected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the rocks here with peculiar intensity.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The path begins at Tomb 16, ascends the hill separating Biban{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Muluk from Der el-bahri and El-Asasif, and descends in zigzags.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It is fatiguing but safe, and is easily accomplished in 3/4 hr. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}* View is most remarkable: first into the desolate valley of the Tombs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofthe Kings, then from the summit and as we descend into the peculiar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ravine of Der el-bahri; we see the steep projecting mountain-side{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its tombs, and buildings old and new, with the rich green of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the fertile plain below spread out on both sides of the Nile , and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here and there its groups of palms and gigantic temples, as far as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Karnak and Luxor on the E. bank. The situation of the temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Der el-bahri is remarkably fine; on the W. and N. it is framed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}precipitous rocks of a light-brown and golden colour, against which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the dazzling white walls of the temple stand out in magnificent re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lief. — Below the temple lies the house of the Egypt Exploration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fund, where the keeper of the ruins lives. Opposite is the Chalet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hatasu, a luncheon-room open only to Cook's patrons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Temple of Der el-bahri, named by the Egyptians 'Zoser-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}zosru', or 'Most splendid of all', was built and adorned with reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and inscriptions, at the beginning of the New Empire, by Queen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Makere Hatshepsowet, the sister, wife, and co-regent of Thut¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosis III. The construction of the temple shared the chequered for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tunes of its foundress (p. lxxxii). When Makere was expelled from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the throne by her brother and husband after a brief reign, the build¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing operations came to a halt, and Thutmosis caused the names and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figure of his sister to be obliterated in all the finished sculptures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and inscriptions at Der el-bahri as well as elsewhere throughout the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}country. Thutmosis II., who succeeded his brother, continued the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work of destruction by inserting his own name in place of that of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Makere. When Thutmosis II. died, however, Makere once more regain¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed the throne, and the building was resumed. Operations were not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carried on with any remarkable activity, for when the queen's long{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reign came to an end, the temple was still unfinished. Thut¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosis III., once more on the throne, so far from supplying what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was still wanting, resumed his former tactics, destroying all allusions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to his sister and sometimes inserting his own name and figure in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place of hers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The splendid temple was thus never finished. Amenophis IV.{% endraw %}
