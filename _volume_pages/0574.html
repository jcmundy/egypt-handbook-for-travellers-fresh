{% raw %}286 Route 24. THEBES (W. BANK). 6. Ramesseum.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the shafts of the columns and the sides of the pillars are representa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions of Ramses II. sacrificing to the gods. The figures of Osiris, most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which are headless, represent Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The S. wing of the wall between the first and second courts is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now level with the ground, but the N. wing is still partly standing,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and its surface, especially that facing the second court, is in good{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preservation. Upon it are two rows of interesting representations.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Those in the Lower Row once more refer to the Hittite war and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}commemorate Eamses II.'s great exploits at the Battle of Kadesh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The king, much larger than the other warriors, dashes along in his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chariot. The Hittites, pierced by arrows or trodden down by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}horses, fall in confused heaps; crowds of them are hurled into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Orontes, where numbers perish. Farther to the right appears the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}battlemented fortress of Kadesh, round which the river flows. Beside{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it, on the other side of the river, are Hittite troops that have had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no share in the battle; some of them hold out helping hands to their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}drowning comrades. The Upper Row (well seen with an opera-glass){% endraw %}
{% raw %}contains scenes from the Festival of Min (the harvest-god), which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was celebrated when the king ascended the throne (p. 300). To the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left stands the king, awaiting the procession which is headed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priests carrying the images of the royal ancestors. Two tall poles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected in front of the king bear the god's headdress. Adjacent are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}priests letting fly four birds, for the purpose of carrying tidings to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the four quarters' of the globe that the Pharaoh has attained the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crown. Farther to the right the king appears cutting a sheaf with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a sickle for presentation to the god, thus discharging the second{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceremony usual at the festival. — This second court also contained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colossal statues of the king. Fragments of one of these (in black{% endraw %}
{% raw %}granite) lie upon the ground; the head is of great beauty (nose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}broken). On the throne are the names of Eamses II., beside which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of Belzoni the explorer (p. 116) has been placed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Tbeeace bounding the second court on the "W. was reached{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by three flights of steps, of which that to the N. is in excellent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preservation. Ascending these we turn to the S. part of the Rear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wall (PI. a), on which are three rows of representations. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bottom row are eleven sons of the king; in the middle row, to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left, is the king conducted by Atum and the hawk-headed Mont,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who holds the hieroglyph for 'life' before the king's face; to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right is the king kneeling before the Theban triad, while Thout,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}behind him, writes the king's name on a palm-leaf; in the top row{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the king is shown sacrificing to Ptah, to the left, and offering in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cense to the ithyphallic Min and a goddess, to the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond this terrace is the Great Hypostyle Hall, which had three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrances, corresponding to the above-mentioned flights of steps.{% endraw %}
