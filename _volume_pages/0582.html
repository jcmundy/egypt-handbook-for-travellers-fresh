{% raw %}292 Route 24. THEBES (W. BANK). Shtkh'Abd el-Kurna.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ascending to the right (N.), we reach *No. 26, the Tomb of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Enne, prince and overseer ofthe granaries of Ammon, whodied after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a long life in the reign of Amenophis III. (18th Dyn.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The facade of the Vestibule is formed by pillars. On the first Pillar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(to the left) are fishing-scenes; 2nd Pillar: Tillage and harvest (a woman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gleaning; three men mowing). 3rd Pillar: Tillage. 5th Pillar. Enne{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at table. 6th Pillar: Enne s garden, with his house and storehouse below,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded by a wall. 7th Pillar: Hunting-scene; a hyaena, struck by an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arrow in the mouth, rears on its hind-legs while a dog dashes at it; hares,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mountain-goats, gazelles. Back Wall: to the left of the door, peasants{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bringing tribute ; adjoining, Enne hunting and spearing fish. To the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the door, Enne receiving tribute (in the upper row are dark-brown{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubians, including two women carrying their children in baskets on their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}backs); Enne receiving the contributions of the peasants (observe the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lines to guide the artist's hand); Enne receiving tribute (only two rows{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remain, in one of which are necklaces, in the other the metal is being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weighed). — Corridor. On the left wall is the funeral, with female mourn¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ers; to the right, Enne and his wife seated at a table. On the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall, sacrificial scenes. — In the Recess are the fine statue of the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and three others. The shaft in front of it has been filled up.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of the other graves, all on the mountain-slope, have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}filled up and are comparatively uninteresting. The *View from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}top includes the Eamesseum and the statues of Memnon and extends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}across the Nile to the buildings of Luxor and Karnak; to the left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are the mountains, with the temple of Der el-bahri at their feet.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We ascend again to the right to No. 16, the Tomb of Haremheb, who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}administered the entire royal revenue under Thutmosis IV. — First Room.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right and left of the entrance is a banquet-scene. On the left rear-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall, Haremheb presents to the king the contributions of the peasants;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above are scribes, registering the peasants, who are arranged in companies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and headed by standard-bearers. On the right rear-wall the tribute from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Syrians and negroes is brought to the king. On the left wall of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Corridor the funeral rites are shown.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Close to it is No. 17, the Tomb of Zenen, a fan-bearer on the king's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right hand, and adjoining this, but turned towards the N., the Tomb of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenhotep (No. 102), second prophet of Ammon, and of his wife Roi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The paintings in this include a scale in which gold rings are being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weighed, various workmen, one of whom is making a sphinx, clerks with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tables writing out the crops, and a statue with a ram's head; on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right, music and dancing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A little higher to the right is the Tomb of Zenen (No. 104), chief scribe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the soldiers under Thutmosis IV. — First Room. On the rear-wall, to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right, the king receives through Zenen the tribute of the Syrians; ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}jacent is the deceased inspecting tribute brought to him (in the lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}row are horses). To the left are the king on his throne (obliterated) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Zenen inspecting the marching of the troops under his command.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N. of this tomb lies No. 110, belonging to Senmut, chief architect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Queen Makere, but in a very ruinous state. Adjacent is the recently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}found tomb of another of Makere's architects.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now descend past No 26 (see p. 291) to the Tomb of Men-kheper-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ra-seneb (No. 34), who was chief priest of Ammon in the reign of Thut¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosis III., with Asiatics bringing tribute, handicraftsmen, etc. This tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is entered by a very narrow gap. — A little to the right is the tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(No. 54) of Amenemhet, the scribe of the harvest, with musicians in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}iirst room and burial-scenes in the corridor. — Still farther to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is No. SO, the Tomb of Entef-oker, with some quaint stucco-reliefs in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corridor (to the right, fishing, hunting, handicraftsmen; to the left, funeral{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rites). — To the_S. of Tomb No. 6U, near Wilkinson's House (PI. c), lie the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tombs of Ahmose, a vizier contemporary with Hatshep-sowet, and Ken,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high-priest of Mut, with uninteresting stucco-reliefs.{% endraw %}
