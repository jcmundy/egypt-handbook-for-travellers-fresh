{% raw %}We next descend to No. 88, the Tomb of Ime-etwe, an official of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of Ammon in the reign of Ramses IX. (20th Dyn.). This tomb was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}originally constructed under the 18th Dyn., but Ime-etwe coated the old reliefs{% endraw %}

{% raw %}We finally descend to the plain, where there are a few more tombs to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be seen in the direction of el-Asasif, including that of Neferhotep, a priest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who lived under King Haremheb, with fine ceiling-decorations and incised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}designs. To the right a funeral song is inscribed: 'Let us then sing and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strike the harp in thy presence. Leave all cares behind and think of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}joys, until the day of the voyage comes when man casts anchor on the land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which delights in silence'. — Near this are the tombs of another Nefer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hotep, overseer of the cattle of Ammon (now used as a magazine by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}keeper of the antiquities), of Kheriuf, of the time of Amenophis III., of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mot, an official in charge of the embankments, and of Kenr, scribe of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}silver-house in the temple of Ammon. The last has gaily painted domestic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenes and the plan of a house.{% endraw %}
