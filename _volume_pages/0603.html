{% raw %}41 M. El-Kab, on the E. bank, the ancient Nekhab, once ranked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}among the chief cities of Egypt. Even under the Ptolemies it was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the capital of the third nome of Upper Egypt, which was afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named Latopolites. Its princes for a time were also governors of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ethiopia. Nekhbet, the goddess of the town, was represented either{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a vulture or a woman with the crown of Upper Egypt; she was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}regarded as a protecting deity of Upper Egypt. She also assisted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}women in childbirth, and was therefore identified with Eileithyia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Greeks, who named the town Eileithyiaspolis. Half-a-day{% endraw %}
{% raw %}suffices for a visit to the chief antiquities.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Etjins or the Ancient Town op Nekhab, which are sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rounded by a massive girdle-wall of sun-dried bricks, lie near the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river. The wall, which probably dates from the Middle Empire, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}damaged only on the S.W., by the Nile. It is of immense thickness{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(37 ft.) and encloses a rectangle, 620 yds. long by 590 yds. broad,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with gates on the E., N., and S. sides, beside which broad ascents{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lead to the top of the wall. The N. wall intersects a necropolis of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Ancient Empire, excavated by Quibellin 1897. The city itself,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enclosed by another double wall, of which distinct traces are still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to be seen, occupied only about one-fourth of the space within the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}girdle-wall. Very scanty ruins now remain of the temple, built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}during the 18th Dyn. on the site of an earlier sanctuary and en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}larged by Nektanebos and others.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Visiters should not omit to ascend to the top of the N. wall, for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sake of the admirable view: to the E., at the head of a desert valley,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appear the isolated rock with the inscriptions, the temple of Amenophis III.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a little nearer, the chapel of Ramses II. (comp. below); to the N. is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the hill with the rock-tombs, and a little farther away is a mushroom-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shaped rock, with the quarries that yielded the stone for the city temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We foUow the route leading due E. from the E. gateway in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}girdle-wall, and cross the dazzlingly white sand, past a small ruined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sandstone Temple (no inscriptions) close to the wall. In rather more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than 72 nr- we reach a Chapel of Ramses II., known to the Arabs as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-Hammam, i.e. 'the bath'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This temple was built in the reign of Ramses II. by Setaw, governor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ethiopia, represented in the entrance-doorway and on the interior walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on each side of the entrance. Other reliefs show Ramses II. sacrificing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Thout, Horus, and other deities. On the upper part of the rear-wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(right) are apes, sacred to Thout; below are praying men.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We keep straight on, leaving the larger temple ofthe Ptolemies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the left, and soon reach two Rocks projecting from the plain, at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the point where the road turns N. towards the desert, halfway be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tween el-Hammam and the E. temple of Amenophis III. Both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rocks bear numerous inscriptions and figures of animals. Most of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these date from the 6th Dyn. and were placed here by worshippers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in an adjoining temple.{% endraw %}
