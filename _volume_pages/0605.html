{% raw %}cient Egyptian domestic life will interest even those travellers who have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}already seen the tombs of Benihasan (p. 197) and 'Abd el-Kurna (p, 287).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We first enter the tomb of Paheri, nomarch of el-Kab , which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is conspicuous by its wide opening. It dates from the reign of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thutmosis III., and is distinguished by a series of representations{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the life of the deceased, with well-preserved colouring.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In front of the entrance is a platform in which is the opening of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deep mummy-shaft. We enter the vaulted Tomb by a much damaged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}door, embellished with inscriptions and representations of the deceased.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Left Entrance Wall: the deceased with a long staff; above is a sailing ship.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Left Wall (W.). In the upper row is the deceased inspecting harvest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}operations (ploughing, sowing, reaping with sickles, collecting and binding{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sheaves, oxen treading out the corn, winnowing the grain, bringing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}home the grain_. in sacks). In the lower row the deceased inspects his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}herds (cattle, asis, etc.); he superintends the weighing of gold made into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rings and the shipping of his grain. In the upper row farther on Paheri{% endraw %}
{% raw %}holds on his lap the young Prince Wezmose, whose tutor he was; Paheri{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his wife in a bower receiving flowers, fruit, etc.; above, vintage{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenes. In the lower row Paheri superintends his fowlers and fishers;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the captured birds and fish are being prepared, and the nets repaired.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther on to the right the burial of Paheri and the accompanying rites{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are shown in five rows one above the other. — Right Wall (E.). Paheri{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his wife at a banquet, with their son officiating as a priest before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them. Below their chair is a cynocephalus. Opposite them are their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relatives at table, and in the lower row are female harpists and flute{% endraw %}
{% raw %}players. Farther on to the right are Paheri and his wife, praying and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacrificing. A door was afterwards made through this wall to two other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chambers. — Rear Wall. In the recess here sit Paheri, his wife, and his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mother. On the side-walls of the recess are various persons at a banquet.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Of the tombs to the right of the tomb of Paheri that of Ahmose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pen-nekhab is noteworthy. It consists of a single vaulted chamber,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the fine reliefs in which have, however, left few traces.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ahmose was a well-known historical personage, who was prominent,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially in war, under the first kings of the New Empire, from Amosis I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Thutmosis III. His biography is recorded in the doorway.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the left of the tomb of Paheri is that of Setaw, high-priest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Nekhbet. This tomb, dating from the time of Eamses IX. (20th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dyn.), is the latest grave with inscriptions at el-Kab. Though{% endraw %}
{% raw %}400 years later than the others, it is decorated on the same plan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Left Wall is much damaged; nothing can now be distinguished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hut four boats apparently bound for a festival of Ramses III. Right Wall.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the left are Setaw and his wife at table; below their chair is a cyn¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ocephalus. Before them their son-in-law officiates as priest, in a panther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}skin; and opposite are their relatives at table, seated in rows. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artist has included his own portrait, identified by the palette. Part of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this scene is destroyed by a door made at a later period to a side-chamber.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther to the right are Setaw and his wife sacrificing. — The stele on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Rear Wall is much injured.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the left of this tomb lies that of Ahmose, Chief ofthe Sailors,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is noted for its long inscription recording the life and deeds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the deceased, more especially his share in the war of liberation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against the Hyksos.{% endraw %}
