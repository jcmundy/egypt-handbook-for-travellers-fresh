{% raw %}EDFU. 27. Route. 315{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Greeks called it Great Apollonopolis, after its chief god Horus-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apollo. It was the capital of the second nome of Upper Egypt, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apollonopolites of Graeco-Eoman times. Horus, who according to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the myth here waged one of his great combats with Set (comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pp. cxx, 215), was surnamed 'he of Tebhet', Tebhet being probably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a district of ancient Edfu; he was represented as a flying hawk, as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a man with a hawk's head, or as the sun with outspread plumes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The way to the (20 min.) Temple of Horus leads almost due W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the landing-place, then, turning to the N. (right), skirts the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Canal of Edfu and crosses it by a good new bridge. It then proceeds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the W. through several streets and finally turns N. again for a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}short distance. — Another route leads straight on from the landing-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place, bends to the right through fields, and then traverses the streets{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the town without crossing the canal.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The **Temple of Horus was freed by Mariette under the auspices{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofthe Viceroy Sa'id from the accumulated rubbish that is now piled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up high all around it; and the edifice is now seen in wonderful,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}almost perfect preservation, exceeding that of any other Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple or even of any antique building in the world, in spite of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2000 years that have passed over it. Unfortunately the faces of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kings and gods in the reliefs all over the temple were scratched out{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Christian period. The present temple occupies the site of an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}earlier sanctuary and was dedicated to the sun-god Horus, Hathor of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dendera, and the youthful Horus, 'Uniter of the Two Lands' (Har-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sem-tewe, Harsomtus). The history of its construction and a de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scription of the entire structure are detailed in lengthy inscriptions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the exterior of the girdle-wall (at PL i and fc). The temple proper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(i.e. the two hypostyle halls and the smaller apartments beside and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}behind these) was begun in 237 B.C. by Ptolemy III. Euergetes I.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and completed, so far as the masonry was concerned, by his suc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cessor Philopator in 212 B.C. The decoration of the walls with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs and inscriptions, postponed by the death of Philopator and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the disturbed reign of Epiphanes his successor, was resumed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}176 B.C. under Philometor and completed in 147 B.C. (under Euer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}getes II.), i.e. 95 years after the laying of the foundation-stone.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Euergetes II. built the great hypostyle hall (completed 122 B.C.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and embellished it with reliefs, and under Soter II. and Ptolemy XL{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alexander I. the colonnaded court, the girdle-wall, and the pylon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were added, though the reliefs on the latter date from Neos Diony-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sos. The final touch was given in 57 B.C. The building-material{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is sandstone.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The great Pylon, to which a flight of steps descends, is covered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on all sides with reliefs and inscriptions. On the front of each of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the towers the colossal figure of the King Neos Dionysos is con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spicuous, smiting his foes, whom he holds by the hair, in presence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the hawk-headed Horus of Edfu and Hathor of Dendera. In two{% endraw %}
