{% raw %}26 M. Gebel Silsileh (Mountain of the Chain; so called from a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chain that is said once to have blocked the passage of the river).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The hills on the two banks of the river approach so close together{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here that they have justly been compared to the pillars of a gigantic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gateway. In very ancient times there were probably rapids at this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}point (like those at Assuan), which were either impassable or nearly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so. They were thus regarded as the beginning of the Nile, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even in later periods the Nile-god Hapi enjoyed special reverence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Silsileh, where great festivals were held in his honour.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We turn first to the N. from the landing-place. The well-beaten{% endraw %}
{% raw %}track skirts the river, passing a number of small tomb-like recesses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and memorial tablets. Beyond the quarries we reach the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Bock Chapel (Speos), which was hewn in the rock under Harem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heb, last king of the 18th Dyn., and in the following centuries was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}embellished with inscriptions and reliefs of great artistic as well as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}historical value in honour of kings and high officials. In front are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}five doorways, separated from each other by pillars (PL a-d) at vary¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing distances, and crowned with the astragal and concave cornice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We enter by the middle door, on the lintel of which are chiselled the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sun-disk and the name of Haremheb. The interior consists of a broad{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but narrow vaulted chamber (PL A), at the back of which is an oblong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room (PI. B). All the walls are covered with carving and inscriptions.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}to King Haremheb, while Khnum stands behind her and Amnion-Re{% endraw %}
