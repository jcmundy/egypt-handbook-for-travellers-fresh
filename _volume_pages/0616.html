{% raw %}After skirting the bushy river-bank for 1/4 hr. we reach the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Monuments, offering a picturesque appearance as seen from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile. The chief of these are two Recesses about 6 ft. deep, which lie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}close beside each other. The architraves over the entrances aTe{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adorned with concave cornices and ureeus-serpents and are borne by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clustered columns. That to the N. (right) was erected by Meren¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ptah I., in the first year of his reign; it represents the king sacrificing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Harmachis, Ptah, and a Nile-god (on the right), and to Ammon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mut, and Khons (on the left). Beneath is a long hymn to the Nile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and lists of offerings to be made to the river-god. On each of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}narrow side-walls are injured reliefs of the king sacrificing to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods. — The scenes in the S. recess are repetitions of these, except{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that Merenptah is replaced by Eamses IL, who constructed the recess{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the first year of his reign. — On the rock between these recesses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a door-shaped Stele, on which King Merenptah is shown present¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing a figure of Maat to Ammon; the king is followed by a prince and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the vizier Panehsi, who erected this stele to his master. — Farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the S. is another Stele, on which Merenptah sacrifices to Ammon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while behind him is Koi, high-priest of Ammon, who dedicated this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monument.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On a curious isolated sandstone rock, to the right, is a Stele of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 6th year of Eamses III., showing the king sacrificing to Ammon,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Harmachis, and the god of the Nile. On the same rock, to the left,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears a priest revering the names of Sethos I. — A few paces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther to the S., and at a lower level than those steles, is another{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and much ruined recess, dating from the reign of Sethos I. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river-bank are traces of an ancient flight of steps descending to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the stream.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The largest Quarries, worked especially under the New Empire,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are found on the E. bank of the Nile. In the reign of Eamses II. no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fewer than 3000 workmen were employed here for the Iiamesseum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alone. An Inscription of Amenophis III. records the transport of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stones by the Nile for a temple of Ptah. There are two Posts of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time of Sethos L, Demotic Inscriptions from Eoman times and an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfinished colossal Sphinx (near the river-bank). — At the N. end{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the quarries are found the scanty ruins ofthe ancient town Khenu{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and its temple (fragmentary inscriptions of Eamses II.). To the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E., towards the top of the rock, is a stele of Amenophis IV., record¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing that Amenophis caused an obelisk for the temple of the sun at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Karnak to be quarried here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The mountains recede from the river immediately above the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}defile of Gebel Silsileh (p. 320), giving space to the desert, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears grey on the Arabian side and yellow on the Libyan side.{% endraw %}
