{% raw %}The narrow cultivable strip is tilled by peasants of a distinctly darker{% endraw %}
{% raw %}complexion than the fellahin of the Thebaid. Both land and people{% endraw %}
{% raw %}approach gradually nearer to the Nubian type.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the E. bank lies Aklit. — At the village of Miniha (E. bank){% endraw %}
{% raw %}we enter the E. branch of the stream, which here forms the island{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Mansurlyeh, with a village of the same name.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On a hill on the E. bank and conspicuous from a considerable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distance appear the ruins of the beautiful temple of Kom Ombo,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially picturesque by the light of the full moon. The islands{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opposite the E. bank were, perhaps, in former times united with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mainland, or separated from it only by a narrow channel; but in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course of centuries the Nile has gradually extended its channel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther and farther to the E.; and during the 19th cent, alone about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}20 ft. have been washed away from the E. bank, along with a large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portion of the building. An embankment has been constructed to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prevent farther mischief.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}42 M. Kom Ombo, the 'Hill of Ombos', lis about 50 ft. above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the average surface-level of the Nile. The ancient Egyptian city{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Ombos probably owes its foundation to the strategic importance of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its site, upon a hill commanding both the Nile and the routes from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubia to the Nile valley. Yet the town attained no great prosperity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}until the Ptolemaic era, when it was converted from an ordinary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}provincial town into the capital of the separate nome of Ombites.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It was at this era that the mighty temples were built, which excite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}our admiration to-day; the earlier sanctuaries have left scarcely a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trace. Not content like other towns with one local deity, Ombos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}possessed two chief gods — the crocodile-headed Sobk (Sukhos){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the hawk-headed Haroeris. With the former were specially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}associated Hathor and the youthful moon-god Khons-Hor; with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the latter, the 'Good Sister' (T-sent-nofret, a special form of Ha¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thor) and the 'Lord of Both Lands' (P-neb-tewe). The ruins of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town, now buried in sand, lie in the N.E. corner ofthe plateau.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In 1893 all the temple-buildings in the S. part of the plateau were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cleared of rubbish and restored under the directions of De Morgan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A marble tablet on the right door to the great hypostyle hall (p. 325){% endraw %}
{% raw %}commemorates the fact. At the W. angle of the plateau is a dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mantled British fort.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the landing-place, to the S. of the hill, a road skirts the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river-bank to a modern flight of steps, which ascends direct to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal entrance. Tickets of admission should not be forgotten.{% endraw %}
