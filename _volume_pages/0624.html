{% raw %}Histoby. The district round the modern Assuan, including the island{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Elephantine, bore in antiquity the name of Yebu, or 'Elephant Land',{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably because the Egyptians here first saw the African elephant. At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a later date that name, was restricted to the island and town of Elephant¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ine. From antiquity down to the present day Elephantine has marked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the limit of Egypt proper in the direction of Nubia. From the erectiou{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Pyramids to the Roman period the ancient Egyptians found material{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for tiieir great temples and statues in the quarries of Yebu (Syene), which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yielded fine coloured granite, containing a large proportion of translucent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quartz and of yellow, brownish, pink, and black mica. Curiously enough,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however, the term 'syenite', which was used by Pliny, is now applied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by geologists to a different variety of stone, containing a much larger pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portion of hornblende. Strategically, Yebu commanded the Nile cataracts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the channels that fostered communication by water between Egypt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Nubia. It was also the starting-point of the great caravan-routes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leading to Nubia and the Sudan, along which passed the earliest com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mercial and military expeditions of the Egyptians, and it thus became{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an important depot for the trade with the interior of Africa. The ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}capital of the province was also named Yebu and lay on the S. side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}island (p. 331).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Another town, named Swenet, the Syene of the Greeks, was situated on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the E. bank of the Nile, but appears not to have attained any great im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portance until a late period. Large mounds of rubbish near the railway-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}station still indicate the former size of this town. Juvenal, the Latin satiric{% endraw %}
{% raw %}poet, was appointed prefect of the garrison at Syene, or, in other words,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was banished to the most remote frontier of the empire, as a punishment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for his biting attacks on the court. A famous curiosity of ancient Syene{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was a well, into which the sun's rays descended perpendicularly, casting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no shadow, at midday during the summer-solstice, thus proving that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Syene was situated under the tropic (which, however, has now shifted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat to the S.). The report cf its existence led the learned Athenian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Eratosthenes (2?6-l9t) B.C.), attached to the Museum at Alexandria, to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}discovery of the method of measuring the size of the earth that is still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}employed. The place suffered greatly at the hands of the Blemmyes (p. 352),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but became the seat of a Christian bishop, and appears to have rapidly re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gained its prosperity under the Khalifs. Arab authors record that no fewer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than 20,000 inhabitants died of the plague at one time, a fact that points{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to a very large total population. After the close of the 12th cent. Assuan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}suffered severely from the incursions of plundering Arab tribes, finally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}put a stop to by a Turkish garrison stationed here by the sultan Selim,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after the conquest of Egypt in 1517.{% endraw %}
