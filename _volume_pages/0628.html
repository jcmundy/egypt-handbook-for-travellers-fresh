{% raw %}330 Route 29. ASSUAN. Quarries.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the summit of the hill to the right of the road are some large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosque-like Cenotaphs of famous saints, such as the Shekh Mahmud, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shekh 'Ali, the Lady (Seyidneh) Zenab, etc., whose memory is celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by festivals on their birthdays, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Granite Quarries (Arabic Ma'ddin), from which the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian builders and sculptors drew their supplies (comp. p. 328),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are situated in the hills to the S., and may be reached in 7* hr.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}either direct from the Bisharin camp or from the town by a routo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leading via the Arab cemeteries (p. 329) and then to the E., passing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a grove of tamarisks. Numerous blocks of granite lie where they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were left when the works were given up; e.g. the huge block (marked{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obelisk' on the Plan) in the N. Quarry, measuring 92 ft. in length{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and 10'/2 ft. in breadth at the broadest part. A moderately lofty{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cliff shows manifold traces of the industry of the ancient stone¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cutters. The blocks were detached from the cliffs by boring nu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merous holes along a prescribed line, driving wedges into these, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then wetting the wedges. By this process tolerably smooth and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}even fractures were obtained. Frequently the blocks were finished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on three sides before being finally detached. Statues , sarcophagi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obelisks, etc., were also roughly worked over by the stone-cutters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the quarries in order to lessen the weight for transport. The hill{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above the N. quarry commands a wide prospect, including the con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vent of St. Simeon (p. 334) in a desert-valley, to the W., beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Nile.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The S. Quarry, 1/2 hr. farther to the S., contains still more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rough-hewn sculptures, ready for removal. The quarry opens to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the E., facing the desert. On a rock here may he seen an inscrip¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion with the name of Amenophis III., though the name and figure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the stone-cutter who carved it have been obliterated. Beside it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two trough-shaped sarcophagi have been begun (in the Ptolemaic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or in the Eoman period); and in the vicinity are the unfinished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colossus of a king (covered, except the feet, with sand), and a large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quadrangular block, perhaps intended for the shrine of a god. Far¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ther on, near the railway, and towards the top ofthe cliff, is a figure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Osiris (called Eamses by the natives), about 20 ft. in height.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This point commands a fine view of the desert and in the direction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of PhilEe.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A massive Embanked Road, by which the huge blocks were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conveyed to the Nile, runs from the quarries to Assuan and is used{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to this day. — If we follow the railway-line we reach the railway-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}station of Shellal (p. 335) in i/i hr.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The verdant island of Elephantine, with its luxuriant palm-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trees, attracts all travellers. The Arabs call it merely Gezireh, i.e.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'island', or Gezlret Assudn. It is reached by small boat (felukeh){% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a few min. from the landing-place, and the entire visit takes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}barely an hour. There are two villages on Elephantine, whose in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}habitants speak Nubian.{% endraw %}
