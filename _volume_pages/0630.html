{% raw %}332 Route 29. ASSUAN. Reck Tombs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the W7. bank, to the N. of Elephantine, rises a hill, crowned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the tomb of a Shekh, in which are excavated the *Koek Tombs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the princes and grandees of Elephantine. These, most of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were opened in 1885-86 by Sir F. Grenfell, date from the close of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Ancient Empire and from the Middle Empire and are therefore'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contemporary with the tombs at Benihasan, which they resemble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both in construction and decoration. The more important tombs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are now kept closed. Tickets should not be forgotten.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We cross the river in a small boat (there and back, 5 pias. each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}person). From the landing-place we ascend a sandy path, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reaches the top at Tomb 31. The ancient staircase, hewn in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rock and consisting of two parallel flights of about 85 steps, with an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inclined plane between them up which the sarcophagi were drawn,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is now covered by the sand. The view from the tombs is fine.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We begin with Tomb No. 25, belonging to Mekhu. This dates{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the 6th Dyn.; and both its construction and decoration are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat crude. It contains 18 somewhat roughly worked columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 3 rows. Between two columns opposite the entrance is a stone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}table with three legs, which was perhaps used as an altar. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rear-wall, opposite the entrance, is a door-shaped stele, within a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recess approached by steps and closed by a screen-wall. The re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presentations on the walls and columns show the deceased receiving{% endraw %}
{% raw %}various votive gifts. To the right ofthe entrance offerings are being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made to the deceased; to the left of this are agricultural scenes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(ploughing ; harvest; asses bringing home the harvest). — Adjoining{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is Tomb No. 26, belonging to Sabni, son of Mekhu. The remark¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}able entrance is divided by a cross-beam into two parts — the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}door proper and a window above it. The tomb chamber, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}14 square pillars, is of little interest. A relief on the Rear Wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represents the deceased in a boat, accompanied by his daughters,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hunting in the marshes: to the left he appears holding the javelin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in his right hand and the slain birds in his left; to the right he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears harpooning two fish at a blow; in the middle is a papyrus-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thicket with birds hovering about it.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Climbing up to the right (N.) from this tomb, we pass several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}others (Nos. 27, 29, 30) which are sanded up, and reach —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}No. 28, that of Heka-yeb (locked). In the small chamber is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representation of the deceased as a negro, with a dark-brown skin.{% endraw %}
