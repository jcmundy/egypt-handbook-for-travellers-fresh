{% raw %}Farther on are the tombs of Aku (No. 32), Khui (sanded up),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Khunes. The first contains a recess with a representation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the deceased and his wife seated at a meal in an arbour, with their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}son before them. In the tomb of Khunes are interesting represen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tations of Egyptian craftsmen: bakers, potters, metal-workers beside{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a furnace, brewers, glass-blowers, leather-workers, etc. The two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last-named tombs were used as dwellings by Coptic monks from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the convent on the plateau, who have added various inscriptions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and figures of saints. — Then follow the small tomb of Khenusew{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(sanded up) and the Tomb of Harkhuf, with inscriptions of great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}historical importance. These inscriptions appear outside the tomb,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on each side of the entrance, above and beside figures of the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(to the left, Harkhuf leaning on a long staff with his son holding a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}censer in front of him). The inscriptions form an extract from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}biography of Harkhuf, recording especially four successful trading{% endraw %}
{% raw %}expeditions to the Sudan, three of which were made in the reign{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Merenre (6th Dyn.) and one in that of King Nefer-er-ke-re, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}successor of Merenre. Among the goods brought back on the fourth{% endraw %}
{% raw %}journey was a dwarf.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Adjoining is the small tomb of Pepi-nakht (No. 35; sanded up),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with important inscriptions on each side of the doorway. These{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extol the exploits of the deceased in the campaigns led by King{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nefer-er-ke-re against Nubia and the Beduins dwelling to theE. of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egypt. Farther on are the grave of Senmose and finally the interest¬{% endraw %}
