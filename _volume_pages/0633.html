{% raw %}The return by boat down the smaller cataract has now been rendered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}impossible by the works for the new dam (see below){% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Eailway, starting at the Luxor Station, to the S. of Assuan,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}describes a wide curve round the N. and S. granite quarries (p. 330){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and ends at the station of Shellal (p. 336).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Desert Eoute, which crosses the railway-line and leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}past the Arabian and European cemeteries (the latter containing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the graves of many British soldiers), is the old road described by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Strabo, who assigns it a length of 50 stadia. On the heights to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right are small British forts and block-houses. Beside the road lie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large blocks of granite, with ancient inscriptions carved by Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}officials employed in the quarries. M. de Morgan has numbered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these inscriptions with white numbers. Farther on, beyond the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}point where the route via the river-bank diverges (see below), we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}notice considerable fragments of an ancient Brick Wall, incorporat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing a few granite blocks, which was probably erected as early as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Middle Empire to protect the road against the predatory attacks{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the E. desert-tribes. The wall is 6 ft. thick and, at places, 12 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high. It first appears, to the W. ofthe road, then crosses it, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains on the E. side as far as Shellal (p. 336).{% endraw %}

{% raw %}about !/2 M. beyond the European cemetery, approaches the river,{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The island of Sehel, lying below the cataract and reached from Assuan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by boat, was dedicated to Anukis, and contains over 200 rock-inscrip¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions, besides the ruins of two temples. One of the temples (to the S.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}dates from the 18th Dyn., the other, near the village of Sehel on the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side of the island, from the Ptolemaic period (Philopator). High up mi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the S.E. rocks of the island is an important inscription of the Ptolemaic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period, recording that in the reign of the primaeval King Zoser (p. 134) the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile failed to rise during a period of seven years and that a famine arose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the land in consequence, until at the prayer of the king the cataract-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}god Khnum put an end to it by a fresh inundation.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}for a short distance, we return to the river. The view of the stream{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its numerous dark-coloured granite rocks, covered with a{% endraw %}
