{% raw %}the details minutely. Luncheon, which the traveller must bring with{% endraw %}

{% raw %}From the landing-place we proceed to the S.W. end of the is¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}land to visit the extensive Outer Temple Court, which is bounded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the N. by the first pylon of the Temple of Isis, on the S. by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vestibule of Nektanebos, and on the E. and W. by colonnades. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}court dates from the late-Ptolemaic period or from the reign of Au¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gustus. Attention should be paid to the strong Quay Wall that per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}haps was carried round the greater part ofthe island, with flights of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone steps at various points. The remains of one such flight may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seen on the S. bank, to the E. of the building of Nektanebos.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Vestibule of Nektanebos was built by Nektanebos as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vestibule for a temple, dedicated to 'his mother Isis, revered at Aba-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ton, mistress of Philae, and to the Hathor of Senmet' (p. 344). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple was swept away by the floods of the Nile very shortly after its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}completion ; but Ptolemy Philadelphus caused the vestibule to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thoroughly restored. The elegant little structure was supported by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}14 columns with varying floral capitals, above each of which was a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}second capital bearing a head of Hathor. Unfortunately only six of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the columns are now standing, and the roof has disappeared. Be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tween the columns were screen-walls, over 6 ft. in height, crowned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with concave cornices and rows of Uraeus-serpents, and interrupted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the E., W., and N. hy exit-doors. These screens-walls bore{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs showing King Nektanebos offering gifts to the gods. Before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the river-front of the temple two Obelisks upon chest-shaped bases{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were erected in the Ptolemaic period. These were made of sand¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone instead of the usual granite. The W. obelisk, bearing a Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and several Arabic inscriptions, is still standing, though it has lost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its apex ; the E. obelisk is represented by its base only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The West Colonnade, which follows the line ofthe shore of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}island, is 100 yds. in length and has a row of 31(formerly 32) plant{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns, each 16 ft. high, no two capitals of which are alike. Most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the columns have two series of reliefs, showing Tiberius offering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gifts to the gods. The ceiling, which is partly destroyed, is decorated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with stars and flying vultures. The rear-wall, the windows in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}command a beautiful view of the stream and of the island of Bigeh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 344), is embellished with two horizontal rows of alto-reliefs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representing the Pharaoh (usually Augustus or Tiberius) offering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}various gifts to the gods. The relief over the window opposite the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first two columns (PL a) is specially noteworthy for its excellently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preserved colouring and the delicacy of its execution; it represents{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nero offering two eyes to Horus, Isis 'the good sister', and the 'lord{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of both lands'. — A subterranean stairway leads outside this colon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nade to a small Nilometer.{% endraw %}
