{% raw %}340 Route 29. PHIL/E. Temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pylon I and on the N. by Pylon II. On the E. and W. are edifices,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}each with a colonnade on the side next the court. On the W. (left){% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Birth House, dedicated to Hathor-Isis and to the memory of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the birth of her son Horus. It is also reached by a doorway in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}W. tower of Pylon I (see p. 339); on the back of the pylon is a relief{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of four priests carrying the boat of Isis, preceded by the king offer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing incense.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Birth House is surrounded on all four sides by colonnades, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns of which have floral capitals surmounted by Hathor-capitals. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns on the W. side are unfinished. The walls, the columns, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}screen-walls between the columns are adorned with the conventional re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liefs and inscriptions, mostly dating from Euergetes II., Neos Dionysos,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Augustus, and Tiberius. Specially noteworthy is the relief (PI. b), in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}central row, to the left: Buto, goddess of the North, playing the harp{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before the youthful Horus (nude, wearing the double crown) and his mother{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Isis, who sits behind him; behind Buto is Augustus bearing a shallow vase.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The cow among marsh-plants, depicted above the vase, is to be taken as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representing the ornamentation within it. To the left of this scene appears{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Pharaoh opening the chapel, in which is seated Isis suckling Horus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the upper row, at PI. c, are two scientificially important Inscriptions of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Epiphanes, unfortunately injured by reliefs atterwards carved over them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Neos Dionysos. These inscriptions are in hieroglyphics and the demotic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}character. One of them is a duplicate (wanting the Greek text) of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscription on the Rosetta Stone (p. 20j.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior of the temple is of little interest. On passing through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Main Entrance (PI. d), behind the great pylon, we enter a columned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vestibule, the mural reliefs in which show Tiberius offering gifts to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods. A door, above which is a window between four heads of Hathor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(one now wanting), leads hence to three rooms lying one behind the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL A, B, C). Room A, on the wall of which are demotic inscriptions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has no reliefs. Room C has three rows of reliefs. At the foot of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rear-wall: Isis giving birth to Horus in the marshes; Ammon and Thout{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accompany her; behind Ammon are the goddess of the Xurth and the god{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Wisdom', behind Thout the goddess of the South and the god 'Reason'. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left wall: a goddess offering her breast to the child Horus, who stands;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Euergetes II. handing two mirrors to Hathor, who places her hands in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blessing on the head of the child Horus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The East Building, opposite the Birth House, was occupied by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the priests, partly for scientific purposes. The colonnade often plant-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns is very elegant. The reliefs and inscriptions date from Neos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dionysos, the votive inscription on the architrave from Euergetes II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The First Room (PI. e) is noteworthy, as from it a staircase ascends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to a larger apartment in the upper story and thence to the roof of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colonnade. — In the Third Room (PL g) are rude reliefs of men (on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left wall), with the related inscriptions in Ethiopian cursive characters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. oix). — The Fifth Room (PL i) was the library. The comparatively{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high reliefs exhibit Tiberius before different gods. On the left wall as we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}emer: Thout, god of wisdom, between Maat, goddess of truth (with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palette), and the lion-headed Tefnut, behind whom stands Sefkhetebui,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}goddess of writing. To the left is a recess (for books), with a seated ibis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above it. — The Sixth Room (PL k) shows Tiberius before the Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods, on the walls to the right and left. A door in the exterior brick-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall here opens on the main street of the ruined Coptic town (p. 343).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There is another door on the right.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the N. end of the colonnade is a Door (PL I), approached by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several steps, opening upon the inner passage round the temple.{% endraw %}
