{% raw %}The Second Pylon is smaller (105 ft. broad, 40 ft. high) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less well-preserved than the first pylon. The Portal between the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towers, approached by a shallow flight of steps, was built by Euer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}getes II. and embellished with reliefs of the usual type.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The large relief on the Right Tower represents the Pharaoh Neos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dionysos placing the slaughtered sacrificial animals before Horus and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hathor. Above are two small reliefs: to the right, Neos Dionysos presenting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a wreath to Horus and Nephthys ; to the left, Neos Dionysus ottering in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cense and pouring water upon an altar, in presence of Osiris, Isis, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Horus. The granite of the site at the foot of the tower has been smoothed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to form a stele, with a six-lined inscription and reliefs relating to a lavish{% endraw %}
{% raw %}grant of lands made to the temple of Lis by Philometor in the 24th year{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his reign (157 B.C.). In front of it are the foundations of a small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chapel (PL in). — On the Left Tower are similar scenes (figures deliberately{% endraw %}
{% raw %}defaced). On both towers are grooves for the flag-staffs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}An inner staircase ascends to the W. pylon (comp. the Plan), whence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we proceed across the central portal to the E. pylon. The ascent of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tirst pylon (p. 339) is, however, preferable in every respect.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Within the doorway, at the top, to the right, are some much{% endraw %}
{% raw %}faded early-Christian pictures.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Temple of Isis proper, entered by this portal, consisted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of an open Court (here very small), a Hypostyle Hall or Pronaos,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several Vestibules, and a Sanctuary, with smaller chambers adjoin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing. The walls are covered both outside and inside with reliefs of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ptolemies (Philadelphus, Euergetes IL, etc.) and Eoman emperors{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Augustus, Tiberius, Antoninus) performing the customary cere¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monies in the guise of Pharaohs ; but the traveller will find detailed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descriptions of similar scenes in our accounts of the temples of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dendera (p. 227) and Edfu (p. 315).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Open Court had a small covered hall on each side, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roof of which rested on a single column. The uncovered portion could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be shaded from the sun by means of a velarium; the holes for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cords are still visible in the upper part of the concave cornice turned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards the second pylon.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Hypostyle Hall, with eight columns, was originally sep¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arated from the preceding court by screen-walls between the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}row of columns. The colouring of this hall, which has been pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served on the ceiling and the columns, must have been very bril¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liant. The floral capitals of the columns both in the court and this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hall are the most instructive of all the specimens that have come{% endraw %}
{% raw %}down to us of the manner in which the Egyptians ofthe later period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coloured their columns. Comparatively little regard was paid to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}natural colours ; e.g. light green palm-twigs receive blue ribs, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christian services were celebrated in the court and hall, of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the numerous Coptic crosses chiselled in the walls are memorials. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek inscription in the doorway to Room D, on the right, records that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'this good work' took place under Bishop Theodorus. This was in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reign of Justinian.{% endraw %}
