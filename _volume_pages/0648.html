{% raw %}At a distance of about 2 hrs. from the Hydreuma the rocks close{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in and form a winding pass or gateway named Mutrak es-Seldm. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Gebel Abu Ku'eh ('father of the elbow'), the rock at the entrance{% endraw %}

{% raw %}one of them contains the name of the heretical king Amenophis IV.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now approach the fine rocky scenery through which the second{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of the Koser route leads. In the distance, to the right, rise the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. foot-hills of the Hammdmdt Mts., while nearer and in front are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the S.W. spurs. From this point onwards the rock-scenery is very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fine. Even the imposing granite cliffs of the Shellal islands and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the quarries of Assuan pale before the rocky mass ofthe Hammamat.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The outliers of the range consist of a yellow sandstone, followed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the red 'Nubian' sandstone, while the great central mass is comp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}osed of granite.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the Mutrak es-Selam the hills again diverge. Among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them, to the N. of the caravan-route, lies a second Eoman station,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a filled-in well. About 2 hrs. farther on the hills of reddish-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yellow sandstone give place to loftier and almost black hills of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}breccia, through the valley of which, now wide and now narrow, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}caravan winds its way. Beginning with the black mountains is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wadi Hammamat, the Rehenu Valley ofthe Egyptians, who quar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ried its hard dark stone for statues and coffins in the most ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}times. In 1 hr. more we reach the Bir Hammdmdt, a well 16 ft. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}diameter, with a stone coping. Near the well are the remains of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman wall, and five unfinished sarcophagi, some completely shat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tered. The quarries contain numerous Egyptian inscriptions. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}earliest expedition to Hammamat of which we have any knowledge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}took place in the reign of King Esse (5th Dyn.). At a later period,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially under the Middle Empire, the quarries were diligently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worked, and even under the New Empire they were in operation.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We hear of a great undertaking under Eamses IV. for the purpose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of procuring blocks for the temple of Ammon at Thebes, in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}no fewer than 8368 workmen and soldiers were employed. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarries were worked under Darius, Xerxes, and Artaxerxes also.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Just beyond the quarries the route turns from the N.E. to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. and passes the ruins of el-Fawdkh'r, an old mining site. Those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who wish to continue their journey to the Eed Sea have still two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}short days' marches ahead of them, the route leading through the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wddi Rdsafa to the Bir el-Beda (Bir el-Inglls) and thence through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Wddi Ambagi to —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Koser or Kosseir, on the Arabian Gulf. Koser is now an unim¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant town of about 1600 inhab., with a quay, a wooden mole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}400 ft. long, two mosques, several bazaars, government buildings,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and a large grain-magazine, belonging to the Egyptian government.{% endraw %}
