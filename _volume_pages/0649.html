{% raw %}Eastern Desert. KOSEE. 30. Route. 317{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Ptolemaic period the desert-route ended at the Leukos Limen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or 'White Harbour', and in earlier times in the vicinity of the Wddi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gasus, near the village of Sauw, a little to the N. of Koser. About{% endraw %}
{% raw %}3 or 4 M. to the N. of Koser lies Old Koser, with some scanty an¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient remains.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In going from Koser towards the Nile the Beduins sometimes prefer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}another and more southerly route than that through the Wadi Hammamat.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This diverges from the route above described at the Bir el-Be'da'(see above){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and leads at first through the winding Wddi Kabr el-Khddim, afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passing the Gebel Nuhds and through the pass'of Ri'at el-Ghazdl into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wddi Ghazdl. To the right rise the conical Gebel Daghaniyeh and Gebel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Moshdghir. We next follow the Wddi Hoinuda, which farther on takes the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name of Wddi el-IIomr from the fine Gebel Homr, which flanks it on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right. The night is spent at Bir el-Mo'ila, a well beside a few huts of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Ababdeh. At the Gebel Wdkif we cross the Tarik ed-dahrdwi, a road{% endraw %}
{% raw %}running from N. to S., and farther on reach 'Amdra, with another well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and more 'Ababdeh huts. Thence our route lies through the Wddi NUr{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Wddi Kash to the Bir el-Kash, a dried-up well, beyond which we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pass numerous quarries of green breccia. Beyond the passes of Ri'at el-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khel (sandstone formation) and Ri'at el-Hamra we reach the Mahwala (see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 345) of Rds dsfar, whence we go on through the Wddi Mdghlat to Mab-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walat KhCr el-Ghir. Lastly we proceed via, Gdhrat ed-l)aVa to Laketa (p. 345),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where our route unites with the more northerly'one already described.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Joueney to Bekenike through the territory of the 'Ababdeh Beduins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is seldom undertaken. We may start from Keneh or Koptos, diverging at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Laketa from the route to Koser, or we may begin at a point opposite Edfu{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Co'ntra-Apollinopolis) or Redesiyeh (p. 319). On both routes traces of old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}watering stations are discernible. The Itinerary of Antonine (3rd cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A.D.) gives a list of the ancient stations (starting from Koptos) with their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}distance from each other in Roman miles, as follows: Phoenicon 24, Di-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dyme 24, Afrodito 20, Kompasi 22, Jovis 23, Aristonis 25, Phalacro 25,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apollonos 23, Kabalsi 27, Keenon Hydreuma 27, Berenike 18 — in all 258{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman miles = about 236 English miles. — A third route, established{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Hadrian, led from Antinoupolis (p. 201) to the Red Sea, and then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}southwards along the coast to Berenike.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Golenisheff, the Russian Egyptologist, who described his journey in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 'Recueil de travaux relatifs a la philologie egyptienne' (1889), took{% endraw %}
{% raw %}11 days from Redesiyeh (p. 319) to Berenike, and returned thence to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Assuan in 8 days.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1st Day, Bir Abbdd (3 hrs.), in the Wddi Midh. The Wadi Miah is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entered opposite the mouth of the Wddi Ammerikbe. This is an ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}station with quarry-marks like those at el-Hosh, near Silsileh (p. 319).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lepsius mentions a ruin here, named Herhush (i.e. sandstone), dating from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some ancient settlement.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2nd Day. Temple of Sethos I. The Temple was discovered in 1816 by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cailliaud, on his first journey to the mines of Mt. Zabdra. It was built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Sethos I. beside a water-station either constructed or restored by that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king, and was dedicated to Ammon-Re. The vestibule is built of blocks{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of sandstone and is supported by 4 columns with lotus-capitals. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reliefs represent the king as victor over negroes and Asiatics. — The{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Hall contains 4 square pillars. Long inscriptions on each side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entrance record the sinking of the wells and the building of the temple.{% endraw %}
