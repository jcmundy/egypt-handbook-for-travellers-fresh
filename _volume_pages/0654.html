{% raw %}The itinerary of the last-mentioned trip is as follows: —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1st Day. Start from Shellal at 2 p.m. and ascend to Dendur.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2nd Day. Via Dakkeh (inspection of the temple) to Korosko.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}3rd Day. In the morning ascent of the Awas el-Guarani. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}steamer starts at 9 a.m. for 'Amada (inspection of the temple) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abu-Simbel.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4th Day. Inspection of the rock-temples at Abu-Simbel; then{% endraw %}
{% raw %}upstream to Wadi Haifa. After an afternoon-visit to the Second{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cataract, the return-voyage is begun in the evening.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}5th and 6th Days. Eeturn-voyage, with a short halt at Kalabsheh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dahabiyehs. — The voyage by dahabiyeh (p. 187) requires from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4 to 6 weeks, according to the wind, and is now only undertaken by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travellers with special aims, such as archaeologists and painters. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenery of Nubia is much more monotonous than that between Cairo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Assuan, and its monuments, with the exception of the temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Abu-Simbel, are inferior in grandeur to those of Upper Egypt,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so that the ordinary tourist who uses a dahabiyeh is not repaid for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the additional cost of time and money. Fairly good dahabiyehs may¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be hired at Shellal for about s£E 20 per month, including the crew;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but provisions and bedding have to be furnished by the hirer.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Additional information may be obtained in the hotels at Assuan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pending the completion of the navigation canal (p. 336), dahabi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}yehs cannot at present be towed up the First Cataract.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}For information as to Felukas, see p. 188.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Land and People. — Nubia extends from the First Cataract to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Khartum, i.e. to 16° N. latitude. It is divided into Lower Nubia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(from Philae to Wadi Haifa) and Upper Nubia (from Wadi Haifa south¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards). Politically, the portion to the N. of Faras (p. 376) belongs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Egyptian mudiriyeh of Assuan, in which it forms the districts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Abu Hor and Korosko. The rest of Lower Nubia and the whole{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Upper Nubia has, since its reconquest in 1898 (p. ciii), been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}placed under the administration of the Anglo - Egyptian Sudan.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Even in Lower Nubia the cultivable area is seldom more than a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few hundred yards in width; and at not a few points, especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the W. bank, the desert advances clear up to the river-brink.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The population is in consequence very scanty, viz. 96,500 in 1897.{% endraw %}
