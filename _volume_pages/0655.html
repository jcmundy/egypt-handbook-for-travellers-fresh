{% raw %}History. Lower Nubia, whence the Egyptians drew their sup¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plies of incense, is mentioned in some of the earliest Egyptian in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scriptions. Its relations with Egypt were still oloser under the kings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the 6th Dyn., who there enlisted negro mercenaries, whiie the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}princes of Elephantine dispatched great trading expeditions to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lands of the Upper Nile. But it was not until the period of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}12th Dyn. that the conquest of Nubia was undertaken and the bor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ders of Egypt advanced to Semneh (p. 379). In order to guard the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}newly acquired possessions against the attacks of the negroes a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chain of forts was erected in the rocky valley of Batn el-Hagar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 378), with its numerous cataracts, between Wadi Haifa and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Semneh. From this base the Pharaohs of the 18th Dyn. penetrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still farther to the S. and made themselves masters of the land of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kosh as far as Napata (p. 382), which then became the southernmost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city of the empire. The conquered regions were incorporated with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the southernmost department of Egypt proper, which began at El-Kab{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 311), and the whole of the great province thus formed was placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under an official who bore the title of 'Prince of Kosh and Governor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Southern Lands'. Under the established rule of Egypt Nubia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rapidly prospered; new towns were founded, and beautiful temples,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}little inferior in size and embellishment to those ofthe motherland,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arose in profusion, especially on the W. bank of the Nile, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was safe from the attacks of the E. Beduins. Most of these temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were dedicated to the great Egyptian gods, Ammon, Ee-Harmachis,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Ptah; but in some of them other deities were also worshipped,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}such as Isis and others of the Egyptian gods, the local Nubian god{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tetun, the deceased King Usertesen III., who united the roles of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first conqueror and patron-saint of Nubia, and occasionally also the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reigning king and queen (pp. 360, 364, 370, 374). The temple-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscriptions were composed in the Egyptian language and written{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Egyptian character, and Egyptian became the official lan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guage, although the great mass of the people adhered to their native{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubian tongue.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubia continued to be a dependency of the Pharaohs until about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1100 B.C. But when the power of Egypt waned under the 21st Dyn.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. lxxxiii), Nubia shook off her allegiance and a native Ethiopian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monarchy was established, with Napata as its capital. But the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}civilization of this kingdom continued to be Egyptian; and its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monarchs, who were dependent on the priests, regarded themselves{% endraw %}
