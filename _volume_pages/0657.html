{% raw %}As we leave the harbour of Shellal (p. 336) we have a fine view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ofthe ruined temples of Philae (p. 336) and of the rocks of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}island of Bigeh (p. 344). To the S.W. of Bigeh lies el-Hesseh, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}largest of the cataract-islands. On the E. bank are the villages of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-B&b and Belial (el-Meshhed), with picturesque ancient mosques{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the rocks above Bella.1 appears the tomb of a shekh — On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E. bank, opposite the S. end of el-Hesseh, is a rock-inscription of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pepi I., relating to a victory over the Nubians.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At el-Guti our course bends to the S.W., afterwards returning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to its S. direction. The scenery becomes less wild, and a narrow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strip of verdure appears on each bank. — To the W. is the Gebel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shemt el-Wah. From time to time we observe ancient dykes of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}huge stones (on both banks), and on the summits of the higher{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hills are ruined guard-houses, dating from the Mahdist insurrection.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}6I/2 M. Debot, a village-community on both banks. On the W.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bank are traces of an ancient quay and the village of el-Birbeh{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through which we pass on our way to the — '{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple or Debot, situated to the S., near the river. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple was built by the Nubian king Ezekher-Amon, a contem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}porary of the earlier Ptolemies, and it was enlarged by Ptolemy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Philometor. We pass through three Doorways, on the second of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which appear the winged sun-disk and a much damaged Greek in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scription in favour of Ptolemy Philometor and his consort Cleopatra.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 42 ft. beyond the third doorway is the small temple proper.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Vestibule, the facade of which was borne by four columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with rich floral capitals, connected by screen-walls, was destroyed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}along with a side-chamber by an earthquake in 1868. The reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the interior of the hall showed Augustus or Tiberius in presence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of various deities. The First Hall was decorated with reliefs by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ezekher-Amon. On each of the right (N.) and left (S.) walls are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}eight reliefs in two rows, exhibiting the king sacrificing to various{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deities. Over the door is the votive inscription of Ezekher-Amon.{% endraw %}
