{% raw %}To the right and left lie two oblong chambers. At the back are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}three chapels, the central and largest of which is the Sanctuary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL D). On the walls of this chamber are reliefs. On the left wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Eamses presents flowers to the boat of Ptah; on the right wall, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king before the boat of Harmachis. In the centre of the Sanctuary{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a pedestal, wrought out of the rock, for the sacred boat. At the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}back is a recess with four seated figures, representing (from left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to right) Ptah, the deified Eamses, Ptah-Tatenen, and Hathor with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the cow's head.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Above Girsheh the scenery relapses into monotony. — 29 M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Koshtamneh, a commune on both banks. On the E. bank rises the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gebel Hayati. On the W. bank is a brick-built fortress of the Middle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire, known to the natives as Kuri. Extensive sand-banks and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}granite rocks interrupt the course of the river.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}35 M. 'Aldki, on the E. bank, and Dakkeh, on the W. bank.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dakkeh is the ancient Egyptian Per-Selke(t), 'House of Selket', the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek Pselchis, near which the Eoman general Petronius defeated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Ethiopians in 23 A.D., on his campaign to Napata. We proceed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the S., through the miserable mud-huts of the village, and in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few minutes reach the —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Temple of Dakkeh, which has been much damaged within the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last few years. The present temple (comp. the Plan, p. 362) occu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pies the site of an earlier shrine, probably of the New Empire, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was dedicated to Thout of Pnubs, an Ethiopian town. By the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greeks this god was called Paotnuphis. The most ancient part of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the building dates from the time of the Ethiopian king Ergamenes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL D) and his contemporary Philopator (PL C). Euergetes II,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}added a vestibule (PL B), and the temple received its final form{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the Eoman emperors by the addition of the Sanctuary (PLE){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and ofthe Pylon (PL A). The temple, situated in the desert, lies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its main axis parallel to the river (i.e. running N. and S.), con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trary to the usual rule in Nubian temples. It stands upon a base{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about 6 ft. in height, now completely exposed by the natives in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}course of their excavations for sebbakh (p. lv).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A broad path, still flanked on either side by a single course of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blocks of masonry, leads to the well-preserved Pylon (PL A), which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood in the outer girdle-wall and formed the entrance to the temple-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}precincts. Each pylon-tower has a groove for a flagstaff; and both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have a few isolated reliefs on the outside and numerous inscriptions,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly Greek, though some, added by visitors to the temple, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Demotic and Mero'ftic. Within the central gateway, on the left side,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the king making offerings to Thout, Tefnut, and Hathor, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Isis appears below.{% endraw %}
