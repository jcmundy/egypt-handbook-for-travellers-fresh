{% raw %}The village of Es-Sebu'a consists of two parts, one on each bank{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Nile. "Beside that on the W. bank lies the Temple of Es-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sebua or Sebu'a (the lions), called by the Egyptians Per-Amon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}('House of Ammon'). This temple was dedicated to Ammon and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ee-Harmachis by Eamses II., and is constructed on the same plan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as the temple at Gerf-Husen (p. 360). Eamses himself was also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worshipped here as a god. The present name of the place is appar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ently derived from the avenue of Sphinxes leading to the temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Most of these are now covered with sand. Beside the two first are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two colossal statues of Eamses II. Statues of the king also stood in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}front of the Pylon, which is 32 ft. high and 42 ft. broad. The sadly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weatherworn reliefs on the front of the pylon-towers represent the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king smiting his foes, in presence of Ee-Harmachis (N. tower) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ammon-Ee (S. tower). The reliefs embellishing the jambs of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}central doorway show the king sacrificing to various deities. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large Court, which we now enter, is 65 ft. square. To the right and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left are colonnades, each with 5 square pillars, against which stand{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colossal figures of the king. The reliefs on the walls, representing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the king in the usual scenes of intercourse with the gods, are poorly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}executed and offer no special interest.{% endraw %}
