{% raw %}366 Route 33. 'AMADA. From Korosko{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears the praying figure of Setaw, who was governor of Ethiopia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under Eamses II. Beyond the pylon extended originally a court{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enclosed by brick walls, while at the back stood a hall, supported{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by four proto-Doric (p. cxxxiii) columns. This court was afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}converted by Thutmosis IV. into a covered Hypostyle Hall (still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in good preservation) by the erection of 12 columns and stone side-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls. The reliefs on the pillars and walls show Thutmosis IV. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intercourse with the gods; and the votive inscriptions on the pillars{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and architraves also date from that king. The columns are em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bellished with perpendicular bands of inscriptions, containing dedi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cations by Thutmosis III. or Amenophis IL, who are also represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the rear-wall before the gods.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The following Transverse Chamber is 24(/2 ft. broad and 6'/2{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deep. To the right on the inside of the Entrance Wall is Thut¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosis III. embraced by Isis, and then Amenophis II. offering liba¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions to Ammon-Ee. On the left Thout and Horus of Edfu pour the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consecrating water, symbolized by the hieroglyphs for 'life', over{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenophis II. — Three doors in the back-wall of this room lead{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into other apartments. The central one enters the Sanctuary, on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the back-wall of which is a Stele of great historical importance. At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the top is a relief of Amenophis II. presenting two vessels of wine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Ee-Harmachis and Ammon; beneath is an inscription of 20 lines,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the 3rd year of the reign of Amenophis IL, recording the comple¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of the temple begun by Thutmosis III. and the campaign of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amenophis II. in Syria. Among other details the king records that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he captured seven Syrian princes and hanged six of them on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls of Thebes and the seventh at Napata (p. 382). — Doors (of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}later construction) to the right and left of the sanctuary each admit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into two Chambers, also accessible from the above-mentioned trans¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}verse chamber. The most interesting reliefs here are those in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first room to the right, representing the ceremonies at the foundation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Between the temple and the river are the foundations of a small Chapel.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Nile here describes a curve from E. to W., on the E. bank{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which are (10 M.) Diwdn and —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Derr, one of the handsomest villages in Nubia, prettily situated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beneath sycamores and date-palms. On the river-bank is the ruined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}residence of the Kashif, who was formerly independent ruler of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lower Nubia. In the village is a large mosque, and between the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}village and the hills lies an extensive cemetery with a tasteful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shekh's tomb. Close by, at the foot of the hills, lies the small and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfortunately much injured Bock Temple of Derr. This was known{% endraw %}
