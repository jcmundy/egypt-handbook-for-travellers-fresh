{% raw %}Paths ascend both on the N. and S. to the only Gate of the for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tress, which lies on the N. side. This is embellished with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Egyptian hollow cornice and the sun-disk. The Girdle Wall of rough{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stones is supported on the S. side by a substructure of carefully{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hewn blocks. The interior of the fortress is occupied by a confused{% endraw %}
{% raw %}group of houses, built of rough stones, but incorporating fragments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of older buildings, such as portions of columns used for thresholds.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Two of the larger buildings are still in good preservation, viz. a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Byzantine Church in the middle and an Egyptian Temple, with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pylon, in the N.W. corner. The church was afterwards used as a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosque, but a dedicatory cross is still to be seen on one of the col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}umns. The temple contains no inscriptions. A steep flight of steps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}descends on the W. side to the river. The view from the castle-hill is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}very tine ; to the E. rise hills crowned with the tombs of shekhs, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Kile flows below; to the W. lies the tawny desert, contrasting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the verdant fields and groves and the grey houses of Anibeh.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Close to the S. slope of the hill lie the ruins of a small Toun, sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rounded by a wall. Farther up the valley is a large Cemetery, with simple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christian graves and some brick tombs on a more ambitious scale. There{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is a similar cemetery in the \alley to the N. of the castle.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the steep W. slope of the castle-hill are five Memorial Re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cesses, of the Middle Empire. These now lie at some height above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the river-bank and some are very difficult of access, but originally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they were reached by means of steps from a path skirting the river{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at a higher level than the present path. The first (on the S.) was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}constructed in the reign of Thutmosis III. by Nehi, governor of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ethiopia. The second was constructed in the reign of Ramses II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Setaw, governor of Ethiopia, who is represented in it with his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}officials. The third chapel dates from the joint reign of Thut¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosis III. and Hatshepsowet, but the name of the latter is every¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where defaced. Both rulers, each beside a deity, are represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the rear-wall. The fourth chapel, the most important, belongs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Amenophis II. On the right side-wall the king receives tribute{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of all kinds (including a panther) from two officials. On the left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall the king appears conducted by Horus, lord of Beheni (Wadi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Haifa), to a row of gods. In a niche in the back-wall is the statue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the king, embraced by Horus of Mem (on the right) and by Satet{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(on the left). — The fifth recess has no sculptures.{% endraw %}
