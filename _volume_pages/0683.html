{% raw %}The third day's route also leads through the desert, from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heights in which we have occasional glimpses of the river-valley.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In 2 hrs. we find ourselves opposite the rocky island of Uronarti{% endraw %}
{% raw %}('king's island'), on the N. end of which are the ruins of a fortress{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Middle Empire and of a chapel built by Thutmosis III. On{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W. side of the island are considerable Tapids. In 2 hrs. more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we reach Semneh, which marked the S. limit of Egypt under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Middle Empire. On the top of the hill are massive fortifications,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with well-preserved girdle-walls. Within the latter is a temple,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built by Thutmosis III. and Hatshepsowet and dedicated to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nubian deity Tetun. The river, here flowing between granite cliffs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forms numerous rapids. — On the E. bank, opposite Semneh, lies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the village of Kummeh, to which the traveller is ferried on a raft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At Kummeh are a ruined fortress of the Middle Empire and a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of the same date as that at Semneh. The numerous inscrip¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions on both banks of the stream are interesting, especially those{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the end of the 12th and of the 13th Dyn. with flood-marks, from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which it would appear that the Nile used to rise about 23 ft. higher{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than it does at present. The explanation probably is that, the river{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was at that epoch retarded by some natural barriers a little lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}down, which it afterwards succeeded in sweeping away.{% endraw %}
