{% raw %}trains weekly) to (173 M.) Kermeh in 24 hrs.; thence steamer. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}following are the chief railway-stations. 35 M. Sarras, with a fort built{% endraw %}

{% raw %}93 M. Ferkeh. On the Nile here is the ruined temple of Amdra, a large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}edifice of the late-Ethiopian period. On the island of Gdi, farther to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S., are the remains of a temple of Thutmosis III. and Amenophis II., and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a Coptic church; and at Soleb is a well-preserved temple of Amenophis III.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— 130 M. Said Fanli. — 173 M. Kermeh or Herman is situated at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Third Cataract. In the vicinity are traces of an ancient city and an ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tensive necropolis, with pylon-shaped erections of sun-dried bricks. To{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N. lies the island of Tomb is, which marked the S. limit of the Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}power at the beginning of the New Empire. On the island are a few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rock-inscriptions. — From Kermeh a government-steamer plies up the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nile, passing the island of Argo, to (31 M.) the town of New Dongola, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}El-Ordeh, situated on the left bank. This thriving town, with 15,000 inhab.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the capital of the province of Dongola, and the starting-point- of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}caravan-routes to the oasis of Selima. — To Old Dongola, see p. 383.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The construction of the railway to Khartum was undertaken in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1896-97 in order to support the advance of the Anglo-Egyptian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}army ; and in spite of the enormous difficulty of laying a railway-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}line across the sandy and stony surface of the desert, the work was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so energetically carried on that it advanced almost a mile daily. As{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in other parts of Egypt (comp. p. 166) the one essential point was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the provision of an adequate supply of water for the workmen and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the machinery. A series of watering-stations (I-^I) was accordingly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}established, at which wells, sunk to a depth of 80 ft., tap the sub¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terranean water, which is found to flow from the S. When the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}train stops, the numerous soldier-passengers flock to the houses of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the guardians of the wells. — On the E. rises the bare, violet-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coloured chain of hills, beyond which lies (124 M.) Bir Murat{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and behind which runs the caravan-route from Korosko to Abu{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hammed (comp. p. 365). The stony desert gradually gives place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to undulating sand-hills. Isolated dum-palms, fields of barley,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conical sayal-acacias, and finally a grove of palms announce the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proximity of the Nile.{% endraw %}
