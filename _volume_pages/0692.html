{% raw %}The non-European business-part of Khartum is restricted to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portion of the town lying back from the river. This is intersected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by three long streets running parallel with the river, viz. Kitchener{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Avenue, Cromer Avenue, and Abbas Avenue, the last of which has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}just been begun. Thousands of dervishes, including representatives{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the most diverse tribes, though the Bagara are most numerous,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are now engaged in the work of rebuilding the town. The Roman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Catholic Church may still be recognized among the ruins.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Trees and Plants that occur in the private and public gardens{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at Khartum nearly all belong to the Sudanese flora, with the conspicuous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exception of the date-palm. Among them the following may be specially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned: the curious Sudanese Balaenites jEgyptiaca or soap-tree (Arab.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}el-heglig), the bark of which exudes a fatty substance and is used as a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}substitute for soap; the Salvadora Persica (Arab, el-arak), the mustard-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tree of the Bible; and the saccharine but poisonous Callotropis procera{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Arab, el-ushar), a large-leaved Asclepiadea. Three specimens of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gigantic Adansonia digitata, baobab, or monkey-bread-tree (Arab, el-homr),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be observed in the town (one to the N. of the ruined Roman Catholic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Church). There are also several india-rubber trees, Parkinsonias, Sesbanias,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and coffee-plants. — Agriculture is carried on by the Nubian fellahin in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}primitive manner of the Dongolese. without plough or harrow, but none{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the less industriously (p. liv). Their Sakiyehs, or water-wheels (p. lv){% endraw %}
{% raw %}are sometimes 25 ft. in diameter, and are worked by zebus. Wooden{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posts are occasionally placed beside these wheels in such a way as to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form a kind of sun-dial, by which the hours of labour are regulated. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief crop is Andropogon Sorghum, the staple food of the country, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sweet potatoes (Ipomoea Batatas; Arab, bem-bay), Maize (recently introduced),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Sudanese sugar-cane (Andropogon Zaccharatum; Arab, el-ankolib){% endraw %}
{% raw %}are also cultivated. The last-named ripens between February and May.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Communication between Khartum and Omdurman is maintained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a steamboat leaving the Palace landing-place once every morning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(fare 5 pias.). At other times a rowing-boat must be hired at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rds Khartum, the promontory between the two arms of the Nile.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Owing to the considerable distances to be traversed at Omdurman,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the traveller is recommended to hire a donkey on arrival.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The great military camp of Omdurman was founded in 1883-84{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Mahdi Mohammed Ahmed, and after his death in 1885, it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the residence of the Khalifa Abdullahi Tai'shi for 14 years, dur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing which it became the scene of the most atrocious cruelties and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the most extravagant orgies. It extends for about 3V2 M. along the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left bank of the united Nile, and has room for upwards of 100,000{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inhabitants. The name is said to be derived from an old woman,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who once spent a solitary existence here. The S. part is the Omm{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed-Durmdn proper. The central part, including the holy buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the walled inner town inhabited by the Bagara tribe, to whom{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Abdullahi belonged, is called by the natives El-Baga, i.e. 'the (holy){% endraw %}
{% raw %}place', a name always given to the wandering headquarters of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mahdists. To the N. is the Haret el-Nusdra or el-Mesihin, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christian quarter, inhabited by Abyssinians, Copts, and between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one and two hundred Greeks.{% endraw %}
