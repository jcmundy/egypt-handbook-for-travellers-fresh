{% raw %}The ramparts round the Baga.ua Town are strong and massive,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hut have no banquette within for the defence. The Mahdi's Tomb{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL 1) was destroyed by the British and its contents scattered. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}base (6 ft. thick), vaulting, and dome of this edifice are said to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been executed by Neufeld, a German kept in captivity for many years{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Khalifa. Beside the tomb are an open space in which the der¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vish army was reviewed, and a Mosque (PL 2), now used as a mess-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room by the Egyptian officers. Close by are Abdullahi's House (PL 3){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the two-storied House ofthe Shekh ed-Dln (PL 4), his son. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}latter is the only dervish house with glass windows. Beside it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the grave of an English war-correspondent, who fell here. Permis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sion must be obtained from the Civil Secretary for a visit to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}treasure-house Bet el-Amdna or Bet el-Mdl (PI. 5), which contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}memorials of Gordon and trophies of weapons. Visits may be paid{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Statin Pasha's House, now the telegraph-office (PL 9), and to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Emir Yakub's House (PI. 6), which was occupied by the governor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}until the summer of 1901. The Prison (PL 12), where many Eu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ropeans languished, is situated 500 yds. farther on, at the S. angle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the town-wall. It is sometimes known as the 'Saier Prison' from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the name of the jailor under the Mahdi and Khalifa.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A wide new street, passing the clean quarter of the Greek shop¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}keepers, leads hence to the N.W. to the (1/4 M.) large *Maeket{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Place, which presents a busy and variegated scene of African life.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}All the articles of consumption of Central Africa are to be seen here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in profusion : curious spices of a hundred different varieties, drugs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and perfumes; soda, saltpetre, salt; betel, bead-nuts, seeds, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wood of every kind; glass beads, filigree work, toilet-butter, an-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}garibs (bedsteads), the dried flesh of wild animals, ostrichs, etc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Skilful smiths, and saddlers dealing with hippopotamus hide, may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be seen at work. Sudanese weapons and curiosities may be purchased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of P. Loiso. Goods intended for importation into Central Africa{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be seen in the covered sheds on the S. side ofthe market-place.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The place of execution under the Khalifa was also in the market¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place, and to the E. of it is the pit ('Tomb of the Martyrs') into which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were thrown the heads and limbs of the condemned. — A visit to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the *M6rada, on the bank of the Nile, should not be omitted; here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are the markets for ivory, india-rubber, feathers, and corn. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slave-market is, of course, a thing of the past. The Suk el-Harlm,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where women were sold, is now devoted to milk, ornaments, oint¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ments, and basket-work.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}friendly and importunate. The warlike oppression of the last de¬{% endraw %}
